**Opening**
Thanks so much for making the time, Elena, and for fitting this around your break. Rather than just send the document over and leave it there, I would much rather walk you through how I got to it, so you can see the thinking and not only the output. It is only eight pages, so I will go section by section, and please stop me anywhere you would like to dig in.

**Pages 1 and 2 — What I changed, and why**
The first thing I want to be clear about is that your draft was genuinely well-planned. The three-phase shape, the discipline of no live posts until October, the case-study-as-sales-asset logic, the EOS cadence, I kept all of it. My job was not to rewrite it, it was to keep the ambition and take out the few things that would quietly break in a real first quarter at twenty-four hours a week.

You will see I have boiled that down to five principles on the first page. The big one is the first, sequence around peak season, not against it. Your draft back-loads the case studies into September to December, which is exactly when, by your own brief, the techs and PMs who hold the raw material are flat out. So I have pulled the case-study interviews forward into the quiet July and August window, banked the material whilst people are actually around, and left the writing to run steadily afterwards. That single move de-risks the biggest deliverable in the plan.

The other four, quickly. Phase the build rather than run all six pillars at once. Make the hours honest, so every month carries a real hour-budget. Respect the review gate on both sides, because front-loading my work also front-loads your reviewing. And calibrate to the market, which I will come back to on the table.

Page two just lists the constraints I deliberately built around, the peak season, the twenty-four-hour week, organic-only for the first year, your approval gate, and the fact that this is genuinely one person plus AI, not a team.

**Pages 3 to 5 — The reworked plan, month by month**
Pages three to five are the plan itself, and I kept your Immerse, Build, Execute shape. On page three, Month one is immersion and three audits, which I have staggered across the weeks rather than dumping all three at the end of week two, plus starting those case-study interviews early. Page four, Month two, is where the systems get built, the content calendars, five case studies, the HubSpot essentials, the website proposal. And I have been honest there that August is actually the heaviest month, so I flag exactly how I protect it.

Page five, Month three, is where content goes live and I would expect to be running the core three pillars on my own. You will notice I am careful with the word independent, I separate owning a workstream from a deliverable being finished, because the website, for instance, runs to 2027 by design, so independence there means driving it, not completing it by Month three.

**Page 6 — The milestones, and the one number I moved most**
Page six is the milestones table, your draft target against my reworked one, with the reasoning in the last column. The headline is the case studies. Your draft aimed for twenty published by December. I have brought that to ten to twelve, and the reason is right there on the page, twenty would mean interviewing through peak season when the team is least reachable, whereas one to two a month is the genuinely sustainable rate. It is the same logic as the front-loading, just carried through to the number.

**Page 7 — How I would actually work with AI**
Page seven is the part I am most keen to talk about, because it is what makes a twenty-four-hour week credible in the first place. The short version is that I build systems to do the heavy lifting and keep the judgement for myself. For case studies, I would record the interviews and run an agentic system that turns a transcript into a first draft, then I edit and you approve. One event becomes a week of social content through a repurposing system. For video, the AI tools handle atmosphere and titles, but the real footage of your work on site stays human-shot, because that is the actual selling asset and AI cannot fake it. And I am deliberate about what AI should not touch, the judgement, the brand fit, the factual accuracy, all of that stays with me and with your approval gate.

**Page 8 — My questions**
Which brings me to page eight, my questions. I will read these fairly closely, because each one is genuinely something I want to know, and a couple of them show you how I would think about the role.
1. How does the current data and system actually look, that is the social analytics, the brand-asset state, the website platform, which I gather is Wix, and the curated list of past projects? That determines how deep the Month one audits can really go.
2. How quickly can approvals turn around in practice, hours or days, and would you be open to a set of pre-approved templates or benchmarks, so the review gate protects quality without becoming a bottleneck?
3. How is AI currently used across Alltech, and is there appetite to move from chatbot-style exchanges to bespoke, agentic workflows that can run non-stop for days? This one is my most important, honestly, because it tells me how far I can take the systems I just described.
4. Which subscriptions are in place, Claude Team or Enterprise, would this role have access, and would you consider API access where a fixed plan caps the value?
5. Is anything deliberately off-limits to AI, say emails or contracts? I draw that line carefully, and I would want ours aligned from day one.
6. And if Alltech could be known for one thing in eighteen months, what would it be? Because the whole content engine should ladder up to that single position.

**Close**
That is the walkthrough. I have kept it deliberately as a working version one, so I would genuinely welcome where you would push back or reprioritise. That is the conversation I actually want to have. Thank you.
