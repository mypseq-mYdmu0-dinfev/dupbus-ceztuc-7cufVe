## 1. What I changed, and why

First, the draft is genuinely good. The three-phase shape (Immerse and Audit, Build Foundations, Execute and Own), the discipline of no live posts until October, the case-study-as-sales-asset logic, and the EOS scorecard cadence are all sound, so I have kept them. The brief is right that the draft is deliberately ambitious, and right that the tension between ambition and realism is the role. My task here was to keep the ambition and remove the parts that would quietly break in a real first quarter at 24 hours a week.

Five principles drove every change.

1. **Sequence around peak season, not against it.** The draft back-loads case-study production into September to December. That is exactly when, by the brief’s own admission, the technicians and PMs who hold the raw material are flat out and hard to pull for interviews. So I have moved the case-study extraction forward into the quiet July to August window, banked the raw material whilst the team is available and whilst I am still learning, and left the writing and publishing to run steadily afterwards. This one change de-risks the largest deliverable in the plan.
2. **Phase the build, do not parallelise it.** Reaching 75% independence across all six pillars by Month 3 asks one part-time person, still learning the business, to run six workstreams at once. I have re-cut this to full independence on the three highest-commercial pillars first (content and social, case studies, and HubSpot essentials), with website, sales enablement, and partnerships progressing alongside but reaching independence across Months 5 to 6.
3. **Make the hours honest.** 24 hours across three days is roughly 72 working hours a month. Each phase below carries a simple hour-budget, so the plan is something a real person can deliver rather than a wish-list.
4. **Respect the review gate, on both sides.** Every output passes through approval whilst trust is built, and the approver works part-time. Front-loading my output into the quiet window also front-loads the review burden onto Elena in the same period, so I have built approval buffers into the timelines, batched reviews where I can, and proposed a set of pre-approved templates, so the gate protects quality without becoming the bottleneck for either of us.
5. **Calibrate to the market.** Recent Australian industry surveys show event-volume confidence cooling, with the share of professionals planning more events in 2026 down sharply on 2025, and AV treated as a budget-pressured line item, with only around a third expanding their technology budgets. Organic, no-paid B2B content typically takes three to six months to produce meaningful inbound. So the targets below aim for steady share-of-voice and pipeline support, which is what the business actually needs, rather than hockey-stick numbers that would not survive contact with the quarter.

The net effect is a plan that is slightly less front-loaded, noticeably more deliverable, and still commercially ambitious where the ambition is earned.

## 2. Constraints I have built around

- **Peak season, roughly September to February.** Team availability for case-study interviews is scarce in this window and plentiful from April to August. The plan harvests raw material early.
- **24 hours a week across three days.** Each phase carries an explicit hour-budget.
- **Organic only for at least 12 months.** No paid acquisition, so the focus is brand, content, case studies, and sales enablement. Traction is a three-to-six-month proposition, and I have set expectations accordingly.
- **The review and approval gate.** Elena signs off on everything published early on, so I have allowed for turnaround time and proposed ways to keep it light.
- **A single headcount.** This is one person plus AI, not a team. The AI working method in Section 5 is what makes the numbers reachable.

## 5. How I would work with AI

The reason a single part-time person can credibly hit these targets is that I build AI systems to do the heavy lifting and keep the judgement for myself. In practice that means four things.

- **Case studies.** I record the PM and Seb interviews, then run a prompt-system that turns a transcript into a first-draft case study in the standard template. I edit for accuracy and brand, and Elena approves. This is what makes one to two a month sustainable alongside everything else.
- **Social.** One event becomes a week of content. A repurposing system drafts the LinkedIn, Instagram, and short-form variants from a single source, which I then shape and schedule.
- **Video.** For atmosphere, titles, and B-roll, current tools such as Veo and Runway produce social-ready clips at a fraction of the traditional cost. The authentic proof, the real footage of Alltech’s work on site, stays human-shot, because that is the actual selling asset and AI cannot fake it.
- **Research and reporting.** Competitor scans, monthly performance summaries, and first-draft proposals are AI-accelerated, then checked.

Two honest limits apply. AI accelerates volume and first drafts, not judgement, brand fit, or factual accuracy, so the approval gate stays. And I would document each of these systems as an SOP, so the capability outlasts me and the next hire inherits a working engine rather than a single person.

This is also why I am comfortable that I have not used HubSpot before. I have built and run other CRMs and systems, and the part of this role that matters is the ability to design a workflow, automate it, and document it, which is exactly what I do.
