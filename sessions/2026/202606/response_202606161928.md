27. Re 15.2 —— noted (your "120°C" runs cooler in practice; 100°C wouldn't dry, 110°C barely did, so 120°C is your sweet spot). No issue, carry on.

28. Re 15.3b —— THE food-safety answer (checked on Tefal's own site + food authorities in CIC, not just web_search):
- 28.1. PTFE (your Tefal's coating) is stable through all normal cooking. It only starts to SLOWLY break down at `~`260°C.
- 28.2. Real fume territory —— "polymer fume fever" (flu-like, temporary; outright lethal to pet birds) —— is `~`300–450°C, with serious breakdown above `~`350°C.
- 28.3. So 260°C is your red line. Tefal's official page only says "use per instructions, don't overheat"; the hard number is a property of the PTFE itself, confirmed by food-safety agencies.
- 28.4. Your three programmes, judged:
  - 28.4.1. 120°C × 5min (dry water) —— SAFE, miles under 260°C. Keep.
  - 28.4.2. 65°C × 15min (after cooking) —— SAFE. Keep.
  - 28.4.3. 100% × 5min (cooking) —— THIS is the one. 100% has NO temperature ceiling; left running, especially with the pan empty/near-empty, it can sail past 260°C. Kill it as-is.
- 28.5. The fix (worth doing exactly as you planned):
  - 28.5.1. Measure the 100% mode with your IR gun, run EMPTY to the 5-min mark —— that's the worst case and the true test.
  - 28.5.2. Replace it with a TEMPERATURE-CAPPED, auto-stop mode. Cap at `~`200–220°C: hotter than you ever need (eggs `~`160°C, prawns/noodles `~`180°C, a hard sear `~`220°C) yet keeps a `~`40°C margin under 260°C.
  - 28.5.3. Iron rule: never run an uncapped 100% with the pan EMPTY. Food or water pins the temperature near 100–200°C; an empty pan is the ONLY way it spikes into the danger zone.
- 28.6. Bottom line: your instinct is correct and worth acting on —— capping the 100% mode below 260°C removes BOTH the fume risk AND the single fastest coating-killer in one move.

31. Re 16.3 —— checked in CIC. Your Tefal React is modern, PFOA-free PTFE (the VB listing states "no PFOA, no lead, no cadmium"; Tefal's own site says PTFE is recognised food-safe by the WHO and EFSA).
- 31.1. The coating flaking into your food as it wears = biologically inert PTFE; it passes straight through. It is NOT poisoning you —— food agencies are clear on this.
- 31.2. The ONLY real PTFE health hazard is fumes from overheating an EMPTY pan above `~`260°C —— i.e. precisely the 100% habit we're capping in 28. Fix that and the health worry is essentially gone.
- 31.3. (Pedant's note: PTFE is technically a "PFAS", but it's a big, stable, non-absorbable polymer. The harmful PFAS are the small ones —— PFOA/PFOS —— used in OLD manufacturing, which Tefal removed years ago.)

32. Re 17.3 —— your two oils, opened in CIC and compared:
- 32.1. Yours: Woolworths Canola Spray, $3.00/400g = $0.75/100g, Health Star 4.5. Hers: Woolworths Extra Virgin Olive Oil Spray, $3.30/150g = $2.20/100g, Health Star 3.0.

- 32.3. Healthier? Hers (EVOO) genuinely is —— polyphenol antioxidants and the strongest heart-health evidence —— DESPITE the lower Health Star. (The Star algorithm scores all oils poorly and unfairly ranks EVOO below canola; ignore it for oils.) Your canola isn't bad either: low saturated fat, some omega-3, and a HIGHER smoke point, which actually makes it the better pick for hot frying.
- 32.4. Worth the price gap? As a SPRAY you use almost nothing per meal, so the extra is a few cents —— if you want the health edge, hers is worth it. For your hotter frying, canola's higher smoke point wins. It's not really either/or.
- 32.5. The catch nobody mentions —— and it bears on your coating fade: aerosol cooking sprays (BOTH of these) carry a propellant + emulsifier (lecithin) that bakes onto non-stick as a sticky varnish, builds up, and wears the coating faster. Tefal's and Anolon's own care guides warn against spray oils for exactly this reason.
- 32.6. Best move: ditch the aerosol can. Get a refillable PUMP oil-mister (`~`$10–15, Kmart/Robins Kitchen) and fill it with whatever oil you like —— same minimal-oil spritz, NO propellant residue, kinder to the coating, and you can mist cheap bulk EVOO for the health win. Coating-life + health + cost, all at once.
