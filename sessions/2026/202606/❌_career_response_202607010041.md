# Response to career_query_202607010041.md
*"Australian Experience" Barrier —— CIC-Validated*

## Verdict

1. The belief is **PARTLY TRUE —— and the strong version is the more accurate one for the labour-market OUTCOME, but the mechanism is "discount", not literal "zeroing".**
- 1.1. "You need Australian experience" is real, measured, and the single most-cited barrier by migrants themselves —— not just anecdote.
- 1.2. BUT the data shows overseas experience/qualifications are **discounted (penalised but still count)**, not formally **disregarded (zeroed)**. Two different things; see §4.
- 1.3. Honest gap up front: there is **NO clean first-party "% of employers who disregard overseas experience" figure** in the AU gov/research corpus. I did not find one and did not fabricate one. The citable numbers measure the *outcome* (underemployment, wage gap) and the *migrant-reported barrier*, not an employer-survey "% who discount". See §5.

## Key Statistics (all CIC-validated on the live source page)

2. First-party —— AU Government (ABS).
- 2.1. **65%** —— of recent migrants who reported difficulty finding their first job, the most common difficulty was "a lack of Australian work experience or references". This is the headline citable number for the barrier itself.
  - 2.1.1. Measures: top self-reported difficulty (of the 33% who had any difficulty).
  - 2.1.2. Next: lack of local contacts/networks 29%; language difficulties 25%.
  - 2.1.3. Year: Nov 2019 (released 12/06/2020) —— ABS "Characteristics of Recent Migrants" (CoRMS). This IS the latest release; ABS has not re-run CoRMS since. Flag the dataset age.
  - 2.1.4. Source: https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/people/people-and-communities/characteristics-recent-migrants/latest-release
- 2.2. **90%** —— of recent migrants who *applied* to have their overseas qualification recognised, had it recognised (same ABS release). This is the key counter-nuance: formal *qualification* non-recognition is NOT the main barrier; it is *experience/network* discounting. See §4.2.
- 2.3. **54%** —— of recent migrants who had a job since arrival used their highest non-school qualification in their first job (i.e. ~46% did not). Same source.

3. First-party —— Research bodies (Deloitte/SSI; CEDA).
- 3.1. **44%** —— "almost half of migrants and refugees in Australia are working in roles below their skill level".
  - 3.1.1. Scope: recent permanent migrants across skilled, family, and humanitarian pathways (NOT skilled-only).
  - 3.1.2. Source report: "Billion Dollar Benefit", by **Deloitte Access Economics**, commissioned by **Settlement Services International (SSI)**; released 20 Jun 2024 (2023 data).
  - 3.1.3. Same report: **$70 billion** could be added to the economy over 10 years, and **~621,000** migrants are unable to work to full potential, if permanent migrants worked to skill at the Australian-born rate.
  - 3.1.4. Source (CIC-read): https://www.ssi.org.au/media-centre/media-releases/skills-shortage-solution-620000-hidden-workers-activated/
- 3.2. **~1 in 4 (almost 25%)** —— permanent **SKILLED** migrants working in a job beneath their skill level. This is the skilled-only subset (lower than the 44% all-stream figure).
  - 3.2.1. Source: **CEDA**, "Making better use of migrants' skills", 21 Mar 2024 (drawing on CEDA's 2021 research).
  - 3.2.2. Source (CIC-read): https://www.ceda.com.au/research-and-policy/research/population/making-better-use-of-migrants-skills
- 3.3. **>10%** —— migrants in Australia 2–6 years earn more than 10% less than otherwise-similar Australian-born workers; the gap takes **~15 years** to close. Same CEDA report —— this is the "birthplace wage gap" figure requested.
- 3.4. **~9%** —— weaker English proficiency reduces recent-migrant wages by ~9% on average (CEDA).
- 3.5. **≥20%** —— recent migrants earn at least 20% less than similar Australian-born workers where educated in a licensed field (e.g. health, teaching, accounting, trades) but not currently working in it (CEDA). This is the occupational-licensing penalty.

4. First-party —— Occupation-specific.
- 4.1. **50%** —— only half of overseas-born qualified engineers in Australia's labour force actually work in engineering; many drive taxis/Ubers or stack shelves. Source: The Conversation (3 Sep 2025, Deakin/ECU academics), cross-citing the CEDA and Deloitte/SSI data. https://theconversation.com/underuse-of-migrants-skills-is-costing-us-billions-discrimination-often-starts-at-the-job-interview-263635

## Nuance —— Disregarded vs Discounted

5. It is **discounted, not disregarded** —— overseas experience still "counts", but is penalised.
- 5.1. Evidence it is NOT zeroed: 90% qualification-recognition rate (§2.2); skilled migrants do get hired (just often below level); wages do catch up over ~15 yr (§3.3). If experience were literally disregarded, none of these would hold.
- 5.2. Evidence it is heavily penalised: 44% below skill level (§3.1); >10% wage gap (§3.3); 65% cite missing Australian experience as the top barrier (§2.1).

6. It varies strongly —— the discount is not uniform.
- 6.1. By **occupation**: worst in licensed/regulated fields (≥20% penalty, §3.5) and specific professions (engineering 50%, §4.1); the licensing system, not employer whim, is a large part of the mechanism here.
- 6.2. By **English / accent**: ~9% wage hit (§3.4); qualitative research found rejection "cloaked" as "communication problems" even for fluent applicants with non-Anglo accents/names.
- 6.3. By **source country / name**: academic interview research (Parris, McAlinden, Jogulu —— Deakin/ECU, 2025) found culturally/racially marginalised migrants (e.g. South/Central Asian, Northeast Asian) often filtered out before interview on name alone. Qualitative, not a %.
- 6.4. By **recency / stream**: gap is largest in the first 2–6 years and for family/humanitarian streams (44% all-stream vs ~25% skilled-only); narrows with time in country.

## Honest Gaps

7. What the data does NOT give (do not cite a number that doesn't exist).
- 7.1. **No first-party "% of employers who require/prefer Australian experience"** —— recruiter surveys (SEEK/Hays/Robert Walters) were not validated here and would be secondary anyway; the gov/research corpus measures migrant-reported barriers and outcomes, not an employer-side "% who discount".
- 7.2. The flagship ABS barrier stat (§2.1) is **2019** —— the most recent ABS CoRMS, but ageing; flag the year when citing.
- 7.3. AHRC, DCA Inclusion@Work, Scanlon "Mapping Social Cohesion", and OECD were triaged but NOT yet CIC-validated for a *specific* "Australian experience"/birthplace-discount figure; Scanlon/ABS GSS give general discrimination prevalence (~1 in 6 overseas-born report discrimination/yr) but not an experience-disregard %. Say so rather than overclaim.

## Bottom Line for Citation

8. Safest, strongest citable set: **65%** (ABS, barrier) + **44%** (Deloitte/SSI, underemployment) + **>10% wage gap / 15-yr catch-up** (CEDA). All first-party, all CIC-read live. Frame as "discounted/underutilised", not "disregarded".
