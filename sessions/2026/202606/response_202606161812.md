13. Re 3.1 (and 5.1/2, 9.2) —— yes, exactly right. Two constructions are immune to the disc-drop:
- 13.1. Whole-body magnetic steel/stainless (the metal IS the pan, so induction grabs the body, no separate disc) with PTFE non-stick inside —— this is the ideal you're describing.
- 13.2. OR any pan with no bonded disc at all, like your wife's solid carbon-steel BK.
- 13.3. The common thread: no dissimilar-metal disc-bond = nothing to fatigue and let go. A "fully-clad" pan (steel-aluminium-steel fused all the way up the walls) counts too, because the base is one continuous body, not a stuck-on plate.

14. Re 3.5 / 6.3 —— your wife is partly right, and there's a free win here.
- 14.1. Every time you boil water in it, that's one EXTRA full heat-up/cool-down cycle on top of the two daily cooks —— more fatigue, faster death. So yes, dropping it slows the detachment.
- 14.2. But honestly it only slows it modestly —— the two cooking cycles are still the bulk of the damage.
- 14.3. Better than just stopping (which hurts cleaning): don't use the INDUCTION to boil. Pour in kettle-boiled or hot-tap water and let it soak. Same residue-loosening for the dishwasher, but ZERO extra induction heat-cycle on the disc. Best of both.
- 14.4. Bonus: boiling water in a near-empty pan is half-way to dry-firing (little thermal mass, base runs hot) —— the kettle-pour dodges that too.

15. Re 4.1 —— NO, don't switch to 100%. Use the 120°C auto-stop. This one matters.
- 15.1. "Not the root cause" does NOT mean "harmless" —— dry-firing is a real accelerant. Hotter + longer + empty = more stress on the bond.
- 15.2. 100% rockets past 200°C and never stops; 120°C flashes the water off and cuts out. Water leaves at 100°C, so 120°C is already overkill —— you gain nothing from 200°C except damage.
- 15.3. 100% is worse on TWO counts: (a) bigger thermal shock → faster disc fatigue; (b) PTFE starts breaking down above `~`260°C, and a screaming-hot empty pan can get there → that's the actual health risk (fumes), not the flaking (see 16).
- 15.4. Best of all: don't dry-fire at all. Towel-dry, or just let the dishwasher's own dry cycle handle it —— the pan doesn't need to be bone-dry going INTO a dishwasher.

16. Re 4.2 —— good observation, and it reframes the whole thing.
- 16.1. You're right that the coating typically fades BEFORE the disc drops. That means non-stick is inherently a consumable —— the coating has a shelf-life no matter what's under it. Even a perfect no-disc pan will eventually go non-non-stick.
- 16.2. So "a pan that lasts years" and "stays truly non-stick" are almost a contradiction —— the disc is one death, the coating fade is the other, and the coating usually goes first.
- 16.3. Health (re 6.2): modern PTFE is PFOA-free; tiny flakes that come off into food are inert and pass straight through —— not poisonous. The genuine PTFE hazard is FUMES from overheating an empty pan above `~`260°C. Which is one more reason to kill the 100% dry-firing, not the pan.
