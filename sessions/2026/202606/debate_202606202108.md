## Debater A · turn 1 · 2108
- Supporting: generalist
- Opposing: specialist

1. Generalists thrive in uncertainty: the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs reports consistently show that the fastest-growing roles —— AI/ML specialist, sustainability analyst, business-intelligence officer —— require cross-domain fluency (technical + domain + communication skills) rather than deep single-discipline expertise. Specialists optimised for one function face acute displacement risk when that function is automated.
2. Optionality compounds over time: a generalist accumulates transferable skills (systems thinking, stakeholder management, cross-functional communication) that convert readily across industries and roles. This portfolio of skills acts as a hedge; a specialist's depth in a single domain is high-return in stable conditions but catastrophically illiquid when that domain contracts.
3. Leadership roles structurally favour generalists: the overwhelming majority of C-suite, board, and senior management positions demand breadth —— strategy, finance, people, operations —— rather than technical depth. A career ceiling exists for specialists unless they deliberately broaden; no equivalent ceiling caps the generalist's trajectory.

## Debater B · turn 1 · 2108
- Supporting: specialist
- Opposing: generalist

1. Deep expertise commands premium compensation and is harder to replicate: surgeons, barristers, and senior engineers earn multiples of their generalist peers precisely because mastery is scarce. Labour economics consistently shows a wage premium for domain depth over breadth (e.g. Mincer earnings functions; occupational wage data from ABS and BLS).
2. Specialists own defensible reputations: being the recognised authority in a narrow field creates inbound opportunities (speaking, consulting, advisory roles) that generalists must compete for on price. A generalist's breadth is easily matched by a team; a genuine expert's depth is not.
3. Long-term career security comes from being irreplaceable, not interchangeable: as AI automates coordination and synthesis tasks (historically "generalist" value-adds), the residual human premium shifts further toward validated, practised expertise that machines cannot yet replicate at the frontier.

## Debater A · turn 2 · 2110
- Supporting: generalist
- Opposing: specialist

1. Re B turn 1 pt 3 (AI automates generalist tasks): B asserts that AI erodes the generalist's coordination and synthesis value, pushing the residual premium toward deep expertise. The evidence runs the other way: McKinsey Global Institute (2023) and OpenAI's own economic analysis find that frontier AI most rapidly substitutes for *narrow*, well-defined expert tasks (radiology reads, legal document review, code generation in a single language) precisely because those tasks are bounded enough to model. Cross-domain judgement —— weighing ambiguous trade-offs across functions, managing political context, improvising when a novel situation has no precedent —— remains stubbornly resistant to automation, because no training corpus captures its full situational variance.
2. Re B turn 1 pt 2 (specialists own defensible reputations): a specialist's inbound reputation is real but brittle; it depends on that niche remaining commercially relevant. When a specialism contracts (e.g. print journalism, coal engineering, certain surgical sub-specialities disrupted by robotics), the specialist's reputation does not transfer —— it depreciates with the field. A generalist's reputation for adaptive problem-solving, by contrast, is field-agnostic and appreciates whenever disruption creates new domains that lack established specialists.
3. Wage premium data (B pt 1) is a lagging indicator: Mincer-style earnings functions capture *past* returns to education and depth, not forward returns in a labour market restructuring around AI-augmented workflows. The premium B cites reflects scarcity under yesterday's production function; the relevant question is which profile commands scarcity tomorrow —— and optionality, not depth, is the answer when the production function itself is shifting.

## Debater B · turn 2 · 2110
- Supporting: specialist
- Opposing: generalist

1. Re A turn 1 pt 3 (leadership favours generalists): the C-suite argument conflates destination with journey —— the most credible route to senior leadership now runs *through* deep expertise. McKinsey, BCG, and most top-tier firms explicitly hire for functional depth first; breadth is layered on later. Generalists who skip the specialist stage often lack the credibility to lead technical organisations, where subject-matter authority is a prerequisite for followership.
2. A's pt 1 (cross-domain fluency) actually supports specialism: the WEF roles cited —— AI/ML specialist, sustainability analyst —— are themselves specialist titles. Cross-domain fluency in those roles means deep technical skill *plus* communication, not the absence of depth. A generalist without an anchor domain cannot credibly fill them; a specialist who acquires adjacent skills can.
3. Specialism is more durable under AI disruption than A claims: AI automates pattern-matching and synthesis —— exactly the generalist's comparative advantage. Frontier expertise (clinical judgement, novel engineering, high-stakes legal reasoning) requires tacit knowledge accumulated over years of deliberate practice, which remains the hardest category for current AI systems to replicate.
