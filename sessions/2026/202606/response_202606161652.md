# Response to query_202606161652.md
*Induction disc fatigue —— cause found, buy advice*

1. Verdict —— the part that fell off is the pan's induction base-disc, and it failed from metal-fatigue at the disc-to-aluminium bond, driven by your induction hob, with the dishwasher as accelerant. Not the coating, not bad luck, and not (this time) dry-firing.

2. What the evidence shows
- 2.1. Your hob is induction. The Mi page title reads 米家電磁爐2 (電磁爐 = induction, NOT 電陶爐 radiant). Confirmed live in CIC.
- 2.2. Induction makes heat INSIDE the steel disc only (eddy currents). The aluminium body above is heated second-hand, by conduction from that disc.
- 2.3. IMG_2204 is exactly that disc: thin, perforated, with aluminium pressed through the holes to grip it. This is a press-fit/riveted induction plate —— the cheapest, weakest way to make an aluminium pan induction-ready.
- 2.4. The clincher: once the disc fell off, the hob "can barely fire up" the pan —— because bare aluminium is non-magnetic, so induction has almost nothing left to grab. That symptom only happens on induction, and it proves the disc WAS the heating part.

3. The mechanism —— why it detaches
- 3.1. Steel and aluminium expand at different rates when heated (aluminium roughly twice as much).
- 3.2. Induction heats the disc hard and fast whilst the aluminium lags, so the two layers fight each other at the exact bond line —— every single heat-up and cool-down.
- 3.3. Repeated hundreds of times (you cook brekkie + dinner daily), the little aluminium "rivets" in the perforations work-harden and shear, and the disc lets go. Textbook low-cycle thermal fatigue.
- 3.4. The dishwasher is the accelerant: hot alkaline detergent corrodes the exposed aluminium at the disc rim (aluminium is eaten by high pH), chewing the bond from the edge inward. The grey pitting on the disc edge in IMG_2204 is that corrosion.
- 3.5. "Boil water after use, then straight into the dishwasher" piles on extra hot→cold shocks —— more fatigue, faster.

4. Clearing the suspects
- 4.1. Dry-firing empty at full power (your wife's theory): a genuine stressor, but you STOPPED it and the pan still failed —— so it was never the root cause, only a past accelerant. That actually frees you from worrying about it.
- 4.2. The non-stick coating: innocent. It was still working when the disc dropped. Coating wear and disc loss are unrelated; the disc kills the pan long before the Teflon wears out.
- 4.3. So there's no single villain. It's the COMBINATION —— induction (heats the disc) + aluminium-bodied non-stick pan (needs a bonded disc) + dishwasher (corrodes the bond). Remove any one leg and the failure largely disappears.

5. Why every pan you've owned dies the same way
- 5.1. Every cheap induction non-stick saucepan —— Woolies, Anko, Kmart, even Tefal —— is built identically: a light aluminium body (for the non-stick and low weight) with a steel disc stuck on the bottom (for induction).
- 5.2. That disc-bond is the universal weak point. Cheap discs fail in `~`1 month; Tefal's better disc lasts `~`3–5 months. Same death, just slower.
- 5.3. Your wife's BK pan is immune for ONE reason: it's a solid carbon-STEEL body (with a PFAS-free GlazeGuard ceramic coating) —— magnetic all the way through, with no separate disc to drop (IMG_2205 shows a unified base, no disc ring). It DOES have a ceramic non-stick, but ceramic is far less slippery than PTFE and wears fast, which is exactly why it's poor for your daily eggs. Your instinct to reject it was correct —— and it's currently unavailable on Amazon AU anyway.

6. The uncomfortable truth —— the trilemma
- 6.1. You're asking for five things at once: induction + real (PTFE) non-stick + dishwasher + a single small saucepan + under $80.
- 6.2. "Real non-stick, cheap and light" forces an aluminium body. "Induction" then forces a steel disc bonded onto it. "Dishwasher" then corrodes that bond. The disc is baked into your own requirements.
- 6.3. An identical replacement (another React, or any "induction" aluminium pan) is GUARANTEED to fail the same way. So "buy the same again" is out —— I can't honestly sell you that.
- 6.4. You can only escape by removing ONE leg: change the PAN's construction, change the HOB type, or accept disposable pans.

7. The money angle (with a twist I found on the page)
- 7.1. Cheap pan: `~`$15, lasts `~`2.5 months → about $6/month.
- 7.2. The $80 you paid for the Tefal, over its `~`2.5–5 month life (2nd: 17 Oct → 4 Jan ≈ 2.5; 3rd: 4 Jan → now ≈ 5), worked out at about $16–32/month —— roughly 3× the cheap pans. So at $80 it WAS a poor trade: you paid for a nicer coating, but the disc (not the coating) is what kills the pan.
- 7.3. BUT —— the same React is right now $30 at Victoria's Basement (RRP $124.99, 75% off clearance), not $80. At $30 it works out at only `~`$6–12/month —— basically level with a cheap Kmart pan, but with a tougher Titanium coating and a French build. That flips the verdict: at $30 the React is a GOOD disposable, not a bad upgrade.
- 7.4. Caveat: $30 is clearance stock —— it may sell out and not return at that price, so treat it as a buy-now (or stock-up-now) deal, not a forever price.

8. Recommendation —— pick one path
- 8.1. PATH A —— accept the disc, buy cheap and treat as consumable: the smartest consumable right now is actually the $30 clearance React itself at Victoria's Basement (better coating than a $15 Kmart pan, same `~`$/month, your trusted store) —— grab one or two whilst it's $30. If/when that clearance dries up, drop back to $15–20 Kmart/Woolies non-stick saucepans, swapped each quarter. Either way: never dry-fire, and let the pan cool before the dishwasher.
- 8.2. PATH B —— actually END the disc failures: buy a pan with NO separate disc, i.e. a fully-clad tri-ply or solid magnetic-stainless body, where the magnetic layer IS the pan (nothing to delaminate) and the stainless outside shrugs off the dishwasher.
  - 8.2.1. Honest catch: in your trusted stores, a pan that is integral-body AND real non-stick AND a saucepan AND under $80 basically doesn't exist right now —— I looked.
  - 8.2.2. Closest real buy: Tramontina Grano tri-ply non-stick FRYPAN 20cm (`~`$80, no disc, dishwasher-safe, fries eggs/prawns beautifully, should last years). Caveat: the coating is PFAS-free "Starflon", not classic Teflon —— a touch less slippery, but fine for eggs. It's a frypan, not a saucepan, so boil noodles in it (shallow but workable) or in a cheap stainless pot.
  - 8.2.3. If you insist on the saucepan shape, the integral-body ones in-store (e.g. Merten & Storck tri-ply) are bare stainless —— durable forever but NOT non-stick, so no good for your eggs.
- 8.3. PATH C —— root-cause fix, bigger change: swap the induction hob for a radiant ceramic hob (電陶爐, `~`$30–80). It heats ANY flat pan from below, so you no longer need a disc at all → use the cheapest, lightest plain-aluminium non-stick pans forever, with nothing to fall off. Trade-off: slower to heat, and the glass top gets hot and stays hot. Best total economics IF you're willing to change the hob.
- 8.4. AVOID: paying FULL price (`~`$80–125) for any aluminium-body "induction" pan with a stuck-on disc, the React included —— at full price you're buying a guaranteed disc-failure at a premium. At $30 clearance it's a different story (see 8.1).

9. In-store test —— works on any pan in 5 seconds
- 9.1. Slide a fridge magnet across the WHOLE underside. Integral body = strong, even pull from centre to edge (often grips up the side too). Disc pan = strong only in the centre, fading to nothing at the rim where bare aluminium shows.
- 9.2. Run a fingernail around the base edge. A felt lip/ridge/ring = a separate disc was added (reject it). Smooth metal running to the rim = clad/integral (good).
- 9.3. Tap the base. Dead, dull tone = solid; hollow, tinny or rattly = a disc that will eventually drop.

10. My honest thoughts
- 10.1. The cause is settled, with physical proof in hand (the perforated disc), a confirmed induction hob, and a textbook fatigue mechanism behind it. I'm confident, not guessing.
- 10.2. Your real problem was never "which pan" —— it's that four fixed habits (induction, daily frying, dishwasher, one cheap small pan) mathematically force a disc, and a disc on induction always eventually drops. No $80 saucepan escapes that.
- 10.3. If I had to choose for you: the lowest-effort answer is Path A using the $30 clearance React —— same routine, your store, good coating, throwaway price. The smartest LONG-term answer is Path C (radiant hob), which kills the problem at the source and unlocks the cheapest pans permanently.
- 10.4. PATH B (Tramontina frypan) is the buy if you want "one nice pan that lasts and fries well" and can live with a frypan shape, a non-Teflon coating, and the top of your budget.
- 10.5. What I would NOT do: pay $80 (or RRP $125) for a like-for-like aluminium+disc pan —— that's premium pricing for a guaranteed re-run. The same pan at $30 is fine; the price, not the pan, was the mistake.
