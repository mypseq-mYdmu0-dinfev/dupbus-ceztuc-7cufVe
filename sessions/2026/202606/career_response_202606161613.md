# Response to career_query_202606161613.md
*WIB display —— strong instinct, segment by role*

1. Verdict first.
- 1.1. The WIB *instinct* is correct and well-supported. AU interview guidance (Hays, Robert Walters, SEEK) all land the "tell me about yourself" answer on "the skills/experience you bring to help them succeed", not a CV recital. So "bring to the table" is the right organising idea.
- 1.2. But the *artefact* as drawn (glossy hero-shot infographic, used as a Zoom background and shown on your Fold mid-interview) is high-reward for creative/marketing roles and high-RISK for consulting/BA/IT/corporate ones. My core counter is one line: don't build a single universal asset —— segment it. Everything below expands that.

2. Your verbal opening —— keep it, it works.
- 2.1. Leading with the discipline arc (multimedia 2010 → producer/director 2015 → marketing 2018 → consulting 2020 → numbers) is genuinely clever, because it converts your one liability (a messy "last job": KE advisor → HKEF → KE again) into your one asset (rare multidisciplinary range). You were right not to open on "my last job".
- 2.2. Numbers as the closer are tall-poppy-safe. AU culture distrusts boastful adjectives but accepts objective figures, so "300 projects, 100 clients, 10 countries" reads as fact, not brag. Keep delivering it flatly, not triumphantly.
- 2.3. One AU-norm tweak: the local default is Present → Past → Future, yours is Past → Present. Consider a one-line present anchor first, e.g. "I'm a cross-disciplinary operator with 16⁺ years across four fields, and here's how I got here", then run the timeline. It gives the listener the "now" before the journey.
- 2.4. Caveat you already flagged, confirmed: drop the multimedia/producer granularity for non-creative roles. For consulting, open on consulting + strategy and fold the rest into "a background spanning content, marketing and operations".

3. The central risk —— is a self-made interview visual "proper" in SYD?
- 3.1. Honest answer: it depends entirely on the role, and the downside is real. Search consensus is that unrequested visual aids can read as "presumptuous or gimmicky", and that the candidate (not the aid) should remain the focus.
- 3.2. Stacked on top of that is tall-poppy syndrome —— 1 in 3 AU workers report experiencing it; humility is prized and overt self-promotion is viewed with suspicion. A polished slide titled "Signature Strengths" beside a posed hero shot of yourself is precisely the stimulus that can trigger the "show-off" read in a conservative interviewer.
- 3.3. So segment by role family:
  - 3.3.1. Creative / marketing / design / product → YES. The visual itself is evidence of the skill, it is on-brand, and it reads as portfolio rather than vanity. Note though: at Rennly it was your *built* AI reel, not a self-slide, that left them "speechless". Showing WORK beats showing a self-summary even here.
  - 3.3.2. Consulting / BA / IT / corporate / government → DEFAULT NO, or a stripped-back version only (no hero shot, no table pun, no phone display; a clean one-page competency summary at most). For these audiences gravitas outranks production value, and the flashy version can quietly cost you.
- 3.4. Net: treat the full WIB as a creative-role instrument, not a universal one.

4. E1 (Zoom background) —— specific cautions.
- 4.1. Drop the duplicate static self-image. Stacking live-you over image-you is fragile; any movement misaligns the two heads, and "two of you" reads as unintentionally comic. Let *live-you* be the centre hero and place the four quadrants in the corners around your live frame.
- 4.2. You intend the background only during the intro, but switching virtual backgrounds mid-call is a visible, clunky few-click action that draws attention to the device and breaks your flow. And for those ~30s a text-dense backdrop still competes with your face. Keep E1 sparse: title + four short quadrant labels only, with the detailed copy pushed to E2.
- 4.3. Keying quality: AI/Zoom background removal halos around hair and clips text sitting behind you. Busy backgrounds expose poor keying. If you proceed, key in OBS with even lighting and keep a clear dead zone behind your torso and where your hands gesture.

5. E2 (physical interview / Fold display) —— the riskiest move.
- 5.1. Showing interviewers a polished self-summary on your phone mid-interview is the single biggest gadget-flex element here. Risks: it feels staged, the screen is small for 2`~`3 viewers, it interrupts the natural close, and it amplifies the tall-poppy read.
- 5.2. Better options, in order: (a) a printed A4 one-pager as a leave-behind, which is conventional and lets them keep or circulate it; (b) the image as your own private cue only, not shown to them; (c) for creative roles only, the Fold wrap as a closing beat —— but read the room before producing it.
- 5.3. If a hero shot stays in E2: the white shirt / navy is clean and neutral, good. The clasped-hands WWDC pose is recognisable enough to read as imitative to some —— a natural, relaxed posture is the safer choice.

6. Content (A1`~`A4) —— accuracy and balance.
- 6.1. Accuracy (no false claims, and consistency with your resume so a tile never contradicts your CV):
  - 6.1.1. A1 "IT Master's" → label it as it appears on your CV, "Master of HCI (Data Analytics)". "IT Master's" overstates and invites a mismatch question.
  - 6.1.2. A2 "Data Analytics Major" → it is a sub-major/specialisation per your profile, so "Major" slightly overstates; use "Data Analytics specialisation".
  - 6.1.3. A3 strengths and A4 numbers → both consistent with your profile and the generic-claim rule. Safe as drawn.
- 6.2. Your implicit logic is actually sound: top row (A1 innovation + A2 analytics) = "the edge I recently sharpened"; bottom row (A3 strengths + A4 track record) = "what I have long proven". Make that row-story deliberate so it does not read as four unrelated tiles.
- 6.3. Balance: A1/A2 carry one line each, A3/A4 carry three, so the top tiles will look thin. Either add one concrete proof to A1/A2, or style them clearly as "headline + single source" so the asymmetry looks intentional.
- 6.4. Copy tone: noun phrases plus numbers, zero superlatives ("world-class", "exceptional"). Numbers are humble-safe; adjectives are tall-poppy bait.

7. Title and table pun.
- 7.1. Make sure "What Do I Bring To The Table" reads cleanly as the full sentence at first glance. The extended vertical "I" should be a second-read aesthetic layer, not something that collapses line 2 into "The I Table", which scans as a typo to anyone who does not know your portfolio cover. Cleverness that needs explaining misfires in the few seconds an interviewer actually looks.
- 7.2. The literal table (Apple-store style) as the "bring stuff to the table" pun is charming for creative roles; keep it a thin, restrained silhouette that frames rather than clutters, and cut it entirely from the corporate/stripped version.

8. Technical flaw in the E1 → E2 crop plan.
- 8.1. The numbers do not reconcile. E1 = 1920×1080 (16:9). You want E2 = 2208×1768 (≈5:4) by cropping "only left/right, top/bottom unchanged". But if top and bottom are unchanged, E2 height must stay 1080, and cropping 16:9 down to 5:4 yields 1348×1080, not 2208×1768.
- 8.2. To then reach 2208×1768 you would upscale ~1.64×, which softens every element on the Fold's sharp, close-held panel —— the opposite of what you want there.
- 8.3. 2208×1768 is the right target (it matches the Fold2 inner panel), which is exactly why E2 should be authored *natively* at that resolution as its own tight composition (shared brand, re-laid-out elements), not a mechanical crop of E1. A deliberately sparse E1 rarely crops into a well-composed E2 anyway.

9. What I would actually do.
- 9.1. Build two variants from one design system: E1-Creative (sparse Zoom background, live-you as hero, minimal labels) and E2-Creative (native Fold one-pager, full content, optional hero shot).
- 9.2. Add a third, E0-Corporate: a single understated A4 PDF (no hero shot, no pun) for consulting/BA/IT, or nothing at all, leaning on the verbal opening plus your portfolio link.
- 9.3. Keep the WIB as a *closing wrap*, always secondary to your live presence and to actual work shown. Your Rennly result proves the order: built work first, self-summary a distant second.
