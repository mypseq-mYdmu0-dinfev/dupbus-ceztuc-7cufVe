37. The one thing I would push hard on —— "win and dominate the room".
- 37.1. An interview is not a debate to win; it is a fit test. "Dominating" or "checkmating" can read as needing to be the smartest person present, which —— especially in AU (tall-poppy) and for any collaborative or reporting-in role —— triggers the silent question "can I work with him, or will he overshadow me and talk down to the juniors?". That question sinks otherwise strong candidates.
- 37.2. The master version of your own skill is the inverse: make the OTHER person feel smart and understood, and let your authority be felt quietly rather than asserted. Resonance beats conquest in a room that is deciding whether to live with you for years.
- 37.3. You already know this —— your Alltech line ("don't need to bully, stay humble") is exactly right. I am only making it the default rather than the exception: dominance is a capability to hold in reserve, almost never a mode to actually run.

38. Two smaller cautions on the framing.
- 38.1. "I always win / nobody has crossed as many domains as me" —— keep the confidence, drop the absolute. Its failure mode is survivorship bias (you remember the rooms you won) and quiet complacency on the day you meet someone sharper, which does eventually happen. A veteran who assumes he might meet his match stays sharper and actually reads as more secure, not less.
- 38.2. The "keyboard fighter" disdain for specialists —— keep the analysis (find their commercial blind spot), lose any contempt. It is often untrue (plenty of engineers are deeply commercial), and for your IT/data/BA targets you will work WITH and FOR these people. A "checkmate them" reflex is a poor foundation; respect the specialist, because you need them more than you need to beat them.
