## 1. Introduction

In knowledge-based firms (KBFs), individual expertise constitutes the primary source of competitive advantage (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992). The strategic architecture of KBFs (spanning mainly legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, IT, and R&D sectors) depends on the continuous integration of specialist knowledge distributed across individuals, rendering knowledge management (KM) a governance priority rather than a peripheral function. Any mechanism that disrupts this integration is thus not a mere interpersonal matter, but an organisation-level risk with measurable commercial consequences.

CYC is a management consulting firm (MCF) specialising in KM, advising KBF clients on the governance conditions that sustain or inhibit effective knowledge contribution[1]. Across its client engagements, CYC has identified a recurring phenomenon carrying direct implications for KM performance, namely Tall Poppy Syndrome (TPS). Theorised by Feather (1989, 1994) as the social disposition whereby high-performing individuals face peer marginalisation in response to their standout performance, TPS represents a threat to the social conditions on which voluntary knowledge sharing (KS) depends. In KBFs, where individual intellectual performance constitutes the organisation’s core commercial offering, TPS is amplified precisely because excellence is simultaneously required and inherently comparative. When a Tall Poppy (TP) is subjected to peer marginalisation, a reactive recalibration of knowledge contribution follows (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021); KS becomes a vulnerability rather than a reciprocal exchange, and the social trust underpinning effective KM erodes progressively. This mechanism converts peer-level interpersonal friction into a boardroom-level strategic concern and latent KM governance risk, particularly for KBFs whose competitive position is inextricable from the depth and availability of their practitioners’ knowledge.

The scale of this risk is evidenced across industry research. Survey data indicate that **87.3%** of high-performing professionals report colleagues actively undermining their achievements (Billan, 2023), whilst disengagement costs approximately **US$10 trillion[2] annually** (Gallup, 2026). In knowledge-intensive organisations, employee departure results in measurable losses of institutional knowledge, productivity, and talent continuity (Banerjee et al., 2023). Internal knowledge is the single highest-rated innovation source across organisations, outranking any single external factor, including clients, suppliers, and competitors, yet employees routinely withhold innovative input when they feel disengaged or perceive their managerial environment as unreceptive (Beatson, 2024). Only 51% employees deem their managers as “good at seeking their views”; only 37% say they are “allowed to influence final decisions” (Young and Tong, 2025, p.29), exposing the management vacuum in which TPS dynamics operate without intervention. The Australian context compounds these conditions, with workplace stress as a driver of “unscheduled absence” cited by 57% employers, up from 42% in 2023; and 33% of employers reporting increased conflict issues in 2024 (AHRI, 2025, p.12). Additionally, 35% of organisations indicate their leaders as “not proficient” in team development and people management (AHRI, 2026, p.6). For KBFs, these deficiencies translate into an elevated exposure to TPS.

Notwithstanding extensive literature on TPS in cultural psychology (Feather, 1989, 1994) and its documented presence in professional workplaces (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), management-oriented application within KBF contexts remains a literature gap. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon driven by group dynamics that transcend individual firm boundaries; studying it within a single organisation risks conflating findings with that firm’s idiosyncratic culture, limiting possibilities to isolate TPS as the explanatory variable. A cross-firm sampling design is, therefore, more methodologically appropriate, consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025). CYC’s primary client base spans KBFs broadly across multiple sectors; the research participant pool is drawn from within this base, providing the cross-firm diversity this study requires.

### Research Aim

To investigate how TPS dynamics in KBFs generate team cohesion and knowledge governance challenges, surfacing management implications for practitioners.

### Research Questions

RQ1. What conditions within KBFs exacerbate or alleviate TPS?
RQ2. How do team members in KBFs experience TPS-related team dynamics?

### Research Objectives

RO1. Critically review existing literature on TPS, team dynamics, and KM in KBFs.
RO2. Explore how TPs’ peers (TPPs) in KBFs perceive and experience TPS dynamics.
RO3. Evaluate the organisational and interpersonal conditions that exacerbate or alleviate TPS.
RO4. Formulate management recommendations addressing TPS-related KM and team cohesion risks.

This study contributes academically by extending TPS studies beyond cultural psychology into management and KM domains, and practically by equipping managers with workforce-derived insights to address an under-recognised governance challenge. For CYC, the findings will inform a cross-sector advisory framework supporting KBF clients in diagnosing and mitigating the knowledge governance risks associated with TPS.

### Structure of Chapters (pre-drafted for Assessment 3 full paper)

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 TPS Foundation

TPS was theorised by Feather (1989) as a culturally rooted disposition towards marginalising high achievers, manifesting as an ambivalent attitudinal pattern in which admiration for standout performance coexists with a drive to devalue and socially distance the individual who embodies it. Empirically operationalised through the “Tall Poppy Scale”, Feather (1989) confirmed that satisfaction at a TP’s fall strengthens amongst peers with higher conformity and security values, establishing value-conditioning as the structural substrate of TPS expression. Extending this framework, Feather (1994) positioned TPS as an equity-restoration mechanism, activated episodically by contextual pretexts rather than expressed as continuous overt hostility.

The social-psychological mechanism underpinning peer marginalisation is grounded in Social Comparison Theory (SCT; Festinger, 1954). Since professional competence constitutes the primary domain of comparison in workplace settings, performance disparity generates chronic uniformity pressure; in ability-based comparison, “a state of social quiescence is never reached” (Festinger, 1954, p. 125). When peer comparison appears threatening, hostility and derogation replace direct evaluation, a mechanism documented across contemporary organisational settings (Matthews and Kelemen, 2024). Li, Xu, and Kwan (2021) further establish in their meta-analytic review that upward comparison yields counterproductive behaviour, ostracism, and undermining, whilst multilevel field research documents a direct pathway from high performance, through peer envy, to victimisation (Kim and Glomb, 2014). In professional workplaces, these dynamics are observable as a live constraint through gossip, informal exclusion, and systematic undermining of standout colleagues (Mancl and Penington, 2011). In Australian professional environments, TPS attitudes are empirically associated with negative outcomes for leader effectiveness, subordinate commitment, motivation, and satisfaction, becoming an embedded cultural feature of organisational life rather than an individual-level aberration (Meng, Ashkanasy and Härtel, 2003).

### 2.2 TPS in KM

The Knowledge-Based View (KBV) of the firm positions voluntary knowledge integration as the primary source of sustained competitive advantage (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992), as firms function to synthesise specialist knowledge collectively rather than independently. Grant (1996) identifies mutual recognition of individual knowledge domains as the foundational precondition for knowledge integration; Kogut and Zander (1992) locate competitive advantage in the tacit know-how embedded within social cooperation regularities. Both mechanisms depend on the social conditions that TPS systematically degrades. Recent empirical evidence confirms these KBV propositions remain operative in contemporary knowledge-intensive business services, with KS measurably predicting innovativeness and market efficiency and its suppression translating directly into competitive underperformance (Danko and Crhová, 2024).

Cabrera and Cabrera (2002, p. 704) argue that voluntary KS relies most consequentially on “a social environment which encourages or even enforces” such behaviour; TPS degrades exactly this precondition. Social Exchange Theory (SET; Blau, 1964) frames voluntary KS as a reciprocal exchange governed by trust and unspecified obligation. When TPS manifests as social devaluation of a TP’s contributions, perceived reciprocity breaks down and voluntary knowledge contribution can thus be reactively withdrawn (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021). Spontaneous KS functions as a form of organisational citizenship behaviour and is therefore highly sensitive to perceived fairness; when employees experience perceived inequity, discretionary contributions are withheld (Podsakoff et al., 2000). The degree of withdrawal is, however, not predetermined. Upward social comparison primarily promotes KS through benign envy; withdrawal becomes more probable when hostile peer marginalisation activates a malicious envy pathway, turning contribution recalibration conditional rather than deterministic (Sung et al., 2024). Specialised knowledge integration is laterally embedded and largely opaque to managers owing to their hierarchical position (Grant, 1996); peer accounts hence offer the most viable vantage point from which the informal realities of TPS-driven KM erosion can be observed.

### 2.3 TPS in KBF

KBFs, operating across professional sectors, are structurally distinguished by the centrality of individual expert performance to commercial output. This attribute amplifies TPS risk in a distinctive way. For KBF teams, professional competence constitutes the primary axis of peer comparison; Festinger (1954, p.132) asserts that when an ability is “necessary or important for the life of the group”, the pressure towards uniformity is maximised and the derogation of standout performers carries disproportionate force. Matthews and Kelemen (2024) affirm that informal social comparisons in the workplace produce outcomes as damaging as comparisons based on compensation or capability; knowledge workers closely tie their professional identity to their expertise and therefore these interpersonal comparisons become especially consequential. In KBFs, creative and proactive performance systematically triggers peer ostracism, with team-level envy functioning as the moderating condition that determines whether standout contribution leads to exclusion (Breidenthal et al., 2020).

Team cohesion, as the interpersonal bonds sustaining collective performance, is a well-established antecedent of team outcomes (Grossman et al., 2022). In high-interdependence KBF contexts, cohesion disruption brings forth magnified consequences, since task interdependence elevates the cohesion-performance relationship such that its erosion causes proportionally greater performance degradation (Gully et al., 2012). Mathieu et al. (2015) found that team cohesion and collective performance operate in a self-reinforcing cycle. Within the context of KBFs, this means TPS-driven marginalisation does not impose a mere single-period cost; rather, the resulting drop in cohesion diminishes collective performance, which subsequently intensifies the comparative pressure driving further TPS behaviour. This self-reinforcing nature renders TPS-induced cohesion erosion a compounding governance risk that escalates without managerial intervention. The tacit knowledge most critical to KBFs’ competitive positioning, embedded within informal social routines and collective problem-solving (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992), is precisely the knowledge most vulnerable to this spiral.

### 2.4 TPS in AU KBF

The academic foundations of TPS research are distinctly Australian. Feather’s (1989, 1994) studies drew on South Australian samples, situating TPS within Australia’s historically egalitarian cultural norms, where collective equality standards constrain the social acceptability of standout individual achievement. Subsequent research has confirmed TPS as one of four distinctively Australian cultural dimensions of organisational life, with empirically documented effects on leader effectiveness and subordinate commitment, motivation, and satisfaction in Australian professional contexts (Meng, Ashkanasy and Härtel, 2003). TPS is, in other words, not merely culturally attributed in Australia but organisationally operative within it. For KBFs operating in this cultural context, this embeddedness translates into an elevated baseline exposure to the KM and team cohesion risks identified in §2.2 and §2.3, making the governance challenge examined in this study particularly acute across the Australian professional services sector, which is a context well represented within CYC’s international KBF client base.

### 2.5 Literature Gap & Conceptual Framework

Existing TPS literature is concentrated in psychology[4], with management-oriented application in KBF contexts largely absent. Feather (1989) himself identified the need for “studies of tall poppies in actual situations” and cross-group replication, which is exactly a call this study directly answers. Peer-level competitive dynamics are explicitly regarded as an under-theorised frontier of KS behaviour (Foss, Husted and Michailova, 2010; Wang and Noe, 2010). This study thus precisely occupies the intersection of these gaps.

The provisional conceptual framework (Figure 1) synthesises four thematic domains. Within this structure, SCT’s cessation-of-comparison mechanism creates the social conditions under which SET’s reciprocity logic predicts withdrawal is more probable, with KBV establishing the strategic stakes that render this a governance-critical concern. RQ1 and RQ2 map onto the conditions and peer experience domains respectively, informing both data collection and interpretation. Domains connect through relational associations, not directional causation, consistent with interpretivist epistemology (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2023).

## 3. Research Methodology

### 3.1. Research Philosophy & Approach

This study adopts an **interpretivist** philosophy grounded in constructivist ontology and subjectivist epistemology. TPS dynamics are constituted through social perception, attribution, and contextually contingent group behaviour; they cannot be meaningfully reduced to objective measurement alone. An inductive, qualitative approach is accordingly adopted, permitting investigation of TPS experience without imposing confirmation bias onto an inherently socially constructed and contextually variable phenomenon (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2023).

The chosen research method is an **explanatory multi-case study** (Yin, 2003; Zainal, 2007). Case study suits examining contemporary phenomena within their real-life contexts, and the explanatory variant is applied here because the study seeks to understand the conditions and mechanisms through which TPS operates within KBFs rather than merely to establish that it does (Zainal, 2007). Each sample unit constitutes a single case, with six organisations yielding six cases[5]. Analytical weight derives from “replication logic”, whereby patterns recurring across multiple independent cases carry stronger inferential value than those arising from a single site (Yin, 2003). This multi-case design also reflects the cross-sector character of CYC’s KBF client base. As an MCF specialising in KM, its advisory engagements span KBFs broadly, and the research participant pool is drawn from within this base.

### 3.2. Data Collection

Semi-structured interviews via Teams constitute the primary data collection means. This approach affords sufficient structure for cross-participant comparability whilst preserving the flexibility to probe emergent themes within each case. TPS dynamics are embedded in interpersonal perception and contextually situated professional experience; the semi-structured format supports participants to articulate their observations and professional reflections in their own terms, a design well-established in qualitative knowledge-behaviour research (Thomas, 2025). All interviews would be conducted via the UoL account to satisfy data security requirements.

### 3.3. Sampling

At least six participants will be recruited through purposive sampling, drawn from KBFs broadly as CYC’s primary client base. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon driven by group dynamics that transcend individual firm boundaries; single-entity sampling would risk conflating findings with that firm’s idiosyncratic leadership or culture rather than isolating TPS as the explanatory variable. Cross-firm design is, therefore, methodologically necessary.

Purposive selection criteria require participants to have worked alongside standout performers in a KBF setting and have directly observed shifts in team dynamics. Primary participants are TPPs, non-managerial professionals occupying a lateral vantage point from which TPS dynamics are fully visible. Specialised knowledge flows are laterally embedded and largely opaque to management hierarchies (Grant, 1996); peer accounts thus offer the most viable access to the informal realities of TPS-driven KM erosion. Supplementary interviews with TPs or managers will be attempted prior to analysis. One-participant-per-organisation design would be practised to isolate TPS dynamics from single-firm cultural idiosyncrasies.

Adequacy is determined by data saturation rather than headcount; Guest, Bunce, and Johnson (2006) confirm empirically that core themes can emerge by six homogeneous interviews, whilst adequacy reflects data depth rather than participant count, with saturation interpreted as the point at which additional data cease to meaningfully surface new codes or themes (Braun and Clarke, 2006, p.87; Fusch and Ness, 2015).

### 3.4. Data Analysis

Thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke’s (2006, p.87) six-phase framework is adopted. Coding commences after the third interview and proceeds iteratively until saturation is reached. The analytic sequence follows a within-case then across-case approach (Ayres, Kavanaugh and Knafl, 2003). Each case is first analysed individually in depth before cross-case patterns, configurations, and variations are identified. This sequence preserves within-case richness before cross-case synthesis is attempted, consistent with the “replication logic” (Yin, 2003) of the study. Data are collected across six independent organisational contexts, enabling cross-case comparison and convergent validation of emerging themes.

A dedicated coding node for disconfirming evidence, capturing instances of continued or increased KS despite TPS conditions, will be maintained throughout analysis to provide analytical balance and to guard against asymmetric interpretation.

### 3.5. Trustworthiness

Qualitative rigour would be addressed through Shenton’s (2004) four trustworthiness criteria. Credibility is maintained through systematic coding and member-checking where achievable. Dependability is supported through an audit trail of analytical decisions. Transferability is addressed through cross-firm sampling and rich contextual description; as Shenton (2004) notes, researchers can describe only the sending context, with applicability to other settings assessed by the reader. Confirmability is supported through reflexivity, with the researcher’s sustained professional exposure to high-performer team environments serving as an interpretive lens and acknowledged throughout the analytical process. This proximity to the research phenomenon provides interpretive depth whilst necessitating active discipline against asymmetric interpretation, addressed through the disconfirming evidence coding protocol described above.

### 3.6. Feasibility & Limitations

The researcher’s professional network spans legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, technology, and public sector entities across 10⁺ countries, providing feasible access to a diverse KBF sample. Primary limitation is the size of qualitative sample; analytical generalisation to theory constitutes the appropriate standard for interpretivist inquiry.

### 3.7. Ethics

Standard UoL ethics approval would be sought prior to any data collection. Semi-structured interview questions will operate at the professional observation level, examining team behaviour and collaboration patterns rather than personal emotional disclosure. Individual participant consent forms would be signed in advance, and all data would be stored on UoL OneDrive. Data collection will not commence until written ethical approval has been received.
