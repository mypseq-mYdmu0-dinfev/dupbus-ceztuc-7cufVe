#### Core Argument

KBV thesis: the firm exists as an institution for **integrating specialist knowledge**, not for creating it. Knowledge creation is individual (Simon); the firm's primary role is **knowledge application**. Because productive knowledge is predominantly tacit and specialist, and because markets fail to transfer tacit knowledge efficiently (immobility) or protect explicit knowledge from expropriation, the firm provides coordination conditions markets cannot replicate.

Grant's individual-centric departure from Nonaka/Spender: organisational knowledge is a reification risk — it obscures the mechanisms through which individuals actually create, store, and exchange knowledge. The firm does not possess knowledge; it possesses coordination infrastructure enabling individual specialists to integrate theirs. This is not merely definitional: it has direct implications for management, organisational design, decision rights, and firm boundaries.

For KBFs: this framing makes Grant the foundational warrant for why KM in KBFs is a strategic (not operational) concern. Individual expertise IS the firm's primary resource. Anything disrupting voluntary knowledge integration — including TPS-driven marginalisation — directly degrades organisational capability at its source.

---

#### Knowledge Typology

| Type | Revealed by | Transferability | Appropriability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Explicit | Communication | Easy; near-zero marginal cost; public good risk | Problematic — buyer acquires without seller losing; resellable |
| Tacit | Application only | Slow, costly, uncertain (Kogut & Zander 1992) | Cannot be directly appropriated; exercisable only by possessor |

Grant identifies tacit knowledge as the strategically critical type: inimitable, non-appropriable by markets, exercisable only by the individual who holds it. This makes the voluntary cooperation of knowledge-holding individuals non-negotiable for KBF performance. Coercion cannot extract tacit knowledge; it must be voluntarily applied.

**Critical implication for TPS:** A TP who withdraws from collective knowledge exchange does not lose their knowledge — they withhold its application. Because tacit knowledge is revealed only through application and cannot be mandated, marginalisation creates conditions under which voluntary application is rationally (or instinctively) restricted. The firm has no structural mechanism to force contribution; it must maintain the social conditions that make voluntary integration worthwhile.

---

#### Four Coordination Mechanisms

Grant synthesises the mechanisms through which specialists integrate knowledge whilst minimising costly cross-learning:

| Mechanism | Description | TPS disruption risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Rules and directives** | Converts tacit specialist knowledge into explicit, comprehensible procedures; low-cost cross-specialist communication | Low direct disruption — formal rules persist; but if TP expertise is devalued, their procedural inputs are ignored or bypassed |
| **Sequencing** | Time-patterned independent specialist inputs; each contributes separately; minimal real-time coordination | Moderate — TPS may cause sequencing gaps if TP's turn in knowledge-flow is disrupted or their handoff is deprioritised |
| **Routines** | Complex interaction patterns triggered by initiating signals; "functioning as recognisable unit in a relatively automatic fashion" (Winter 1986, p.165 via Grant); operates without verbal communication | **High** — routines are informal and socially embedded; TPS-driven exclusion breaks the triggering signals and interaction patterns; routine knowledge exchange becomes unreliable |
| **Group problem-solving** | High-interaction, reserved for complex/uncertain tasks; most costly coordination mode | **High** — TPS directly corrupts group coordination; marginalisation produces non-participation, hostile attribution, withheld contributions from TP; cross-specialist integration fails at its highest-stakes mode |

Grant notes: organisations should maximise rules/routines and reserve group problem-solving for unusual tasks (efficiency principle). TPS most severely degrades routines and group coordination — the mechanisms that handle tacit knowledge exchange — while leaving formal rule-following relatively intact. This explains why TPS is invisible to management: formal outputs may persist while tacit exchange erodes beneath.

---

#### Common Knowledge Framework

Common knowledge = elements of knowledge shared across all organisational members; the social infrastructure enabling integration without full knowledge transfer. Grant identifies five types:

| Component | Role in integration | TPS disruption mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Common language** | Fundamental to rules/directives and group problem-solving; enables cross-specialist communication | TPS-driven hostile communication patterns corrupt shared vocabulary; TP's contributions deliberately misread or reframed by peers |
| **Symbolic communication** (literacy, numeracy, software familiarity) | Raises sophistication of communication-based integration | Marginalisation excludes TP from shared symbolic contexts (excluded from meetings, briefings, shared tools) |
| **Commonality of specialised knowledge** | Enables integration depth — paradox: too much overlap = no integration benefit; too little = integration impossible | TPS produces artificial divergence; peers deliberately avoid acquiring overlap with TP's domain as social distancing |
| **Shared meaning** | Tacit knowledge communicated via shared cognitive schema, metaphor, analogy; "indwelling" (Polanyi 1966) | **Most disrupted by TPS**: shared meaning requires relational trust and mutual orientation; TPS erodes both; TP's tacit knowledge becomes inaccessible even when present |
| **Recognition of individual knowledge domains** | "Effective knowledge integration also requires that each individual is aware of everyone else's knowledge repertoire"; mutual recognition enables coordination in novel situations | **Most direct TPS mechanism**: ostracism = deliberate non-recognition or devaluation of TP's knowledge domain; integration severs at source — peers stop knowing what TP knows, stop routing tasks to them, stop leveraging their expertise |

The recognition of individual knowledge domains is the most analytically precise connection between TPS and KM failure. TPS-driven marginalisation is definitionally a refusal to recognise the TP's knowledge domain as legitimate or valuable. Grant establishes that this recognition is a structural prerequisite for integration — its withdrawal is not merely social rudeness but a functional degradation of the firm's integration capacity.

---

#### Organisational Capability & Strategic Implications

Organisational capability = outcome of knowledge integration. "The extent to which a capability is 'distinctive' depends upon" the knowledge integrated within it — breadth and inimitability of integration. Higher common knowledge → more efficient integration → stronger capability. Competitive advantage longevity depends on the inimitability of broad-scope integration: the harder to replicate, the more valuable.

For KBFs specifically: the knowledge being integrated is expert tacit knowledge held by individual specialists. This cannot be stockpiled, codified, or easily replaced. TPS attacks capability at its foundation: it degrades exactly the integration conditions (common knowledge, mutual recognition, shared meaning, informal routines) that make KBF-level capability possible. The loss is not visible in any single knowledge transaction — it compounds as integration infrastructure erodes.

**Decision rights co-location principle:** Grant argues that decision rights should be co-located with knowledge because tacit knowledge cannot be transferred upward. Employees — knowledge owners — effectively co-own the firm's primary resource. TPS, from this angle, is a governance pathology: peers collectively withdraw recognition of a knowledge-holder's asset, undermining the firm's ability to deploy what it theoretically possesses. Management, positioned hierarchically above the TPS dynamic, cannot access the relevant knowledge to intervene — hierarchy fails precisely where Grant predicts it fails.

#### Flags

**(a) TPS→KM withdrawal link:** Grant provides the theoretical architecture explaining *why* TPS-driven peer withdrawal is a strategic KM failure, not an interpersonal inconvenience. Key mechanisms:
- Tacit knowledge is "appropriated only through its application" — a TP who stops voluntarily applying their knowledge to collective tasks withholds the firm's primary resource, and no structural mechanism can compel retrieval
- The five components of common knowledge are all socially produced and socially vulnerable: TPS corrupts shared meaning, mutual recognition of knowledge domains, and communicative trust simultaneously
- Routines as the primary informal integration mechanism are socially embedded — TPS-driven exclusion disrupts triggering signals, making routine knowledge exchange structurally unreliable
- Grant's hierarchy critique (hierarchy fails when tacit knowledge is distributed) applies directly: management cannot diagnose TPS-driven KM erosion from above because the relevant knowledge — about what is not being shared and why — is itself tacit and withheld
- For the disconfirming evidence node: Grant does not provide conditions under which TPS-equivalent dynamics would NOT produce KM withdrawal; pair with Sung et al. (2024) [RefRepo] for the benign/malicious envy moderator

**(b) Sampling and saturation:** N/A — theoretical/conceptual paper; no empirical design.

**(c) Conceptual framework:** Grant = primary KBV anchor. The framework should show: KBF structural dependence on voluntary knowledge integration (Grant) → integration requires specific social conditions (common knowledge, mutual recognition, routines) → TPS disrupts these conditions (study's empirical claim) → KM governance failure results (Challenge A). Grant also provides the vocabulary for the framework: "knowledge integration," "knowledge application," "common knowledge," "mutual recognition of knowledge domains" — these should appear in the framework as named constructs, not paraphrases. Pair with Nonaka (1994) [entry 02] for the creation/SECI dimension; Grant covers the integration/application side.

#### Core Argument

Humans possess a drive to accurately evaluate their own opinions and abilities (Hypothesis I). When objective, non-social referents are unavailable — which Festinger treats as the default — this evaluation occurs by comparison with similar others (Hypothesis II). Comparison is preferentially made with those close to oneself (Hypothesis III), and discrepancy within a comparison group generates pressure toward uniformity manifesting in three behavioural channels: change own position, change others' positions, or redefine the comparison group to exclude divergent members (Derivation D, D₁, D₂, D₃).

For **abilities** specifically (as distinct from opinions), two asymmetries apply: (1) a unidirectional drive upward — higher performance is intrinsically more desirable, so comparison never fully stabilises (Hypothesis IV); and (2) non-social restraints make ability change slow and difficult, so the gap between a high performer and peers cannot easily be closed by individual effort (Hypothesis V). The combination means that in ability-based comparison, **a state of social quiescence is never reached** (p. 125) — the comparison pressure is chronic, not episodic.

When a member's ability is too divergent upward to serve as a usable comparison referent, peers cease comparing — and this cessation is accompanied by hostility or derogation **to the extent that continued comparison would imply unpleasant consequences** (Hypothesis VI). In KBF contexts, professional competence is both the comparison currency and the core group activity; continued comparison with a TP is maximally threatening to peers' self-evaluations. Hypothesis VI therefore predicts TPS-equivalent derogation as the structurally expected outcome, not an aberration.

Two amplifier factors increase all comparison pressures: group attractiveness (Corollary VII A — the more members value belonging to the team, the stronger the pressure) and issue relevance to the group (Corollary VII B — "realm of relevance"; where the ability in question is central to the group's life, pressure is maximised). KBFs score high on both: professional teams involve sustained membership (high attractiveness) and expert performance is the group's raison d'être (maximum realm of relevance). SCT therefore predicts that TPS pressure is structurally amplified in KBF settings specifically.

#### Key Constructs

- **Drive to evaluate** — innate human motivation; not culturally contingent in Festinger's framing (though see footnote 4: Hopi exception for unidirectional drive)
- **Social comparison** — default mechanism when objective referents absent; ubiquitous in professional settings where "good performance" is socially defined
- **Similarity attraction** — comparison targets are chosen for similarity, not divergence; extreme divergence renders comparison impossible
- **Unidirectional drive upward (abilities)** — higher performance always preferable; peers perpetually orient toward being slightly better than the TP; because this is chronically unachievable, quiescence is never reached
- **Pressure toward uniformity** — operates via D₁/D₂/D₃ simultaneously; relative strength determined by proximity to group mode (Hypothesis IX)
- **Realm of relevance** (Corollary VII B) — group-specific domain in which comparison is active; professional expertise = maximum realm of relevance in KBFs
- **Status stratification** — when D₃ (cessation) occurs for abilities, result is stable superior/inferior categorisation rather than rejection per se (p. 129); for our study: TP placed in "superior" incomparable status, which licenses peer disengagement from normal reciprocity norms
- **Forced comparability** — when a member cannot leave a group and continues to diverge upward on an important ability, the group exerts pressure via punishment or demands for conformity; relevant to TPs who are embedded in long-term client or project teams
- **Hostility/derogation** (Hypothesis VI) — accompanies cessation of comparison when continued comparison implies unpleasant self-evaluation; the psychological mechanism that converts comparison-frustration into interpersonal marginalisation

#### Core Argument

KS among organisational employees is structurally analogous to a **public-good dilemma**: knowledge is non-rivalrous (sharing does not deplete the contributor's stock), accessible to all regardless of contribution, and individually costly to produce/distribute. This structure creates a dominant defection strategy — individual rationality dictates withholding, because the free-rider outcome (enjoy the shared pool without contributing) is always individually superior. Left unmanaged, groups converge on a **deficient equilibrium**: no one cooperates, and all suffer collectively sub-optimal outcomes.

The paper identifies three categories of organisational intervention to resolve this dilemma:
1. **Restructure the pay-off function** — reduce individual cost of sharing or increase perceived benefit
2. **Increase perceived efficacy** — make contributors believe their input has real impact (information self-efficacy) and will reach relevant others (connective efficacy)
3. **Promote group identity and personal responsibility** — leverage social belonging, identifiability, and reputational accountability to activate cooperative norms

Paper is explicitly theory-building, not empirical; draws on `~`50 years of social dilemma research from economics, sociology, and social psychology to generate falsifiable hypotheses for KM research.

---

#### Key Constructs

- **Public-good dilemma** — collective resource from which all benefit regardless of individual contribution; non-diminishing with use; generates free-rider incentive (pp. 693–694)
- **Deficient equilibrium** — stable non-cooperation outcome: once reached, no individual incentive exists to break it; group suffers collectively (p. 693)
- **Dominant strategy** — defecting (withholding) is always individually rational in public-good situations, regardless of others' behaviour (p. 693)
- **Social fence** — the structural barrier keeping groups trapped in non-cooperation (Messick & Brewer 1983 via p. 693)
- **Information self-efficacy** — belief that one's knowledge would be helpful to co-workers if shared (p. 698)
- **Connective efficacy** — belief that contributed information will actually reach relevant others (p. 698); both types decline with group size (p. 699)
- **Pay-off restructuring** — selective incentives (individual reward contingent on behaviour) vs. public-good transformation (increasing perceived value of collective gain); latter preferred as it encourages quality over quantity (pp. 695–697)
- **Cooperation-contingent transformation** — selective incentive changes the dominant strategy to cooperating (p. 696)
- **Group identity / personal responsibility** — social belonging increases restraint and contribution; Axelrod's conditions: frequent/durable interactions, identifiability, information availability about actions (pp. 701–702)
- **Critical mass** — minimum contributor threshold below which individual cost outweighs collective value (p. 699)
- **Psychological contract shift** — from "commit to outstanding individual performance" to "commit to building organisational capabilities" (p. 697)

The paper cites consulting firms explicitly:
- p. 691: Hansen et al. (1999) — large consulting firms (Accenture/E&Y) use codification/groupware; strategy consultants (McKinsey/Bain) use professional networks for tacit KS — two distinct KM strategies matching business model
- p. 698–699: consulting firm budget example — if a project manager can budget time for compiling and sharing lessons learned, opportunity cost of KS is reduced — concrete KBF-level intervention

#### The TPS–KM Withdrawal Link (Flag a)

Two passages directly map the paper's public-good dilemma onto TPS dynamics in KBFs:

**p. 694** — "in organisations with a competitive internal work environment, the contributor of an idea may be assuming a great deal of personal vulnerability by revealing the secrets of his or her own competitive edge. Depending on the relative weight of these costs and benefits, some individuals may feel that they are better off hoarding, rather than sharing, what they know."

> TPS relevance: TPS-driven marginalisation is precisely this competitive-internal-environment condition, but peer-generated rather than structurally imposed. The TP's withdrawal is not irrational — it is the individually rational dominant-strategy response to an environment where sharing reveals competitive vulnerability without assured reciprocity.

**p. 697** — "in a scenario where employees' incentives are aligned with extraordinary performance, sharing one's skills with others may harm one's capacity to outperform the rest."

> TPS relevance: the most citable passage for the TPS→KM withdrawal argument. TPs face exactly this incentive misalignment: their extraordinary performance is already a source of peer hostility; contributing knowledge amplifies both the vulnerability and the relative performance signal that provoked marginalisation in the first place.

**p. 697** — psychological contract shift: organisations must redefine performance to include "contributions towards building the organisation's strategic capabilities" and align HR policies (selection, appraisal, career management, compensation) accordingly.

> Managerial implication: if TPS persists in an environment where individual performance is the only rewarded currency, the public-good dilemma worsens; Cabrera & Cabrera's prescription maps directly onto Challenge A/C recommendations.

**p. 704** — "the other, even more important, requisite is that of a social environment which encourages or even enforces knowledge sharing."

> Inversion: TPS degrades exactly this social environment precondition; the paper establishes that without it, even sophisticated IT/structural interventions fail.

**Reciprocity mechanism (pp. 692, 695)** — Wasko & Faraj (2000) via Cabrera: participants share because "they considered it due reciprocity for the help they themselves had obtained from that community." TPS disrupts perceived reciprocity at source: peer exclusion and devaluation signal that the community will not return equivalent value, triggering rational KS withdrawal under SET logic.

**Partial contradiction / boundary condition (p. 695)** — Connolly et al. (1992): one-third of participants refused to contribute to a discretionary database even when cost was minimal. This supports TPS→withdrawal risk. But also: some contribute because of personal fulfilment, reputation gain, and pro-social motivation — consistent with boundary condition framing (some TPs continue sharing despite marginalisation, driven by intrinsic motivation; cf. Bari et al. 2025 in RefRepo).

Three-category framework generates concrete recommendations directly applicable to Challenge A/C:

**Category 1 — Pay-off restructuring (pp. 695–698)**
| Intervention | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| User-friendly KS technology | Reduce contribution cost |
| Selective individual incentives | Make cooperating dominant strategy |
| Gain-sharing programmes | Increase perceived collective gain value |
| HR policy alignment (appraisal, career, compensation) | Redefine performance to include KS contribution |

**Category 2 — Increasing efficacy (pp. 698–700)**
| Intervention | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Contributor feedback loops | Increase information + connective efficacy |
| Critical mass assurance | Ensure collective value exceeds individual cost |
| Advanced search/indexing technology | Offset redundancy as group scales |
| Training on KS tools and contribution norms | Increase both efficacy types |

**Category 3 — Group identity and personal responsibility (pp. 701–703)**
| Intervention | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Communication/group discussion | Identity salience; commitment to cooperate |
| Communities of practice | Increase interaction frequency + identifiability |
| Publicising contribution data | Reputational accountability; social control |

Axelrod's (1984) three conditions for sustained cooperation (via p. 702): frequent/durable interactions; identifiable participants; information available about each individual's actions. TPS undermines all three by eroding group belonging and reducing willingness to interact.

#### Core Argument

Blau's central thesis: social life is constituted by exchange processes that simultaneously produce integrative bonds and power differentials. Individuals seek social rewards (approval, respect, compliance, services) by making themselves attractive and valuable to others. When exchange is reciprocal and balanced, bonds of fellowship and trust result. When one party supplies benefits the other cannot reciprocate, power asymmetry develops. The same dynamics that integrate groups also stratify them. This reciprocity-vs-imbalance dialectic is not a flaw but the generative engine of social structure: balanced states at one level depend on imbalances at others.

For this project, Blau supplies three analytically separable contributions:
1. **Ch4** — the SET mechanism: what social exchange is, why unspecified obligations require trust, and what failure to reciprocate costs
2. **Ch1** — the structural framing: attraction → exchange → power; the dialectic between reciprocity and imbalance as constitutive of social structure
3. **Ch2** — the TPS-proximate paradox: why outstanding qualities simultaneously attract and repel, generating structural resentment and defensive exclusion

---

#### Chapter 1 — The Structure of Social Associations (pp. 12–32)

**Foundational claim:** Social associations evolve from processes of exchange driven by individuals seeking social rewards. Exchange → status differentiation → legitimation and/or opposition. Forces sustaining equilibrium on one level create disequilibrium on others.

**Social exchange defined (pp. 14–21):** Social attraction produces a desire to prove oneself attractive to others. Mutual attraction → exchange transactions. Person who furnishes needed assistance obligates others to reciprocate (p. 16). "Favours make us grateful, and our expressions of gratitude are social rewards" (p. 16). Reciprocation expected; failure = social sanction of ingratitude (p. 16).

**Apparent altruism / underlying egoism (p. 17):** "the tendency to help others is frequently motivated by the expectation that doing so will bring social rewards." Social approval requires appearing genuine — cannot be coerced (p. 17). This is critical for understanding voluntary KM sharing: sharing appears altruistic but is instrumentally motivated by expected social returns.

**Power from unilateral services (p. 22):** When a person needs services from another but has nothing to offer in return, four options: force, alternative source, do without, or subordinate oneself. The fourth — compliance — is the generic mechanism by which power asymmetry develops. "A person who commands services others need, and who is independent of any at their command, attains power over others by making the satisfaction of their need contingent on their compliance" (p. 22).

**Reciprocity and imbalance (pp. 25–29):** Individuals strive to maintain exchange balance but aspire to accumulate credit (imbalance in their favour). "There is a strain toward imbalance as well as toward reciprocity in social associations" (p. 26). A key impetus for discharging obligations is avoiding subordination to the supplier (p. 29). Balanced exchange rests on underlying imbalances of dependence and power.

**Macro extension (pp. 24–32):** Group-level analysis: collective approval legitimates power; collective disapproval generates opposition. Complex macrostructures have emergent properties not reducible to microstructural dynamics. Common values mediate exchange across collectivities without direct contact.

---

#### Chapter 2 — Social Integration (pp. 33–59)

**Foundational claim:** Group formation involves a paradox — the impressive qualities required to prove oneself attractive also constitute a status threat that triggers defensive exclusion. This produces the TPS-equivalent structural dynamic in Blau's framework.

**Attraction and group integration (pp. 33–34):** "A person's attraction to a group stimulates his desire to become a member, but only if he proves himself attractive to its other members can he realise this desire and gain social acceptance" (p. 33). Integrative bonds rest on forces of social attraction; without them, a work group is merely "an assembly of individuals under a foreman" (p. 34).

**Competitive differentiation (p. 37–38):** "The desire of many individuals to impress each other with their outstanding qualities engenders competitive processes, and social differentiation develops in the course of the ensuing competition" (p. 37). Impressive qualities require differentiation — sharing the same values as everyone else does not make one differentially attractive (p. 39).

**The Paradox of Integration (pp. 43–44) — TPS-proximate core:**
"In a group situation, impressive qualities make a person attractive in one sense and unattractive in another, because they raise fears of rejection and pose a status threat for the rest of the group" (p. 43).
"The rewards expected from associating with an impressive person that make him attractive also would make us dependent on him and subject to his control. For it is in the nature of rewarding social experiences to produce simultaneously social attraction and social dependence. Paradoxically, the very attributes that make a person an attractive associate for others also raise fears of dependence that make them reluctant to acknowledge their attraction" (p. 44).

This is the structural-exchange grounding for TPS: peer marginalisation of high performers is not irrational deviance but a rational defensive response to anticipated dependence and status displacement. It operates even among colleagues who genuinely find the TP valuable.

**Expert dependence mechanism (pp. 44–45, 600–601):** If colleagues repeatedly ask an expert's advice, they become obligated to comply with his requests. "The obligation to defer to the wishes of an expert on whose guidance others have come to depend is what detracts from his attractiveness as a sociable companion for them. Deference to another impedes relaxed sociability with him" (p. 44–45). Anticipation of future dependence inhibits easygoing sociability even before actual obligation is incurred (p. 44–45).

**Competition as mixed game (pp. 44–46):** Competition for social recognition is a series of interlocking mixed games. Each member has an interest in withholding evidence of being impressed by others (preserves own standing). Pay-off matrix: mutual withholding → peer relation; one-sided expressing → subordination of the expresser. Each prefers superordinate status; minimum acceptable is peer status; worst outcome is no relation.

**Status differentiation → resentment (pp. 47–57):** High performers make undeniable contributions that obligate others to accord them superior status — but doing so generates resentment. "Although contributions of undeniable significance constrain others to accord superior status to a person, doing so leaves a residue of resentment" (p. 57). Those displaced from superior positions are most resentful.

**Self-depreciation as integration strategy (pp. 47–50):** Group members who cannot command superior status through contribution may withdraw from the competition via self-depreciation — publicly surrendering claim to superiority in exchange for peer acceptance. This is how integrative bonds form: "As some group members find it necessary to withdraw from the competition for superior status, they establish integrative bonds which become the foundation of group solidarity" (p. 50).

**Blau's explicit paradox statement (p. 57):** "For social acceptance requires some outstanding qualities that make an individual a differentially attractive associate, but outstanding qualities raise fears of dependence that inhibit acceptance as a sociable companion." This is the cleanest single-sentence expression of the TPS mechanism in exchange-theoretic terms; cite this passage as the structural antecedent to Feather's cultural-psychological account.

**Group cohesion paradox (p. 57–58):** "Acceptance as a sociable companion requires that an individual have attractive qualities and that he not pose a status threat, but outstanding qualities that make him especially attractive also pose a status threat." Solidarity depends on contributions AND integrative bonds, but the status differentiation required to incentivise contributions is detrimental to those bonds.

#### Chapter 4 — Social Exchange (pp. 88–114)

**SET definition (p. 92):** "Social exchange...refers to voluntary actions of individuals that are motivated by the returns they are expected to bring and typically do in fact bring from others." Excludes: physical coercion; conformity with purely internalised norms. Includes: all voluntary social conduct oriented toward expected social returns.

**The core distinction from economic exchange (p. 93):** "Social exchange differs in important ways from strictly economic exchange. The basic and most crucial distinction is that social exchange entails unspecified obligations." Economic exchange involves formal contract, stipulated quantities, exact terms. Social exchange involves "the principle that one person does another a favour, and while there is a general expectation of some future return, its exact nature is definitely not stipulated in advance" (p. 93). Equivalence cannot be bargained or enforced; left entirely to the giver's discretion.

**Unspecified obligations → trust requirement (p. 94):** "Since there is no way to assure an appropriate return for a favour, social exchange requires trusting others to discharge their obligations." Trust builds gradually via expanding transaction sequences — minor exchanges first, risk-tested, then progressively expanded (p. 94). "Processes of social exchange, which may originate in pure self-interest, generate trust in social relations through their recurrent and gradually expanding character" (p. 94).

**Social vs economic obligation (pp. 94–95):** "Only social exchange tends to engender feelings of personal obligation, gratitude, and trust; purely economic exchange as such does not." A bank mortgage generates technical debt, not personal obligation. A favour that goes beyond contractual terms creates genuine personal indebtedness. This distinction is critical for our KM argument: knowledge sharing in KBFs is social exchange (unspecified, trust-dependent, personally obligating) not economic transaction.

**Unspecified obligations are substantively real (p. 95):** "It is essential to realise that this is a substantive fact, not simply a methodological problem. It is not just the social scientist who cannot exactly measure how much approval a given helpful action is worth; the actors themselves cannot precisely specify the worth of approval or of help." This grounds why KM reciprocity violations are felt rather than calculated — the breach of unspecified obligation is experienced as relational, not transactional.

**Social benefits cannot be bartered (p. 99):** Approval, respect, personal affection — these are rewards that "must not be explicitly bartered in exchange lest their value as genuine feelings or judgements be compromised." If a TP suspects that colleagues' apparent appreciation is simulated (e.g. instrumentally motivated), the approval loses its value. TPS erodes the authenticity of positive evaluations, destroying their function as social exchange rewards.

**Perpetuation of obligations (p. 99):** Trust is strengthened by remaining obligated; premature reciprocation is socially condemned ("excessive eagerness to discharge an obligation is a form of ingratitude"). Ongoing indebtedness maintains social bonds. TPS disrupts this: if TPs perceive that their contributions generate no reciprocal social return (only resentment and marginalisation), the obligation cycle breaks.

**Failure to reciprocate — costs (p. 108):** "Failure to reciprocate engenders loss of credit and loss of trust, and it ultimately brings about exclusion from further exchanges and a general decline in social status, particularly as a person's reputation as one who does not honour his obligations spreads in the community." For TPs: their peers fail to reciprocate (marginalise instead of honour contributions) → TP perceives reciprocity as broken → rational recalibration of KM contribution.

**Overwhelming benefactions → superiority (pp. 107–111):** Distributing gifts and services that others cannot match generates power and status. "A person who gives others valuable gifts or renders them important services makes a claim for superior status by obligating them to himself" (p. 108). If they cannot adequately reciprocate, they validate his superiority. TPs' ongoing knowledge contributions — which others cannot match — may inadvertently function as overwhelming benefactions, generating resentment rather than gratitude when others cannot reciprocate equivalently.

**Social context modifies exchange (pp. 104–106):** Exchanges in a pair are influenced by role-set, prevailing group exchange rates, potential coalitions among weaker parties, and the presence of background exchanges (e.g. worker helps newcomer to impress supervisor, not primarily to earn gratitude). Knowledge sharing in KBFs is never a simple dyadic exchange — it occurs within team and organisational contexts that modify the reciprocity structure.

**(a) TPS→KM withdrawal link:**
