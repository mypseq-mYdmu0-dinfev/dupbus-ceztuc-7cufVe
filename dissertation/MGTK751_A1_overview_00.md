This research investigates Tall Poppy Syndrome (TPS) as a management challenge in knowledge-based firms (KBFs), including legal, accounting, consulting, IT, and R&D organisations. TPS, the social phenomenon whereby high-performing individuals face peer marginalisation in response to their standout performance, has been extensively studied in cultural and social psychology but remains a largely underexplored management problem. This gap is particularly prominent in KBFs, where individual intellectual excellence constitutes the primary source of competitive advantage: when a marginalised high performer (Tall Poppy/TP) reactively withdraws from collective knowledge exchange, the organisation faces both team cohesion erosion and a governance-level knowledge management (KM) risk.

Four research objectives guide the project: (RO1) critically reviewing existing literature on TPS, team dynamics, and KM in KBFs; (RO2) exploring how Tall Poppies' peers (TPPs) perceive and experience TPS dynamics; (RO3) evaluating the organisational and interpersonal conditions that shape TPS; and (RO4) developing management recommendations for practitioners. These objectives give rise to two research questions: (RQ1) "What conditions within KBFs exacerbate or alleviate TPS?" and (RQ2) "How do team members in KBFs experience TPS-related team dynamics?"

The theoretical framework draws on four bodies of literature: (1) TPS foundational literature establishes the phenomenon and its cultural psychology origins. (2) Knowledge-Based View theory grounds the strategic stakes of KM in KBFs. (3) Social Comparison Theory and associated organisational behaviour research account for peer-level reactions to high performance, including envy-driven exclusion and cohesion erosion. (4) Social Exchange Theory frames knowledge withdrawal as a rational response to disrupted reciprocity, building on KM sharing literature. The intersection of these frameworks positions TPS not as a personal or cultural curiosity but as a concrete organisational governance problem, where management cannot observe directly from above, rendering peer-level research both methodologically justified and practically necessary.

The study adopts an interpretivist philosophy with a constructivist ontology. Qualitative, semi-structured interviews will be conducted with at least 6 TPPs across multiple KBF organisations. Participants will be recruited through purposive sampling using a professional screening criterion, targeting individuals who have worked alongside a standout performer (TP) and observed friction or dynamic shifts in that context. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon and confining inquiry to a single firm risks conflating findings with that firm's idiosyncratic culture. Thematic analysis will follow Braun and Clarke's six-phase framework, with data saturation guiding sampling closure. Trustworthiness will be addressed using Lincoln and Guba's criteria: credibility, dependability, transferability, and confirmability.

This research offers two contributions: Academically, it extends TPS scholarship beyond cultural psychology into management and KM domains within KBFs, an empirical gap currently unaddressed in the literature. Practically, it equips managers with workforce-derived insight into a team-level phenomenon that operates largely below their line of sight, with direct implications for KM strategy, talent governance, and organisational culture design.

Ethics approval will be sought from the University of Liverpool prior to any data collection. Interview questions are framed at the professional observation level, requiring no personal emotional disclosure.
