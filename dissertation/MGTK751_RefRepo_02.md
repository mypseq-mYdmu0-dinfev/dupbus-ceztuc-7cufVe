### 02 — Kim, E. and Glomb, T.M. (2014) 'Victimization of high performers: The roles of envy and work group identification', *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 99(4), pp. 619–634. doi: 10.1037/a0035789.

**Full text confirmed**

### 04 — Kogut, B. and Zander, U. (1992) 'Knowledge of the firm, combinative capabilities, and the replication of technology', *Organization Science*, 3(3), pp. 383–397. doi: 10.1287/orsc.3.3.383.

- Pairs with Grant (1996) [entry 08]: Grant = why knowledge matters strategically and how it is integrated; K&Z = the social structure through which knowledge is held and transferred; together form the full KBV scaffold

### 07 — Feather, N.T. (1989) 'Attitudes towards the high achiever: The fall of the Tall Poppy', *Australian Journal of Psychology*, 41(3), pp. 239–267. doi: 10.1080/00049538908260088.

**Full text confirmed** ⚠️ *Page numbers in current synthesis unreliable — use first-10-words anchors when locating specific passages for A3 drafting*

- Foundational TPS empirical paper; three studies confirming satisfaction at TP's fall; Tall Poppy Scale (Favour Fall α=.85; Favour Reward α=.80; two partially independent attitudinal dimensions); culturally rooted tension between collectivist equality norms and achievement values as structural mechanism

- Feather himself calls for "studies of tall poppies in actual situations" and cross-cultural/cross-group replication — our study directly answers both calls; citable contribution framing from the founding author
- Self-esteem moderator: lower SE → stronger Favour Fall (r=–.31, p<.001); South Australian samples; no workplace/org context — all KBF extensions are our own theoretical inference
- A1 §2.1/§2.5; A3 Ch2 (TPS foundations, ambivalence model, pretext mechanism, Tall Poppy Scale); A3 Ch5 (episodic dynamic, boundary conditions); A3 Ch6 (contribution framing against Feather's own 1989 research calls)

### 08 — Grant, R.M. (1996) 'Toward a knowledge-based theory of the firm', *Strategic Management Journal*, 17, pp. 109–122. doi: 10.1002/smj.4250171110.

**Full text confirmed** ⚠️ *Page numbers in current synthesis unreliable — use first-10-words anchors when locating specific passages for A3 drafting*

- KBV thesis: the firm exists to integrate specialist knowledge, not create it; "KBV is not, as yet, a theory" — cite as framework/perspective, not theory; tacit knowledge appropriable only through its application — a TP who stops volunteering knowledge withholds the firm's primary resource with no structural mechanism to compel retrieval
- Mutual recognition of individual knowledge domains = the common knowledge component most directly destroyed by TPS-driven peer marginalisation; ostracism = deliberate non-recognition of TP's knowledge domain, severing integration at source
- Four coordination mechanisms (rules/directives, sequencing, routines, group problem-solving): TPS most severely degrades routines and group problem-solving — the informal mechanisms handling tacit exchange — while leaving formal outputs superficially intact; explains why TPS is invisible to management hierarchy
- Decision rights co-location principle: management cannot diagnose TPS-driven KM erosion from above because the relevant knowledge is itself tacit and laterally embedded — directly justifies TPP peer-primary design
- Pairs with Nonaka (1994) [RefRepo]: Grant = knowledge integration/application; Nonaka = knowledge creation/SECI; together = full KM theoretical scaffold for A3 Ch2
- A1 §2.2/§2.4; A3 Ch2 (KBV grounding, coordination mechanisms, common knowledge framework); A3 Ch5 (mutual recognition withdrawal as TPS mechanism); A3 Ch6 (knowledge governance recommendations)

### 09 — Festinger, L. (1954) 'A theory of social comparison processes', *Human Relations*, 7(2), pp. 117–140. doi: 10.1177/001872675400700202.

**Full text confirmed**

- Foundational SCT paper; for ability comparison (unlike opinion), there is a unidirectional drive upward — "a state of social quiescence is never reached" (p. 125) — TPS pressure in professional settings is structural and chronic, not situationally episodic
- Hypothesis VI: cessation of comparison with a divergent peer is accompanied by hostility/derogation "to the extent that continued comparison...implies unpleasant consequences" (pp. 128–129) — core TPS derogation mechanism; structural prediction, not aberrant behaviour
- Corollary VII B (realm of relevance): where the ability is "necessary or important for the life of the group," comparison pressure is maximised — professional expertise in KBFs = maximum realm of relevance; KBFs are structurally highest-risk TPS environments per SCT
- Boundary condition (p. 124): a TP with no comparable peers may either deteriorate slightly or choose to improve others' performance — SCT-grounded mechanism for continued/increased KS despite TPS conditions; feeds disconfirming evidence node
- A1 §2.3; A3 Ch2 (full SCT theoretical scaffold; Hypothesis VI derogation; Corollary VII B for KBF-specific amplification); A3 Ch5 (episodic vs. chronic distinction; boundary condition); A3 Ch6 (interrupting uniformity-pressure mechanism via Derivation D₂)

### 10 — Cabrera, A. and Cabrera, E.F. (2002) 'Knowledge-sharing dilemmas', *Organization Studies*, 23(5), pp. 687–710. doi: 10.1177/0170840602235001.

**Full text confirmed**

- Theory-building paper; KS is structurally analogous to a public-good dilemma — non-rivalrous, free-rider-incentivised, prone to deficient equilibrium (dominant defection strategy; no individual incentive to break); SET reciprocity as the primary mechanism governing voluntary contribution
- Two most citable passages for TPS→KM withdrawal argument: contributor "may be assuming a great deal of personal vulnerability by revealing the secrets of his or her own competitive edge" (p. 694); "sharing one's skills with others may harm one's capacity to outperform the rest" (p. 697) — individually rational KS withdrawal under competitive peer conditions
- Social environment as the critical non-technological KS precondition: "the other, even more important, requisite is that of a social environment which encourages or even enforces knowledge sharing" (p. 704) — TPS destroys exactly this precondition; consulting firms cited explicitly (p. 691) for KBF context specificity
- Three-category intervention framework (pay-off restructuring; increasing efficacy; group identity and personal responsibility) → direct scaffold for A3 Ch6 SMART recommendations; authors flag KS behaviour as under-researched (p. 705) — citable gap acknowledgement
- A1 §2.4/§2.5; A3 Ch2 (SET/public-good mechanism; KS social environment precondition); A3 Ch5 (deficient equilibrium spiral, pairs with Mathieu et al. 2015 cohesion); A3 Ch6 (three-category recommendations)

### 13 — Blau, P.M. (1964) *Exchange and Power in Social Life*. New York: Wiley. doi: 10.4324/9780203792643.

**Chapters 1, 2, 4 confirmed** *(Ch3, Ch5–12 unread — re-upload targeted chapters if social approval mechanisms, power differentiation detail, or expectations/fairness norms needed for A3)*

- SET foundational text; social exchange = voluntary actions motivated by expected returns; crucially involves **unspecified obligations** requiring trust — "Only social exchange tends to engender feelings of personal obligation, gratitude, and trust" (pp. 94–95); KS in KBFs is social exchange, not economic transaction; trust builds gradually through expanding exchanges
- Ch2 paradox of integration (p. 57): "outstanding qualities raise fears of dependence that inhibit acceptance as a sociable companion" — structural-exchange grounding for TPS predating Feather (1989) by 25 years; peer marginalisation is a rational defensive response to anticipated dependence and status displacement, not pathological deviance
- Expert dependence mechanism (pp. 44–45): repeated reliance on a TP's knowledge obligates peers to defer; anticipated deference "detracts from his attractiveness as a sociable companion" — explains why TPs in KBFs trigger avoidance precisely because of their knowledge contributions
- Reciprocity failure → KM withdrawal chain (p. 108): "failure to reciprocate engenders loss of credit and loss of trust, and it ultimately brings about exclusion from further exchanges" — TPS = peers failing to reciprocate TP's contributions → TP's rational recalibration of KS; unifies Ch2 exclusion mechanism with Ch4 SET mechanism
- A1 §2.1/§2.2/§2.4; A3 Ch2 (SET foundation, paradox of integration, expert dependence, reciprocity failure chain); A3 Ch5 (resentment residue as structural not pathological; overwhelming benefactions as boundary condition); A3 Ch6 (TP self-moderation recommendation grounded in Ch2 self-depreciation strategy)

## Social Comparison Theory — Org Applications

---

### Matthews, M.J. and Kelemen, T.K. (2025) 'To compare is human: A review of social comparison theory in organizational settings', *Journal of Management*, 51(1). doi: 10.1177/01492063241266157.

**Open access**

- Comprehensive review of 70+ years of SCT in org settings; upward comparison leads to envy, counterproductive behaviour, turnover; social dynamics comparisons most subjective and idiosyncratic
