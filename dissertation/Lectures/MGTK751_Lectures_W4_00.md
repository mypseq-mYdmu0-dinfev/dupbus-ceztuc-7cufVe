Characteristics of quantitative research methods
Arguably, quantitative methods have dominated management research and they are
generally associated with experimental and survey methods and data analysis
procedures that generates numerical data and uses statistical techniques. Saunders
(2019) provides a useful summary of the characteristics of quantitative research
methods, and we will explore these in more detail during this lecturecast.
However, you will see from this list that quantitative methods tend to relate to a positivist
philosophy of research which believes that there is an external, objective social reality
and the researcher is generally seen as independent from those being researched. It
tends to take a deductive approach where a theory is used to generate specific
propositions or hypotheses (Bryman and Bell 2008) and focuses on the 'what?', 'when?'
and 'how many?' questions., For example, often an aim of quantitative research is to
determine the relationship between one thing (an independent variable) and another (a
dependent variable) in a population. Thus, the theory may be that there are gender
differences with regard to participation in sport and the hypothesis generated is that
women are less likely to participate in sport than men.
In order to test this hypothesis quantitative date would then be collected through a
large-scale survey, the data from which would then be analysed using statistics and the
results would be used to support or reject the hypothesis or proposition.
1

Key Terms in Quantitative Research
Before we go on to the quantitative research design process there are a number of key terms that underpin this that it is important to be familiar with.  

- Variables: A measure of characteristics or behaviour patterns which may vary from one individual to another (e.g. age, place of birth, social class, strength, sex).
- Independent Variable: Presumed to explain the effect that is being researched.
- Dependent Variable: Can be explained by the effect of the independent variable. For example, the dependent variable could be sport participation and this is affected by the independent variable of age.
- Population: Can be very general (all human beings) or very narrow (all male, left-hand, vegetarians called Bob) but in either case, it's rare to have access to every member of a population.
- Sample: A small subset of the population. Used to infer things about the population as a whole. The bigger the sample the less likely it is to have sampling errors that mean that it does not reflect the behaviour of the population as a whole.
- Hypothesis: Statement of the relationship between two variables that can be empirically tested.

Hypothesis/Research Question(s)/Research Objective(s) 
Hypotheses tend to be used most in experimental methods and are defined by Smith (2010: 201) as an "informed speculation, which is set up to be tested, about the relationship between two or more variables".

 They are more common in the natural sciences than in the social sciences. In the social sciences research questions or objectives tend to be used, these are the overarching questions that focus on the topic and define the scope, scale and conduct of the research study. As highlighted in earlier sessions, it is crucial to have a research question or objective in order to ensure that your research is focused. 
You must have a clear and agreed research aim and objectives before you decide on a method or anything else for that matter. 

These can be slightly refined throughout the project but you need a direction!

Operationalisation of Concepts

Operationalisation involves defining concepts into variables that are measurable. This can be done in two ways: 
- first, using measures that have been developed in previous research, common ones include gender and social class; 
- second, we have to develop our own which may be based on informants from the particular group being researched and/or literature. 

There may well be debate and conflict about how a concept has been operationalised which results in review papers that attempt to pull together the research to try and identify how the concept can be conceptualised. It is important that you discuss such debates within your review of the literature in order to provide a justification for your measures.

Sampling: who is included?
Simple random sample
This approach involves choosing the sample group from a larger group (population)
entirely by chance, as each person has an equal probability of inclusion in the sample.
This tends to be used when the population is a homogeneous group, and you have
access to them all. For example, if you were to undertake a survey on frequency of
participation of students at a university sport centre you could undertake a random
sample using the list of students attending the university. In order to do this, you would
use the following steps.
Steps in devising a simple random sample
• Define the population (N) - All students enrolled at the university.
• Select or devise a comprehensive sampling frame - The list of students enrolled
on university courses.
• Decide your sample size - In order to generalise beyond your sample group you
are likely to want to use statistical tests (which will be covered in session 5). At a
simple level, the larger the sample the more confident we can be that it is
representative of the population from which it is drawn. It is also important to take
into account the likelihood of non responses (see the next section for more
discussion on this).
• List all the individuals in the population and assign them consecutive numbers
from 1 to N
• Using a table of random numbers, or a computer programme that can generate
random numbers, select n different random numbers that lie between 1 and N
• The students to which the n random number refers constitute the sample
Systematic Sampling
This is a more commonly used method. Everyone in the sample frame is again allocated
numbers, the first person is generated randomly and then the subsequent participants
are chosen at a constant interval for example every 9th person.
1
Using the same research project of university students' use of the sport centre the
following steps would be followed:
• If there were 15,000 students and a sample of 500 is required, we would calculate
the sampling interval by dividing 15,000 by 500 to give 30.
• Randomly generate a number between 1 and 30
• If the number 20 were generated select the individual who is allocated number 20
and then select every 30th person after that - 20, 50, 80 etc.

Sample size
The question students most often ask is how big the sample should be and the simplest
answer is to get as many people as practically possible. Even with random sampling it is
unlikely that the sample will be perfectly representative, this is termed sampling error
and is likely to decrease the bigger the sample.
Gratton and Jones (2010) suggest that the minimum size on which descriptive statistics
may be undertaken is 30, but a sample of 50 would be more desirable. Don't forget if you
have subgroups within this you will need a larger sample, so if you are comparing males
and females you are going to need at least 30 in each group. For example, the well-
known national polls frequently use samples of about 1,000 persons to get reasonable
information about national attitudes and opinions.
Please see the link on screen or the UK Management and Expectations Survey (MES)
document uploaded online. This gives you a good example of a large survey that was
conducted in the UK. Each section outlines the various steps taken to build this report
and conduct the research.

Response rates and non-response 


A response rate is: 

(Number of usable questionnaires / Total sample) ×100

Obtaining responses to surveys can be difficult. De Vaus (2014) stated that 30% non-response rate is actually quite good. The key issue with non-response is whether those that have responded are still representative of the population or whether there has been a systematic non-response from certain categories of people. For example, in our university sport centre research people may have been less likely to have responded because they don't use the sport centre thus creating a bias within the sample. Thus, it is important, where possible, to try and identify if there is a pattern to the non-responses/responses. 

The Management and Expectations Survey (MES) surveyed 25,006 firms, through a voluntary postal survey. Of this number, 4.8% elected to opt out of the voluntary survey, 38.7% replied with usable information, while 56.5% did not respond, including businesses that may have closed over the period.
This gives you an idea of the response rates for a large survey. You need to manage your expectations on the response rate as you could be really disappointed but equally delighted.

Research Design
A research design is the framework for the collections and analysis of data. There are a
number of different research designs that relate to quantitative research; prior to
outlining the different approaches it is important to examine two key criteria that are
used to assess if the research design is appropriate: reliability and validity.
Reliability is the extent to which a test or procedure produces similar results under
constant conditions on all occasions. In other words, is the research repeatable?
Validity refers to the extent to which the measure actually reflects the phenomenon
being researched. There are four key types of validity:
Measurement/construct validity relates to the extent to which a measure that is
devised to represent a concept really does reflect that concept, as many constructs that
we are investigating in research are not directly observable we use a hypothetical
construct to approximate what we are trying to observe. For example, occupations are
commonly used to indicate social class.
Internal validity: the level of confidence that the independent variable caused a change
in the dependent variable.
External validity: the extent to which the results of the study can be generalised beyond
the sample studied.
Ecological validity: the extent to which the findings are applicable to peoples' everyday
lives and natural social settings.
1

Classic Experimental Design (Bell et al, 2022) 
It is extremely unlikely that your MBA Project would be based on a classic experimental design but it is worth just providing a brief overview.

It is used to investigate whether a change in an independent variable has an effect on another dependent variable as shown in the diagram where two groups are created and in the experiment the independent variable is manipulated. The subjects are assigned randomly to their respective groups which means there would be confidence that any difference between the two groups is attributable to manipulation of the independent variable.

Experimental Group
So the experimental group receives the treatment, for example if looking at the impact of exercise on health then the experimental group would receive an exercise programme and it is compared against the control group, which does not.

Dependent Variable
The dependent variable of health is measured before and after the treatment
and a before-and-after analysis would be undertaken

Limitations
There are limitations to this approach in the social sciences: for example if experimental methods were used to examine the impact of sport on reducing crime then it would be difficult to set up treatment and control groups and it would be particularly difficult with regard to control for the other different variables that may also be impacting (Long,2017). Consequently scientific research designs are not common in management and business studies of recent.

Surveys as a research tool
Surveys are a common quantitative method in management research and could be an
appropriate tool for your project depending on your research aims and objectives. The
two main techniques are questionnaires and interviews. Questionnaires are usually
paper-and-pencil/online instruments that the respondent completes whilst Interview
based surveys are completed by the interviewer based on what the respondent says.
The advantages of questionnaires are that they are usually cheaper, quicker and
relatively anonymous. The disadvantages are that you can have a patchy response,
they might be incomplete or there is a frivolous response, therefore, more care is needed
in design. With regard to interviews the advantages are that there is more accuracy,
higher response rates, fuller and more complete answers and the design can be less
user-friendly. However, they can be more costly and there is less anonymity
Before deciding to undertake a survey there are a number of issues that you need to
consider. First, is it the best way to collect data with regard to the aims /research
questions of the study? Second, on a more practical level, it is important to identify
whether the population can be specified in order for you to then identify your sample.
You also need to think about whether or not the population is literate or if there are any
language issues, for example when researching children. Other practical administrative
issues include the cost of the survey in relation to the resources you have and also any
time constraints.
After considering any issues you need to consider how you will administer the survey as
identified in the introduction the main tools are respondent completion surveys, in the
form of questionnaires, and interviewer completion in the form of structured interviews.

However, Gratton and Jones (2010:130) have identified a number of potential disadvantages to online surveys:

Obviously, this approach will be restricted to online populations so you would need to consider whether this would exclude sections of your population. 

- Sampling bias: online surveys will generally not involve random samples from the population under investigation. Pilot The Questionnaire
It is critical to pilot the questionnaire in order to identify any problems thus it serves three key functions: 
- To check it makes sense
- To check it will work in situ
- To check that it is possible to analyse it in the way planned.

Pilot the questionnaire: If possible, piloting should be a two-stage process. 

Firstly, give it to a small number of people and sit with them whilst they complete it in order to identify if they have any problems.

Questionnaire design
Practical Steps to Questionnaire Design
In order to explain the approach to questionnaire design an example questionnaire
survey will be used throughout . This was a survey that was undertaken on behalf of what
was the then Department of Employment (DofE) and examined the perceptions and
expectations of employment of undergraduate sport students. As this was contract
research this created a number of issues which will become clear as we go through the
design process. A key issue is that the research was overseen by a steering group
consisting of representatives from the DfE, the sport industry, higher education and the
researcher meaning that there were a number of different perspectives on the research.
As with any research you need to clearly identify what your research aim is and reflect
on whether a questionnaire survey is the most appropriate way of achieving this aim.
From the aim research objectives are developed in order to address the research aim,
these questions need to be clear and focused. This is particularly important in survey
research as it is the research objectives that will underpin your questionnaire and are
used to operationalise the concepts that you are researching. Figure 1 shows the

In terms of operationalising key concepts through the research objectives, then the

• To categorise the types of sport organisations students wish to work for.
• To ascertain the skills and knowledge that students think they will need to gain
employment in the sport industry.
• To determine the extent to which UG students believe they have developed the
appropriate skills and knowledge to work in the sports industry during their
undergraduate degree.
• To ascertain the student’s expectations on the level at which they will enter the sport
industry.

Constructing the questionnaire
Many surveys involve comparisons with the aim of investigating how variables are linked
or how one may impact on another; the terms used are dependent and independent
variables. The dependent variable is that which will alter as a consequence of the value
of the independent variable. It is important you think about these variables as you
construct your questionnaire. For example, one of the areas for investigation of the DfE

skills that employers need. Undertaking work experience is the independent variable and
knowledge of skills is the dependent variable, we wanted to know if the students' level of
knowledge of skills was dependent on whether they had undertaken work experience or
not. Another example is sex as the independent variable and how that impacts on
expectations of entry-level into an organisation (dependent variable).

Structured interviews
Structured interviews, hereafter termed interviews in this section, involve the
administration of an interview schedule by an interviewer.
They enable the interviewer to establish a rapport with the respondent which addresses
the issue of individuals who ignore self-completion questionnaires or who may not
answer sensitive questions. It also enables a trained interviewer to employ appropriate
techniques to help a respondent who has problems understanding or interpreting a
question without affecting the integrity of the interview. For example, research with
children. It also has the advantage of enabling standardisation both when asking
questions and recording the answers, this means that any variation in people's replies
will be 'true' and not caused by the interview context (Bryman and Bell 2007). The
interview context refers to all respondents being given exactly the same stimulus, in that
interviewees have to read out questions in exactly the same way and in the same order.
Thus, any variation between respondents occurs not because of how the question was
asked or how the answers were recorded but because of a difference in the respondents'
behaviour, attitude or whatever the question is trying to find out about.
Most structured interviews are similar to questionnaires in that they contain closed,
closed-ended, pre-coded or fixed-choice questions (Bell et al, 2022). Question types will
be discussed in a separate section. Open questions could be used where the respondent
has more scope in their reply; however, the problem with this in face-to-face interviews
is that the interviewer has to interpret the respondent's reply when writing it down.
There are three different approaches to structured interviews:
In-person: this is the most expensive and time-intensive option but does enable direct

What are statistics?
In this lecturecast, we will examine some of the basic techniques for analysing
quantitative data, such as that obtained from surveys.
The first point is that you need to think about how you are going to analyse your data in
the planning stage of your project, not after you have completed your data collection,
this is because the decisions you make on the type and amount of data you collect will
have implications for the types of analysis that you use. A lot of people worry about
quantitative data analysis because of statistics, it is important to start with the point that
statistics are simply tools and techniques that are used to organise, summarise and
describe data that are quantitative or can be measure numerically; this includes
methods of drawing inferences and generalisations. Consequently they are there to turn
the data collected into information.
The key point is understanding which tests are most appropriate for your data, why they
are used and any limitations with the data and results. In terms of the mathematical
calculations there are packages such as the Statistical Package for the Social Sciences
(SPSS) that can perform the calculations.
There are two branches of Statistics, which we mention in your data analytics:
Descriptive Statistics: which are the methods used to describe general trends and
patterns in a data set including measures of central tendency and dispersion. This type
of analysis is termed univariate analysis as it involves the analysis of one variable at a
time
Inferential Statistics: Where ‘inferences’ are made about the population from a sample.
Can we rely on our findings? (Or how certain can we be that they are real!)
We will be covering both branches in this lecturecast.

Types of Variables
One of the things we need to be aware of before identifying what statistical tests we need to use and what are the types of variables we have. For example, has our questionnaire collected responses that are real numbers, such as age, or are they rank-ordered dichotomies in terms of yes/no or categories such as "every day" or "once a week"?  

In order to make sense of these and decide on the appropriate statistics that we would use we need to categorise them into a type of variable. 

Nominal variables
–
Comprise of categories that cannot be rank ordered (also known as categorical variables) e.g. sex (male/female), place of birth, course type, and work experience. Such variables must be mutually exclusive and the numbers assigned to them during coding have no numerical meaning

Ordinal variables
–
These are variables whose categories can be rank ordered but the distances between the categories are not equal across the range. for example Likert Scale ( completely disagree to completely agree)

Interval/ratio variables
–
These variables can measure the difference between values, for example, age if collected in the form of actual age rather than categories or actual income. This is the highest level of measurement which means that a number of different techniques can be applied. The difference between interval and ratio variables is that a ratio scale has a logical Zero point for example age or income, whilst interval scales do not have an absolute zero point - for example, temperature in f. 

Dichotomous variables
–
These variables contain data that only has two  categories such as yes/no

Categorising variables
The diagram from Bryman (2016), provides useful guidance on how to identify variables of each type. This is important because only certain statistical procedures can be undertaken on different types of data (or are useful in analysing them).  For example, What is the mean of Red, Blue and, Green??? Colour is ‘Nominal’ data and to think of using a mean is nonsense! We know this because we are familiar with means. But with more complicated stats you need to understand the type of data before you can select the appropriate statistical analyses.

2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 14, 16, 18, 20, 21

Inferential Statistics
Hypothesis Testing
This process of assessing the statistical significance of findings from a sample is
significance testing or generally known as hypothesis testing. The purpose of testing a
hypothesis is to determine the accuracy of the statement.
Statistics enable us to judge whether the differences/relationships found in the data are
statistically significant or due to random fluctuations in the data.
"A hypothesis is a statement of, or a speculation about, the relationships among the
variables that you intend to study" (Saunders and Berman Brown 2007:73).
The first stage in the process is to develop a null hypothesis, Ho which says there is no
association between the characteristics, and an alternative hypothesis, H1 which states
that there is an association between the two. Statistics can then be used to accept or
reject the null hypothesis
Hypothesis testing can be directional or non-directional.
Non-directional means that the hypothesis simply states that there is a statistical
difference or association - these are called two-tailed tests.
Directional tests mean that the hypothesis has stated if the direction of association is
positive or negative - these are termed one-tailed tests
An example of a one-tailed test is that women are more likely to be paid less than men
in the IT sector.
Need to know if you need a one or two-tailed test for some stats tests.
1

Statistical Significance
Important in hypothesis testing is the notion of statistical significance which relates to whether a research finding is probably true and is not due to chance. When reporting statistics we turn this around, thus significance level indicates the probability of a result being due to chance.

It is normally accepted that a finding is real if the probability of it occurring by chance is less than 5 in 100. In other words, there is a 95% chance of it being true. 

To explain further: if you were to take a sample from a population 100 times and see a relationship/difference, by chance you would find the same ‘strength’ relationship/difference 5 times when there was actually NO relationship/difference.

Statistical significance is expressed by showing how likely something is of not being true. E.g. p<0.05

As has already been identified, the key to using statistics effectively is not necessarily how to work out the mathematical equations, computer packages such as SPSS can do that, but to understand which test to use and when. 

The first thing to identify is what you are trying to analyse, this will probably fall into two main approaches: 
- Whether there are any relationships or associations between the different variables that have been measured. For example, the more money that is spent on marketing the higher the levels of purchases will be on a business development scheme.

Parametric/ Non-Parametric Statistics
As we have already explained, the test used will depend on the type of variable, but it will also be affected by the pattern or distribution of the data. Does it have a normal distribution? Fortunately, there is usually a corresponding non-parametric test for every parametric test. In addition, non-parametric tests are used for nominal and ordinal data whilst parametric tests are used for interval/ratio data. Relationships and Associations
So, let's start with relationships and associations, a key area for investigation for business
management research is the relationship between different variables. For example, is
there a relationship between the availability of capital and entrepreneurial start-ups?
Measures of association/correlation enable the investigation of relationships between
variables: variables are said to be associated/related if one variable differs in
accordance with another. However, it is important to note that measures of
association/correlation techniques will only show if there appears to be a relationship
not if there is causality, i.e. you cannot infer that one variable causes another.
There are a variety of statistical tests of association/correlation but, as with all statistical
tests, their use depends on the nature of the two variables being analysed in terms of
whether they are nominal, ordinal, interval/ratio or dichotomous and whether the data
has a normal distribution
Chi-Square: Non-parametric, nominal level variables.
Chi-square is a statistical test that is frequently used with questionnaire data and is
applied to contingency tables when both variables are nominal where it enables a
judgement to be made on the level of confidence we can have about there being a
relationship between the two variables. The premise behind Chi-Square is to compare
the table with what would be the expected results due to chance or probability. Sapsford
(2007:168) describes it as model-fitting "working out what the table would look like if
there was no association between the variables (if the null hypothesis were true) and
then assessing how different the observed figures are from the 'expected' ones". If there
is a sufficiently large difference this would suggest that there is a real difference in the
sample being studied.
Chi-square only tests whether or not there is a relationship or association between
categorical variables. In order to test the strength of the relationships correlation-like
measures such as the Phi coefficient or Cramer's V are used. In both cases, the strength
of the association ranges in value from 0 to +1.
1
A value of +1 indicates a perfect association
A value of 0.5 indicates a moderate association
A value of zero indicates no association

Measuring Associations with Numerical Data
When the data collected is numerical in the form of interval/ratio/ordinal variables then
a correlation coefficient can be used to identify the strength of association between the
two variables. Unlike with nominal data the correlation coefficient value can be between
-1 and +1 which indicates whether the association is positive or negative (direction) as
well as the strength. For example, if the number of purchases increased as the amount
spent on marketing increased this would be a positive correlation, whilst if there was a
decrease in purchases as marketing spend decreased this would be a negative
correlation. The strength of a correlation lends weight to the assumption that two
variables may be causally linked. There are two statistical measures that can be used:
Pearson's Product Moment Correlation Coefficient: This is a parametric test that can
only be used with interval/ratio level data. It is based on the assumption that both
variables come from populations that are normally distributed and that the relationship
between the variables is linear. To check these two preconditions a histogram and
scattergram should be constructed.
Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient: This is a non-parametric test used if the data
is ordinal or if you find that there is not a normal distribution.

Which Statistical tests?
Tests of Difference
Social science research often involves analysing differences or changes. This may be in
the form of trying to identify if two groups are different (differences in well-being
initiatives participation between men and women) or whether an intervention may have
caused a change (reduction in customer complaints after staff have undergone
customer service training). The purpose of tests of difference is to determine whether the
difference between the two groups or the difference in groups pre and post-intervention
is small enough to be due to chance or large enough to apply to the whole population. If
there is a large difference then it can be argued that the independent variable has
affected the dependent variable, although as with measures of association we can
never be 100% certain. As discussed in the introduction to this session the choice of
statistical test will depend on the nature of the data.
T Tests: parametric, interval/ratio data.
The simplest form of analysis of difference is to compare the means of the two groups to
determine if they are significantly different, the T test. There are two types of T test:
Independent samples T test - this is used to establish the difference between two groups
- for example, the level of participation in coaching by men and women
Paired sample T tests usually work with one sample to consider if there is a difference in
their behaviour in relation to different things (for example comparing the average
amount spent on hobbies compared to holidays) or a change in behaviour, attitude or
performance following an intervention. For example, whether there was a change in
customer complaints following staff customer care training.
