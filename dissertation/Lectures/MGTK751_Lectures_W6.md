What is a Recommendation?
A recommendation is a specific action or advice given to someone based on analysis,
expertise or experience. It is a suggested course of action or decision that is intended to help
the recipient solve a problem, improve performance or achieve a goal.
Recommendations are often presented in a formal or informal report, such as a research
paper, business plan or performance review. They can take many different forms, such as a list
of steps to follow, a set of guidelines to consider or a specific decision to make.
Recommendations should be specific, actionable and based on evidence or analysis.
Effective recommendations are important in many contexts, such as business, healthcare,
education, and government. They can help individuals or organizations make informed
decisions, improve performance, and achieve their goals. Good recommendations should be
well-informed, evidence-based, and take into account the specific needs and circumstances
of the recipient.
Overall, recommendations are an important tool for providing guidance and direction in a wide
range of contexts, and are often a key outcome of analysis, expertise, or experience.

Crafting a Conclusion
Crafting a strong conclusion is an important part of any written or spoken communication.
Here are some tips to help you craft a strong conclusion:
1. Summarise key points: A good conclusion should summarise the key points or ideas
presented in the communication. This helps the reader or audience remember the most
important aspects of your research message.
2. Provide closure: A conclusion should provide a sense of closure to the communication,
bringing the ideas or arguments to a logical end. This can help the reader or audience feel
satisfied with the communication and understand its overall purpose.
3. Offer insights: A conclusion should offer insights or perspectives on the topic, drawing from
the main points or ideas presented in the communication. This can help the reader or audience
understand the broader implications of the topic and why it matters.
4. Encourage action: A conclusion may encourage the reader or audience to take action based
on the insights or perspectives presented. This can help to motivate the reader or audience to
do something with the information they have received.
5. Be concise: A good conclusion should be concise and to the point. It should avoid repeating
information that has already been presented and focus on the most important insights or
perspectives.
6. Use language that is appropriate for the audience: The language used in the conclusion
should be appropriate for the audience and communication context. It should be clear,
concise, and free of jargon or technical terms that may be unfamiliar to the reader or
audience.

Stages of Crafting Recommendations

Click through the interaction below to see stages of crafting recommendations.

1 - Define the Problem
The first step in crafting recommendations is to clearly define the problem or issue you are addressing. What is the problem that needs to be solved? What are the underlying causes of the problem? What are the consequences of not addressing the problem? The better you can define the problem, the more targeted and effective your recommendations will be.

2 - Analyse the Data
Once you have defined the problem, you need to analyse the relevant data. This might include quantitative data, such as statistics or surveys, or qualitative data, such as interviews or case studies. You need to use the data to identify patterns, trends or insights that can inform your recommendations.

3 - Consider Alternatives
Before you make any recommendations, it's important to consider alternatives. What are the possible solutions to the problem? What are the pros and cons of each solution? By considering alternatives, you can ensure that your recommendations are well-informed and that you have considered all the available options.

4 - Prioritise Recommendations
Once you have identified potential solutions, you need to prioritise your recommendations. Which solutions are most likely to be effective? Which solutions are feasible given the resources available?
Which solutions are most important in addressing the underlying problem? By prioritising your recommendations, you can ensure that the recipient knows which actions to take first.

5 - Communicate Clearly
The final step in crafting recommendations is to communicate them clearly.
Recommendations should be written in clear, concise language that is easy to understand. Use bullet points or numbered lists to make the recommendations stand out. Provide concrete examples or case studies to illustrate your recommendations and make sure to explain why you are making each recommendation so that the recipient can understand the reasoning behind your suggestions.

Conclusion

In conclusion, crafting recommendations requires a systematic and analytical approach. By defining the problem, analysing the data, considering alternatives, prioritising recommendations and communicating clearly, you can create effective recommendations that can help others make better-informed decisions, solve problems or improve their performance.

Impacts of Poor Recommendations
If you have poor recommendations, it can have several negative consequences. Poor
recommendations can lead to:
1. Ineffective Decisions: Poor recommendations can lead to ineffective decisions that fail to
solve the underlying problem or improve performance.
2. Wasted Resources: Poor recommendations can lead to wasted resources such as time,
money or manpower that could have been better utilized elsewhere.
3. Lost Opportunities: Poor recommendations can cause individuals or organizations to miss out
on valuable opportunities for growth or improvement.
4. Decreased Trust: Poor recommendations can damage the trust between the expert and the
recipient. If the recommendations are not well-informed or not based on evidence, the
recipient may lose confidence in the expert's credibility or expertise.
To avoid poor recommendations, it's important to follow a systematic and analytical approach.
This includes defining the problem clearly, analysing the relevant data, considering
alternatives, prioritising recommendations and communicating clearly. By taking the time to
develop well-informed and thoughtful recommendations, you can increase the likelihood that
they will be effective and well-received.
If you have already provided poor recommendations, it's important to take responsibility and
take steps to correct the situation.
This might involve revisiting the recommendations and identifying areas for improvement or
working with the recipient to find a better solution. By taking ownership of the situation and
working to improve the recommendations, you can help restore trust and demonstrate your
commitment to providing high-quality guidance and insights.

Recommendations and Conclusions
Recommendations and conclusions are closely linked as both are important elements of any
analytical or research report.
Conclusions are the final thoughts or insights drawn from the analysis presented in the report.
They summarise the key findings and insights and provide an overall perspective on the
problem or issue at hand. Conclusions are typically presented at the end of the report and are
intended to provide closure and help the reader understand the significance of the analysis
presented.
Recommendations, on the other hand, are specific actions or steps that should be taken based
on the conclusions and analysis presented in the report. Recommendations are intended to
provide guidance and direction to the reader, and to help them apply the insights and findings
from the analysis in a practical way. Recommendations are typically presented after the
conclusions and are often structured as a set of actionable steps or suggestions that the
reader can implement.
In other words, conclusions are the "what" of the report, summarising the key findings and
insights, while recommendations are the "so what" of the report, providing guidance on what
actions should be taken based on those findings and insights.
While conclusions and recommendations are distinct elements of a report, they are closely
linked and should be developed in tandem. The conclusions provide the foundation for the
recommendations, and the recommendations should be based on the insights and findings
presented in the conclusions. By developing strong conclusions and recommendations that
are closely linked, you can provide a clear and actionable perspective on the problem or issue
at hand.

2.4. How your research has been conducted (briefly describe the methodology and data used)
2.5. Outline of the report

3. Literature Review
3.1. Brief review of relevant literature 
3.2. Identification and discussion of the key relationships between the variables

4. Methodology
4.1. A detailed description of the research method and data collection techniques employed

Minto Pyramid
As a reminder, the Minto Pyramid Principle is a methodology developed by Barbara Minto, a
former McKinsey consultant for organising and communicating ideas in a clear and structured
manner. The Minto Pyramid Principle is based on the idea that ideas should be presented in a
logical and hierarchical order with the most important ideas at the top of the pyramid and
supporting ideas presented in a structured and organised manner below.

The Minto Pyramid Principle consists of three parts:

1 - The Situation:
The first part of the pyramid is the situation, which provides context for the ideas being presented. This section should be concise and focused on the key facts and background information that are relevant to the ideas being presented.

2 - The Complication:
The second part of the pyramid is the complication, which identifies the problem or challenge that the ideas are designed to address. This section should be clear and concise, and should clearly articulate the problem that needs to be solved.

3 - The Solution:
The third and final part of the pyramid is the solution, which presents the ideas and recommendations for addressing the problem or challenge identified in the complication. The solution should be structured in a logical and hierarchical order, with the most important ideas presented first and supporting ideas presented in a structured and organised manner below.
