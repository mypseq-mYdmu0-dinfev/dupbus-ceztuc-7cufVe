Welcome to the Research Methods course for MBA students. In this course, we will explore the fundamental principles of research, data analysis, and statistical techniques used in business research. This course aims to provide students with the necessary knowledge and skills to design and conduct their research projects, analyse and interpret data, and present their findings in a clear and concise manner.

This module aims to:
Develop students’ ability to analyse and synthesise complex and ambiguous data from a wide variety of sources, in order to make reasoned judgments and recommendations underpinned by ethical values;
Enable students to integrate their knowledge of management theory and critically apply relevant theory to the analysis of management practice;
Provide students with an opportunity to undertake independent, practice-based research in a rigorous and systematic fashion using appropriate methods to investigate a management challenge facing an organisation, and to prepare and present findings, conclusions and recommendations.

Management Research
What is management research?
This work-based project is based on the notion of management research which is
described by Cameron (2021, page 485) as “a process of finding things out in a
purposeful and systematic fashion in order to increase knowledge”. In the context of a
work-based project this is likely to be your own and the specific organisation’s
knowledge but may also contribute to wider applied professional knowledge of the topic
or question you are focusing on.
Cameron suggests that management research can be characterised across three
dimensions from pure research to applied. The work-based project sits somewhere in
the middle of the continuums, it will focus on practice in terms of addressing an industry
or organisational issue, but it must be grounded in theory, it is unlikely to be
generalisable as it is small scale and focused, but there are likely to be themes and
learning that can be applied elsewhere, for example in similar organisations, finally as it
is focused on a real life problem it should be relevant but as real life situations are
complex then rigour in terms of all variable will be difficult. However, the research
process should be systematic, valid and reliable.

Management Research:
Other key points that need to be taken into account when undertaking management
research have been identified by Cameron and also Saunders et al.
First, it is transdisciplinary as it may draw on a range of disciplines or modules from
across the MBA programme, but what should also have become clear as you studied the
range of modules it that within many modules, they themselves have drawn on a range
of disciplines such as psychology, sociology and economics. This means there is likely to
be a vast amount of related research and literature that could underpin your project.
The key is to identify what is most relevant.
Second, management research has practical consequences – because the research is
applied it is likely to lead to some form of action or has an influence on individual or
organisational practice.
It also involves a reflexive process in that theory and practice inform one another, this is
often referred to as a virtuous circle of theory and practice where research on
managerial practice informs practically derived theory. Which then becomes a blueprint
for managerial practice, which increases practical management knowledge.
Consequently, management research must engage with both the world of theory and
the world of practice and problems addressed should develop from the interaction
between these two worlds rather than either on their own.
Finally, it requires organisational sponsors who need to be convinced of the potential use
of the findings – this could be you, your manager or your client organisation.

What is Philosophy?
The research onion
The research onion is a conceptual model developed by Saunders et al. (2012) that
represents the different layers of research design and methodology. The onion
metaphor suggests that the research process is layered and complex, with each layer
building upon the previous one to create a coherent and comprehensive research
design.
The layers of the onion are:
Research Philosophy: The first layer of the onion represents the researcher's underlying
beliefs and assumptions about the nature of reality and how knowledge is acquired. This
layer includes ontological and epistemological considerations, such as the researcher's
stance on objectivity and subjectivity, positivism or interpretivism, and realism or
relativism.
Research Approach: The second layer of the onion represents the broad approach to
research design and methodology, such as deductive or inductive, quantitative or
qualitative, or mixed methods. This layer includes decisions about the research design,
research question, and data collection methods.
Research Strategy: The third layer of the onion represents the specific research strategy
or plan, such as case study, experiment, survey, or ethnography. This layer includes
decisions about the sampling strategy, data collection methods, and data analysis
techniques.
Data Collection: The fourth layer of the onion represents the actual collection of data,
which may involve primary or secondary sources, and may be collected through
methods such as surveys, interviews, observation, or archival research.
Data Analysis: The fifth layer of the onion represents the analysis of data, which may
involve quantitative or qualitative techniques, such as statistical analysis, content
analysis, or thematic analysis.
1
Findings: The sixth and final layer of the onion represents the interpretation of the data
and the presentation of the findings, which may involve drawing conclusions, making
recommendations, or proposing further research.
Overall, the research onion provides a structured and systematic approach to research
design and methodology, helping researchers to ensure the validity and reliability of
their findings. The onion metaphor suggests that each layer of the research process is
interconnected and builds upon the previous one, highlighting the importance of a
comprehensive and integrated research design.

Two major philosophical schools of thought that offer important perspectives for contemporary businesses research: Positivist and Interpretative. 

These two approaches are largely considered to exist in opposition to each other and you could imagine them as being positioned at either end of a 'research philosophy continuum’. 

Gratton and Jones (2003:15) stress, the positivist and interpretative approaches have: 
"Differing epistemological and ontological assumptions, and each has differing implications for the methodology adopted by the researcher, the data that is (are!) collected, and the interpretation of such data".

Two main stances
Positivist
With regard to Ontology: the positivist approach is based on the ontological assumption
that there is "a measurable and objective reality that determines a universal truth"
(Andrews et al 2005:6).
Positivists argue that 'scientific' knowledge, based on 'facts' and 'laws', is the only true
form of knowledge and the only way to study human behaviour therefore is by using
scientific principles and methods.
The positivist Epistemology (which if you remember refers to how knowledge is
acquired) is "centred on data collection, objective distance between the researcher and
the subject, quantitative measurement, hypothesis testing and statistical analysis to
prove causality" (Andrews et al, 2005:6).
The positivist approach is founded on the strict avocation of objectivity. As Gratton and
Jones (2003) state. The whole research process should be objective, and no
measurements should be "subject to the influence of the researcher's values or
interpretation..the researcher has no influence on the findings, and has no personal
influence on the results" (Gratton and Jones, 2003:16). Thus, research should
be Replicable in that another individual could reach exactly the same conclusions based
on the precise evidence presented. Thus, the Truth can be objectively obtained through
the rigorous testing of hypotheses in terms of accepting or rejecting.

Two main stances
Interpretivist
The interpretative approach emerged fundamentally as a challenge to positivist
domination, adopting ontological and epistemological assumptions that opposed those
of positivism.
Ontological level -rather than suggesting that 'reality' or truth will be the same for
everyone (positivist ontology) -interpretative approach is founded upon the premise
that the social world is complex, that researchers and subjects are fundamentally and
subjectively attached to the world, and that people define their own realities
At the epistemological level, an interpretative approach prioritises "understanding the
particular behaviours, meanings and realities of individuals within particular social
settings" in order to acquire knowledge about the social world (Silk et al, 2005:6).
Interpretative researchers are interested in the experiences and viewpoints of their
research subjects. They seek to explore and interpret people's feelings, emotions,
behaviours and other intangible concepts which are too complex to be measured
numerically or reduced to numbers (Gratton and Jones, 2003:15).
Those adopting an interpretative approach see the merit in studying the social world in
its natural state rather than using strict, laboratory standards in an artificial and
unnatural environment (as positivists would advocate). Interpretative researchers
therefore promote qualitative methodologies "centred on observation, texts,
conversation, interpretation, narrative, writing, performance, and small-scale and local
interaction... a set of approaches to gathering knowledge of the social world that are
more fluid and flexible, and often emerge as the project unfolds" (Andrews et al, 2005:6).

Other philosophical approaches
As already mentioned, there are other philosophical approaches to research, including
critical realism, post-positivism, constructionist, and pragmatism, which are based on
different ontological and epistemological assumptions. Some find middle ground
between the positivist and interpretative approaches. We will briefly explore pragmatism

Research 'paradigms'
It is worth noting that you may well come across the term research 'paradigm' in your
reading and this is a term closely associated with research philosophy. In fact some
academics refer to the philosophical approaches discussed above as the positivist
paradigm and the interpretative paradigm and the terms are often used
interchangeably, but there is a very subtle difference.
Whereas research philosophy essentially underpins the research process and refers to
underlying assumptions about how the world is perceived and how we can best
understand it, it could be argued that the research paradigm encompasses a more
holistic view of research. The concept of the paradigm was introduced to research
design by Thomas Kuhn (1962) and can be defined as "the entire constellation of beliefs,
values and techniques shared by the members of a given scientific community"
(Andrews et al., 2005:5).
Denzin and Lincoln (2000:19-22) summarise that the research paradigm involves the
interconnected issues of ontology (what is the nature of reality?), epistemology (what is
the relationship between the inquirer and the known?) and methodology (how can we
know the world or gain knowledge of it?). Similarly, Long (2007:196) explains that a
paradigm can be seen as "the framework of beliefs shared by a (research) community;
sharing through practice and writing produces common models of the world and how
things work".
1
Clearly research philosophies and research paradigms are inextricably linked, so much
so that, as mentioned, the terms are often used as substitutes. So do not be surprised if
one text discusses research philosophy with no mention of research paradigms, or vice
versa, but try to keep in mind the subtle differences between the two.

Purpose of Research questions
Formulating Research Questions
Formulating research questions is an essential part of any MBA business research
project, as it sets the foundation for the entire research process. Research questions help
to clarify the scope and purpose of the study, guide the selection of appropriate
research methods, and ensure that the research is focused and relevant.
Here are some tips for formulating research questions in MBA business research:
Start with a broad topic: Begin by selecting a general topic that you are interested in
exploring. This could be a problem or opportunity that you have identified in a particular
industry or business context.
Narrow down the focus: Once you have identified a broad topic, you need to narrow it
down to a specific research question. This will help to ensure that your research is
focused and manageable. Consider the following questions to help you narrow down
your focus:
• What specific aspect of the topic do you want to explore?
• What research questions are you trying to answer?
• What are the key variables or factors that you want to investigate?
Ensure the research question is relevant and important: It is important to ensure that
your research question is relevant and important to the business community. Think
about the practical implications of your research and how it could be applied in a real-
world context.
Ensure the research question is feasible: Make sure that your research question is
feasible and can be answered within the constraints of the research project. Consider
the available resources, time, and data that are needed to answer the question.
1
Use clear and concise language: Your research question should be clear and concise,
using language that is easy to understand. Avoid using technical jargon or overly
complex language that could confuse or alienate your audience.
Seek feedback: Once you have formulated your research question, seek feedback from
your peers, professors, or industry experts to ensure that it is relevant, important, and
feasible.
By following these tips, you can formulate research questions that are relevant, focused,
and feasible, and that will guide your MBA business research project towards meaningful
results.

Types of Research Questions
Again be conscious of the research philosophy and your approach. 

What is it you want to find out?
- Predicting an outcome: Does y happen under circumstances a and b?
- Explaining causes and consequences of a phenomenon: is y affected by x? What are the consequences of x?
- Evaluating a phenomenon: does y exhibit the benefits that it is claimed to have?
- Describing a phenomenon: what is y like or what forms does y assume?
- Developing good practice: how can we improve y?
- Empowerment: how can we enhance the lives of those we research?
Denscombe, 2010

Qualitative (Interpretivist ) RQ’s
- Usually start with ‘what’ or ‘how’ (avoid beginning qualitative questions with ‘why’ as this implies cause and effect).
- Identify the central phenomenon you plan to explore (tell in your question what you are going to describe, explore, generate, discover, understand).
- Avoid the use of quantitative words such as relate, influence, effect, cause.

With qualitative research, you will usually have one central question and possibly also some sub-questions to narrow the phenomenon under study further. The sub-questions will generally be more specific.
Qualitative questions often change in the research process (e.g., refined over time). 

Examples of qualitative research questions: 
- How do family business idiosyncrasies influence the formation of organisational path dependence and, in the case of a lock-in, path-breaking, as well as strategic outcomes? 
- How do couples negotiate their work and family responsibilities when they encounter a conflict between the two?
- What challenges do MBA students identify when learning qualitative research?

Quantitative (Positivism) RQ’s
Remember the positivist approach – you are testing, measuring and experimenting. 
You will usually have a set of hypothesis upon which you set out to test if they are true or not
Often informally referred to as a ‘top-down’ approach. The starting point is with a particular pre-determined theory which is narrowed down to a more specific hypothesis. Research is conducted in order to collect observations which can be used to test hypotheses. This then confirms or rejects the original theory. 

- What is the relationship between employee training and development programmes and employee retention rates?
- Does workplace diversity influence firm performance?
- What is the influence of supply chain management practices on firm performance and profitability?
- What is the relationship between entrepreneurial orientation and firm growth in small and medium-sized enterprises?

should at the very least hold out the prospect of being able to make an original, novel
contribution—however small—to the topic.
Questions should be neither too broad nor too narrow. The research questions should
be neither too large (so that you would need a massive grant to study them) nor too
small (so that you cannot make a reasonably significant contribution to your area of
study).
