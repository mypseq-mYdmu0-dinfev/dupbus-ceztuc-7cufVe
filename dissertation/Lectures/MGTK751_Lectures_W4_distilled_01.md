## W4.1 — Quantitative Research Methods

### Core Characteristics
- Positivist, deductive, numerical; researcher independent of subject
- Focuses on relationships between variables (independent → dependent)
- Process: theory → hypothesis → operationalise → sample → collect → analyse → findings → loop back

### Sampling
- **Probability sampling** (each person has known/equal chance): simple random, systematic, stratified, cluster
- **Non-probability sampling** (some have greater chance): convenience, snowball, key informant, quota
- ⭐ **Sample size rule:**
  - Minimum 30 for descriptive statistics only
  - 50+ preferred; 100+ ideal for inferential analysis
  - If comparing subgroups (e.g. male vs. female): need 30+ per subgroup
- Response rates: De Vaus (2014) — 30% non-response = "quite good"; expect and plan for it
- ⚠️ Convenience sampling (surveying friends) = tempting but creates bias; not generalisable

### Research Designs
- **Cross-sectional** = most common; one point in time; snapshot
- **Longitudinal** = same group over time; tracks change
- Time series = measures variable before/after intervention
- Experimental design = extremely unlikely for MBA

### Survey Tools
- Questionnaire (self-completion): cheaper, quicker, more anonymous; risk of incomplete/frivolous responses
- Structured interview (interviewer-administered): higher accuracy, better response rate; costlier, less anonymous
- ⭐ Pilot testing is critical: 2-stage (small group for sense-check → small sample in situ); fix issues before full survey

### Questionnaire Design
- Closed questions (Likert, semantic differential, checklists, ranking): easy to process, comparable, but lose spontaneity
- Open questions: richer data but harder to code, more effort for respondent
- Language must be clear, unambiguous, non-leading; avoid double-barrelled questions; sensitive questions at end
- Code questions during design phase, not after data collection

## W4.2 — Quantitative Analysis

### Descriptive Statistics
- Frequency distribution (bar charts for nominal; histograms for interval/ratio)
- Central tendency: mean (interval/ratio only; sensitive to outliers), median (mid-point; outlier-resistant), mode (only for nominal)
- Dispersion: range (crude), standard deviation (most useful; average variation around mean)
- Normal distribution = bell curve; basis of many statistical tests; skewed data = bias risk

### Inferential Statistics
- Makes inferences from sample to population
- Hypothesis testing: H0 (null = no relationship) vs. H1 (alternative = relationship exists); use stats to accept/reject H0
- Significance level: p<0.05 = 95% confidence result is not due to chance
- **Chi-square**: non-parametric; nominal data; tests if relationship exists (not strength)
- **Pearson/Spearman correlation**: interval/ratio or ordinal; tests strength + direction of relationship
- **T-tests**: parametric; compares means between groups (independent samples) or pre/post (paired samples)
- Parametric = normal distribution + interval/ratio data; non-parametric = skewed/ordinal/nominal data
- ⭐ Tools: SPSS (UoL-licensed; LinkedIn Learning training available), Excel for basics

### Key Distinction
- ⭐ **Correlation ≠ causation** — statistical association shows relationship, not proof that one variable causes another; crucial for write-up language
