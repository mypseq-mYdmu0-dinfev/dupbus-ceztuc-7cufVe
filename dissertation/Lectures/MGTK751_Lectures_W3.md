There are many definitions of the literature review but there are many common themes. A quick search around throws up different ways of saying the same thing.
A literature review is a critical analysis and synthesis of the existing research and scholarly literature on a particular topic. It involves examining, summarising, and evaluating the key ideas and findings from a range of sources, such as academic journals, books, and other relevant publications.

An academic literature review is an analytical summary of published research studies, scholarly articles, books, and other sources relevant to a particular topic or research question.

The purpose of a literature review is to provide a summary and evaluation of the existing research and knowledge in a particular field or subject area.
A literature review typically involves a systematic and thorough search of databases and other sources to identify relevant publications. The review then involves reading and critically analysing the selected publications, with the goal of identifying key findings, trends, gaps in knowledge, and areas for further research.

The structure of a literature review can vary depending on the specific research question or topic, but it typically includes an introduction that explains the research question and purpose of the review, a section that describes the search strategy and selection criteria for the publications, a synthesis section that summarises the key findings and themes, and a conclusion that discusses the implications of the review for future research and practice.


Literature reviews are commonly found in academic research papers, dissertations, and theses, and are important for establishing the context and significance of the research being conducted.

Reviewing the Literature

summarises the current state of knowledge on a particular topic, providing readers with

gaps or inconsistencies in the existing research.
4. To provide evidence for the significance of a research topic: A literature review can
demonstrate the importance and relevance of a research topic, by showing the ways in
which it has been studied and the impact that it has had on the field.
5. To inform the methodology and design of research studies: Literature reviews can help
researchers to identify appropriate methods and designs for their own research studies,
by highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of existing research in the field.
Overall, literature reviews are an essential part of academic research, providing a
foundation of knowledge and context for further research and helping to guide the

Sources of information
Sources of information for a literature review should be scholarly and reputable,
meaning they have undergone a rigorous peer-review process to ensure the accuracy,
validity, and reliability of their content. Examples of sources you can use for a literature
review include:
1. Academic journals: These are publications that specialize in publishing original
research articles and reviews written by experts in the field. Examples of academic
journals include Academy of Management, Journal of Management, Human
Relations or Organisational studies plus many many more.
2. Books: These can include both academic and non-academic texts, such as
textbooks, monographs, and edited collections.
3. Conference proceedings: These are publications that compile the papers and
abstracts presented at academic conferences and symposiums.
4. Online databases: These include electronic resources such as JSTOR, PubMed, and
Web of Science.
It is important to critically evaluate each source you use for your literature review to
ensure that it is relevant, reliable, and current. Additionally, you should ensure that you
are using sources that reflect a range of perspectives and approaches to the topic
under review.
1

Grey literature 
In some cases, given your topic, you may need to explore non-academic sources for the literature review. 

Grey literature refers to information that is not published commercially or indexed in traditional academic databases. This can include reports, working papers, policy documents, conference proceedings, and other materials that are produced by government agencies, non-profit organizations, and other non-commercial sources.

Grey literature can be an important source of information for researchers, as it often contains valuable data and analysis that is not available through traditional academic sources. However, because grey literature is not typically peer-reviewed or subject to the same quality control standards as published academic research, it is important to critically evaluate the sources and methods used in grey literature.
Some examples of grey literature sources include government reports, conference proceedings, technical reports, white papers, preprints, dissertations, and policy briefs. Many organisations also maintain online repositories of their own grey literature, which can be accessed through their websites. 
Validate and consider the source carefully! NEVER Wikipedia!
Criteria for evaluating sources of information
There are four criteria suggested by Easterby-Smith et al, (2015). 

Purpose: What is the purpose of this source? What are the motivations or interests that led to its creation? This is usually set out in the abstract of the paper or could be found on the organisations website

Authorship: Who created it? Who is responsible? Are they authoritative in this area?

Credibility and Accuracy: Why is it credible? Do they give enough information on the methods used and data upon which they make claims. Has the work been peer reviewed and taken up in academic journals? If not why might that be? Could there be an element of bias in the information given and how accurate is that information?

Timeliness: When was the information created? When was it last updated? Has anything changed within the field that means the work is no longer valid?

Types of literature review
There are several types of literature reviews that can be conducted in academic
research, depending on the research question and purpose of the review. Here are some
common types of literature reviews:
Systematic literature review: This type of review involves a thorough and comprehensive
search of published literature, using specific inclusion and exclusion criteria to identify
relevant studies. The studies are then critically evaluated and synthesized, with the goal
of identifying key themes, trends, and gaps in knowledge.
Narrative literature review: A narrative review is a more general overview of the
existing literature on a particular topic. Unlike a systematic review, a narrative review
is not based on a specific search strategy or set of inclusion and exclusion criteria.
Instead, it provides a broad overview of the literature on a particular topic, often with a
more subjective and interpretive approach.
Meta-analysis: A meta-analysis is a type of systematic review that uses statistical
methods to synthesize the results of multiple studies. The goal of a meta-analysis is to
identify patterns or trends across studies, and to provide a more quantitative
understanding of the findings.
Scoping review: A scoping review is a type of review that aims to identify and map the
existing literature on a particular topic. It is often used when a topic is new or under-
researched, or when there is a need to identify gaps in the existing literature.
Rapid review: A rapid review is a streamlined version of a systematic review, designed to
be completed quickly in response to a pressing research question or policy need. A rapid
review often involves fewer studies than a full systematic review, and may use less
rigorous inclusion and exclusion criteria.
Overall, the type of literature review chosen will depend on the research question, the
available resources, and the purpose of the review.

Venn Diagram – Key Words
A Venn diagram is a useful tool for visually representing the overlap and differences
between two or more sets of data. In the context of a literature review, a Venn diagram
can be used to compare and contrast the findings or ideas of different sources.
To create a Venn diagram for a literature review, follow these steps:
Determine the sources you want to compare: This may include two or more authors or
studies that you want to compare and contrast.
Identify the key themes or ideas in each source: As you read each source, identify the
main themes or ideas that are relevant to your research question.
Group the themes or ideas: Based on the key themes or ideas you have identified, group
them into categories that overlap or differ between the sources.
Create the Venn diagram: Using a diagramming tool, such as Microsoft PowerPoint or
Google Slides, create a Venn diagram with overlapping circles. Label each circle with the
name of the source you are comparing. Place the overlapping themes or ideas in the
area where the circles overlap, and the unique themes or ideas in the areas that do not
overlap.
Interpret the results: Analyse the Venn diagram to identify similarities and differences
between the sources. This will help you to develop a more nuanced understanding of the
topic and to identify gaps or areas of agreement in the existing literature.
Using a Venn diagram can help you to visually represent complex ideas and to identify
patterns and relationships between different sources. It can also be a useful tool for
organising your literature review and presenting your findings to others.

How to conduct a literature review
Conduct a literature research
The literature is vast, so the first job is to plan a search strategy.
First you need to identify the key words and search terms you will use; you can do this by
breaking down your Research Aim and objectives to define relevant key words/
concepts and then listing the various terms that might be used for each of your
concepts – these are likely to evolve throughout your search. When you are reading
relevant articles make a note of any specialist terminology or other useful key words that
you might want to add to your own search strategy
Think about the database/search engines you will use, and the library guides will help
you here. You then use Boolean Logic, which is a logical process for searching
databases based on combining terms such as AND, OR NOT).
Think about the parameters to your search – depending on the volume of literature that
exists already on your specific topic area/problem – it might be useful to widen your
parameters – E.g., Explore what has been found in different contexts/industries/
countries – but demonstrate awareness of how these different contexts may have had
an impact on the findings and what might be different within your context of focus and
why?
Also think about the criteria to assess the quality of information found (e.g., will you limit
your search to peer reviewed articles?), what about industry research – the balance
should be towards academic literature from peer reviewed journals, but it is likely you will
use industry/government reports and research within your study.
N.B. You will probably need to test and refine your search strategy numerous times.

Documenting your literature searches is important for several reasons. It allows you to
keep track of the sources you have searched and the terms you have used, which can
help to avoid duplication of effort and ensure that you have conducted a
comprehensive search. Here are some techniques for documenting your literature
searches:
Use a search log: Create a document or spreadsheet where you can record the details

used, and the number of results retrieved. You may also want to include notes on the

sources.

can help you to keep track of your search results and easily import and organize the
sources you find.
Take notes: As you conduct your search, take notes on the sources you find and their
relevance to your research question. This can help you to quickly refer back to important
sources and identify gaps in the literature that need further exploration.
Overall, documenting your literature searches is an important step in conducting a
rigorous and comprehensive literature review. By using these techniques, you can
ensure that you are conducting a thorough search and keeping track of the sources you

General steps to follow
Here are some general steps for conducting a literature review:
1. Define the research question: Before beginning the literature review, it is important to
define the research question or topic that you will be exploring. This will help to guide
your search and ensure that you are focused on relevant literature.
2. Develop a search strategy: Identify the databases and sources that you will use to
search for relevant literature. This may include academic databases, search engines,
and other sources such as grey literature repositories. Consider using keywords and
phrases that are relevant to your research question, and use Boolean operators (such as
AND, OR, and NOT) to refine your search.
3. Conduct the search: Use your search strategy to identify relevant literature and record
your search terms and sources. It is often helpful to use a reference management tool
(such as Sotero or EndNote) to organise and track your search results.
4. Evaluate the literature: Review the titles and abstracts of the literature that you have
identified and select those that are relevant to your research question. Read the full text
of these articles, taking notes on key findings, methods, and limitations.
5. Synthesise the literature: Analyse and synthesise the literature that you have reviewed,
identifying key themes, trends, and gaps in knowledge. Consider using a systematic
approach to organise your findings (such as a matrix or thematic map) and identify
areas where further research is needed.
6. Write the literature review: Summarise and discuss your findings in a clear and
organised manner, drawing conclusions and making recommendations based on the
literature that you have reviewed.
7. Revise and edit: Review and revise your literature review, ensuring that it is well-
organised, clearly written, and free from errors and inconsistencies. You may come back
to this several times over the lifecycle of the project to refine it and make it sharper and
better linked to the project.
1
Overall, conducting a literature review requires careful planning, thorough research, and
critical analysis of the literature. By following these steps, you can ensure that your literature
review is comprehensive, rigorous, and relevant to your research question.

LESSON 9 OF 9
Literature reviews are critical components of research studies in all fields of inquiry because they provide a comprehensive overview of the existing research and knowledge on a particular topic. By reviewing and synthesizing relevant literature, a literature review helps researchers to:

- Identify gaps and limitations in the existing literature, which can inform the development of new research questions and hypotheses.
- Develop a theoretical framework for their research, which can help to guide the study design and data analysis.
- Evaluate the validity and reliability of existing research studies and identify areas where further research is needed.
- Provide a basis for comparing and contrasting different studies and approaches to the topic.
- Identify key themes and patterns in the literature, which can help to inform the development of new theoretical frameworks or models.

Overall, literature reviews are critical for advancing knowledge and understanding in a particular field of inquiry and can help researchers to design and execute more informed and effective research studies.

Hubbard and Dunbar (2017) study
Hubbard and Dunbar 2017 complete a paper titled "Perceptions of scientific research
literature and strategies for reading papers depend on academic career stage“ in 2017
which explores how academic career stage influences scientists' perceptions of
scientific research literature and the strategies they use to read academic papers.
The authors surveyed 113 researchers from various academic career stages
(undergraduate students, graduate students, postdoctoral researchers, and faculty
members) to understand how they perceive academic papers and how they approach
reading them.
The study found that academic career stage significantly influences researchers'
perceptions of scientific research literature. For example, undergraduate and graduate
students were found to view academic papers as intimidating and difficult to
understand, while postdoctoral researchers and faculty members viewed them as
essential and informative sources of knowledge.
Graph A shows the sections of a paper people found easiest to read against career
stage.
Graph B shows which sections rank most important against career stage. It is clear that
Abstracts and introductions are easier to read but the results findings are rank more
important. So, we need to find good sources, journal articles, books and so on quickly
and harvest their findings in an efficient way.
The study also found that researchers' reading strategies differed based on their
academic career stage. For example, undergraduate and graduate students tended to
read academic papers in a linear fashion, from beginning to end, while postdoctoral
researchers and faculty members used more targeted reading strategies, such as
focusing on specific sections or skimming the paper for key information.
Overall, the study highlights the importance of recognizing the influence of academic
career stage on scientists' perceptions and strategies for reading academic papers. The
authors suggest that educators and mentors can help to support students and early-

Why is it difficult and time consuming?
Academic papers can be challenging to read for several reasons:
1. Technical language: Academic papers often use specialized technical language that
can be difficult for readers who are not familiar with the subject matter to understand.
2. Dense content: Academic papers are typically dense and packed with information,
making them challenging to read and comprehend.
3. Lengthy and complex sentences: Many academic papers use lengthy and complex
sentences that can be difficult to follow and understand.
4. Jargon and abbreviations: Academic papers often use jargon and abbreviations
specific to the field of study, which can be confusing for readers who are not familiar
with them.
5. Formal structure: Academic papers typically follow a formal structure and use
conventions such as abstracts, introductions, methods, results, and conclusions. This
structure can be difficult to navigate for readers who are not familiar with it.
6. Limited context: Academic papers often assume a certain level of prior knowledge and
provide limited context or background information on the topic, which can make it
challenging for readers to understand the significance and relevance of the research.
It is important to note that reading academic papers is a skill that can be developed
through practice and familiarity with the conventions and language of the particular
field of study. Researchers and educators can also take steps to make academic papers
more accessible to a wider audience by using plain language, clear and concise
sentences, and providing context and background information where necessary.

Hints and Tips
The best advice we can give you is to Know what you’re looking for!
Start with the Abstracts. Read it to determine if its relevant. If yes, then continue to read
the introduction and conclusion, confirm its relevant.
If you don’t know the word, don’t get too hung up on it! Highlight it and move on. It’s likely
you will know what it means later on in the paper.
Textbooks are a great starting point in a discipline as they usually give away who the key
authors are. They are good starting point but cannot be relied upon for this MBA project.
You need to go deeper, you need breadth and depth.
It is worth noting that good papers from authoritative journals will use section
headings very well. You could jump between section to determine which are most
relevant and read efficiently. Good papers will have well written opening sentences to
paragraphs which can also help us to determine is the rest worth reading.

Listed here are some takeaways to think about.
- What previous research and ideas were cited that this paper is building off of? 
- Was there reasoning for performing this research, if so what was it? Why is it of interest? 
- Clearly list out the aims and/or objectives of the study
- Were any statistical tests used? What were their results? 
- What are the main findings? 
- How do these results fit into the context of other research and their 'field’? 
- Are any of the authors familiar, do you know their previous work? What key terms and concepts keep appearing in my reading that do I not know and need to look up in a dictionary, or textbook, or ask someone?
