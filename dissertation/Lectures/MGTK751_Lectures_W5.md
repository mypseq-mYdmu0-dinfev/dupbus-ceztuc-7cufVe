Qualitative Research

Qualitative researchers are interested in the complexity of social interactions that occur in daily life and the meanings the participants themselves attribute to these interactions. That is to say they are concerned with individual’s accounts of their own experiences, behaviour, actions, motivations, perceptions and intentions. 
Qualitative Research is an approach for exploring and understanding the meaning individuals or groups ascribe to a social or human problem (Creswell 2017)
When done properly, it involves the designing of an approach that does justice to the complexity of the object under study, thus it is not reduced to single variables, but is studied in all of its complexity.

Interviews

We will start by examining the interview approach to research. If you think about it, the most logical way to find something out from someone is to talk to them directly and in a way that enables you to gather information as you are able to ask specific questions relating to what it is you want to find out, basically that is the approach of interviews. 
Brinkmann and Kvale (2015:3) suggest that interviews attempt to “understand the world from the subjects’ point of view, to unfold the meaning of the experience, to uncover their lived world.
Thus, undertaken appropriately, interviews generate data that enable a rich insight into peoples’ experiences and the meanings people attribute to those experiences, this aims to gain an understanding of the participant's social world and also how they act within it. Although Johnson and Duberley (2003:168) warn that it is “impossible to experience the experience of another”, Miller and Glassner (2004) concur with this when they state that research cannot provide a “mirror reflection of the social world” however, they argue that information about that world is achievable through in-depth interviewing”. 

Thus, interviews are not as simple as having a chat and it is very important to have a systematic and well-thought-through approach that enables you to achieve your research objectives.

As outlined by Gratton and Jones (2010) there are several different types of interview structure: 

STRUCTURED
Structured interviews are essentially a questionnaire read out to respondents, and are akin to that which we covered in the session on quantitative methods. However the interviewer is able to seek clarification of responses although they need to adhere rigidly to predetermined questions. Thus this approach is often used when there are multiple interviewers on a larger research project and is usually based on a deductive research design. 

SEMI-STRUCTURED
Semi-structured interviews use a standard set of questions or schedule, however, there is a flexible approach in that the interviewer can alter the sequence of the interview or probe with subsidiary questions in response to the answers that the participant might give, so it feels more like a conversation, but does require the interviewer to have good listening skills and by the end of the interview the schedule of topics or questions needs to be covered. Generally, the more experienced the interviewer the less structured the interview schedule is, whilst an inexperienced interviewer is likely to have the actual questions written down. This emphasises why it is important to pilot your interview as this is also about developing your own skills and confidence in interviewing. Semi structure interviews are often based on a research design that is a balance between deductive and inductive, in that theory has informed the approach but other key themes may be uncovered during the interview process.

UNSTRUCTURED
Unstructured interviews consist of very general ideas of topics or themes and the participant tends to lead the direction of the interview with further questions developing as the interview progresses so the participant is developing the areas that are important to them. These are usually based on an inductive research approach.

Focus groups

We will now move on to look at focus groups, these are similar in many ways to interviews, with the key difference being that it involves a small group of people, rather than a single respondent. They are useful as they enable members of the groups are able to interact with each other, with the interaction leading to a greater depth of discussion, in that ideas can be generated and discussed between group member which allows for ‘richer’ information to be gathered than if participants were asked individually. 
Continued

When to use focus groups
When a particular group is important in a study but is very small in number – they would not be adequately represented using other methods (surveys, individual interviews). An example would be when examining brand perception. Focus groups can be used to explore how customers perceive a brand and its image. By examining the associations and emotions that customers associate with a brand, businesses can develop strategies to improve brand equity and differentiation from competitors.

This is particularly true when you want to gather collective experiences, so maybe if exploring the collective experiences of a group of staff with regard to the introduction of a new system of working. Finally, when the interaction/discussion process is itself interesting, this might be regarding a group of managers' views on corporate social responsibility within an organisation which may enable debate and uncover tensions within and across the manager's views.

Investigative Workshops 
An investigative workshop is a collaborative event designed to explore a specific topic or problem through the collective efforts of a diverse group of participants. The primary goal of an investigative workshop is to generate new ideas, insights, and knowledge through active engagement and collaboration among the participants. 

Investigative workshops can take many forms, but they typically involve a mix of presentations, discussions, and small-group activities focused on a particular topic or problem. They may be organised by academic or research institutions, non-profit organisations, or private companies, and may include participants from a wide range of disciplines and backgrounds.

During an investigative workshop, participants may engage in activities such as brainstorming, problem-solving, data analysis, and prototyping. The goal is to encourage open dialogue and exchange of ideas, and to foster a collaborative and innovative environment where new ideas can emerge.

Overall, investigative workshops are an effective way to bring together diverse perspectives and expertise to address complex problems and generate new knowledge. They can be used in a variety of settings, from academic research to industry innovation, and can help to catalyse progress and change in a particular field.

Other varieties of this approach
There are several other terms that are used to describe the type of research that involves
collaborative problem-solving and innovation, such as:
1. Action research: This is a collaborative research method that involves participants working
together to identify and solve practical problems in a particular context.
2. Participatory research: This is a research approach that emphasises the participation of
stakeholders in all aspects of the research process, including problem identification,
data collection, analysis, and interpretation.
3. Design thinking: This is a problem-solving approach that involves a collaborative and
iterative process of empathising with users, defining problems, ideating potential solutions,
prototyping and testing those solutions, and implementing the best one.
4. User-centered design: This is an approach to design that involves understanding the needs
and experiences of users and designing solutions that meet their needs.
5. Co-creation: This is a process of collaborative creation in which stakeholders work together
to develop new ideas, products, or services.
These approaches all share a focus on collaboration and engagement with stakeholders, and
are often used in business, social sciences, and other fields where practical problem-solving
and innovation are valued.

Analysing qualitative data
We will now examine how to analyse the qualitative data that is generated by the focus groups and interviews – the raw data is normally recordings of the interviews and focus groups that are then transcribed in full, in fact qualitative research can generate enormous amounts of data. 

There is no uniform way to analyse qualitative data because the nature of qualitative data differs and at the most basic level qualitative data analysis consists of the processes and procedures that take the qualitative data collected   and provide some form of explanation, understanding or interpretation of the people and situations that are being investigated. 

The most respected writer on qualitative analysis are Miles and Huberman (1994: 3) who stress that:
Words, especially organised into incidents or stories, have a concrete vivid, meaningful flavour that often proves far more convincing to a reader – another researcher, a policymaker, a practitioner – than pages of summarised numbers.

Thus it is important to do justice to the qualitative data that you have generated. The process of qualitative data analysis is a crucial part of the research process and one that can be exciting, stressful, confusing, enlightening and inspiring, all at the same time. 

The most common approach to analysing qualitative data is to search for, code and organise your data around, key themes.

Stages of qualitative analysis
Prior to data collection, you are likely to be concerned about whether you are going to be able to gather enough ‘good’ data for meaningful analysis. Hopefully, your data collection will result in the generation of pages and pages of the interview or focus group transcripts, field notes, archive documents and so on. The next challenge is to work out how to organise and manage the data you have gathered, how to interpret it, how to make sense of it and how to present it.

Stages of qualitative analysis
Gratton and Jones (2010) provide a useful overview of the stages of qualitative data analysis
based on the framework that was devised by Miles and Huberman
First Data reduction - should take the researcher from a mass of raw qualitative data to data
that is reduced and organised either through "coding, writing summaries, discarding irrelevant
data and so on" This is not done before analysis; it is an integral part of the analysis, the
researcher will need to make decisions on which data chunks to code and which to pull out,
which patterns best summarise a number of chunks, which evolving story to tell – these are all
analytic choices. Thus Data reduction is a form of analysis that sharpens, sorts, focuses,
discards, and organises data in such a way that 'final' conclusions can be drawn and verified.
Data display - this part of the analysis activity involves assembling qualitative data in an
organised and compressed way to enable conclusion drawing. Miles & Huberman (1994: 12)
advocate the use of 'displays' that are: Designed to assemble organised information into an
immediately accessible, compact form so that the analyst can see what is happening and
either draw justified conclusions or move on to the next step of the analysis the display
suggests may be useful.
Conclusions: drawing/verifying - from the effective analysis you should be able to develop
conclusions. You should not view these as your final conclusions however, as you are likely to
verify the initial conclusions drawn through further reference to your qualitative data and
possibly further data collection (Gratton & Jones, 2010). Many researchers also seek verification
from other researchers who can cast a more objective eye over the data reduced and
organised.

Thematic Analysis
A common approach to analysing qualitative data is thematic analysis which entails searching across a data set to identify, analyse, and report repeatedpatterns (Braun and Clarke). This is much more than simply summarising the data; a good thematic analysis interprets and makes sense of it. Criteria for evaluating qualitative research

In the session on quantitative research, we highlighted how you can assess the quality of the research using the notion of reliability and validity. Although the approach is different, the terms are still applied by some in qualitative research. However, Guba and Lincoln (1994) two of the foremost writers on qualitative research proposed more specific criteria to assess quality – trustworthiness and authenticity – it is trustworthiness that is most widely used and we will focus on that. Trustworthiness is made up of four criteria: 

Credibility
–
Parallels internal validity and relates to acceptability to others. Therefore, it focuses on ensuring that the research is conducted in accordance with what is considered good practices and that the findings are shared with the participants to confirm the accurate understanding of their social world, which is termed respondent validation. Another approach to credibility is to be reflexive such as considering how your own beliefs and assumptions may have affected your work. 

Transferability
–
The extent to which the results can be transferred to other contexts or settings with other respondents. Guba and Lincoln (1994) suggest that a way of establishing transferability is to provide a dense description of the population studied by describing the demographics and geographic boundaries of the study. 

Dependability
–
Dependability relates to reliability and suggests that researchers should keep an audit trail of complete records of all phases of the research process and the decisions made with regard to: 
- The specific purpose of the study
- How and why the participants were selected for the study
- How the data was collected and how long collection lasted
- How the data was reduced or transformed for analysis
- Interpretation and presentation of the findings
- The techniques used to determine the credibility of the data

Confirmability
–
Confirmability relates to objectivity. It is recognised that achieving complete objectivity is impossible, as discussed earlier in the module within interpretivist research philosophies. However, the research should show that the researchers acted in good faith and made efforts to minimise the influence of personal values on the research and findings as much as possible. This involves being self-reflective. For example, taking notes on personal feelings, biases, and insights immediately after an interview.

Mixed Methods

The final approach we will look at is mixed methods which combines quantitative and qualitative research. Given the discussions regarding research philosophy that we covered in the second week of the module, you will probably realise that this approach can be seen as controversial by those who take a purely positivist or interpretivist perspective, however, we did note that a mixed methods approach is often used by pragmatists. The argument for using mixed methods is often based on the view that quantitative and qualitative methods have individual weaknesses and it makes sense to combine the two. Plana Clark and Ivankova define mixed methods as a research design in which researchers mix quantitative and qualitative methods in specific ways to address a research purpose” Plano Clark & Ivankova (2016).

It is important to note that there are truly mixed designs and quasi-mixed designs. Quasi designs are where researchers collect and analyse both quantitative and qualitative data but make no attempt to meaningfully mix the methods or results to address the research Objectives. Truly mixed methods involve the researcher mixing quantitative and qualitative methods to generate inferences grounded in both sets of results. (Plano Clark and Ivankova).

Mixed methods can be a Fixed design that is planned from the start or emergent in that the research does not produce the necessary information or there may be unexpected findings so another method is added. Given the word count, time and resource constraints, you are advised to avoid using mixed methods for your MBA Project.

Three Basic Mixed Methods Design Logic (Plano Clark &
Ivankova 2016)
The concurrent Quan + Qual design is where quantitative and qualitative strands are
undertaken concurrently or independently from each other with the purpose of comparing or
merging quantitative and qualitative results to produce more complete and validated
conclusions. Neither method has any priority over the other. Plano and Ivankova note that the
advantage of the concurrent Quan + Qual design is that it can produce well-validated and
substantiated findings, because concurrent strand implementation allows for obtaining
“different but complementary data on the same topic. However, it would be a lot to manage for
an MBA project as you are the sole researcher.
The sequential Quan → Qual design involves the undertaking of quantitative and qualitative
strands in sequence where the follow-up qualitative data is used to elaborate, explain, or
confirm the initial quantitative results. The results from the quantitative survey can also be
used to identify individuals for follow-up qualitative interviews and then the two sets of results
can be interpreted together so that the qualitative findings can provide a better understanding
of the initial quantitative result. The design of this approach means it is easier for one
researcher to undertake but it will take longer due to the sequential nature of the method.
The sequential Qual → Quan design involves the undertaking of qualitative and quantitative
strands in the sequence where the follow-up quantitative data is used to generalise, test, or
confirm initial qualitative results. Researchers can use the results from qualitative interviews to
inform the development and administration of a survey and then interpret the two sets of
results together the quantitative results can verify, confirm, or generalise the initial exploratory
qualitative findings. As with design 2, this approach means it is easier for one researcher to
undertake but it will take longer due to the sequential nature of the method.
