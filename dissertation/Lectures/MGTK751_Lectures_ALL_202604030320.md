Management Research
What is management research?
This work-based project is based on the notion of management research which is
described by Cameron (2021, page 485) as “a process of finding things out in a
purposeful and systematic fashion in order to increase knowledge”. In the context of a
work-based project this is likely to be your own and the specific organisation’s
knowledge but may also contribute to wider applied professional knowledge of the topic
or question you are focusing on.
Cameron suggests that management research can be characterised across three
dimensions from pure research to applied. The work-based project sits somewhere in
the middle of the continuums, it will focus on practice in terms of addressing an industry
or organisational issue, but it must be grounded in theory, it is unlikely to be
generalisable as it is small scale and focused, but there are likely to be themes and
learning that can be applied elsewhere, for example in similar organisations, finally as it
is focused on a real life problem it should be relevant but as real life situations are
complex then rigour in terms of all variable will be difficult. However, the research
process should be systematic, valid and reliable.

Management Research:
Other key points that need to be taken into account when undertaking management
research have been identified by Cameron and also Saunders et al.
First, it is transdisciplinary as it may draw on a range of disciplines or modules from
across the MBA programme, but what should also have become clear as you studied the
range of modules it that within many modules, they themselves have drawn on a range
of disciplines such as psychology, sociology and economics. This means there is likely to
be a vast amount of related research and literature that could underpin your project.
The key is to identify what is most relevant.
Second, management research has practical consequences – because the research is
applied it is likely to lead to some form of action or has an influence on individual or
organisational practice.
It also involves a reflexive process in that theory and practice inform one another, this is
often referred to as a virtuous circle of theory and practice where research on
managerial practice informs practically derived theory. Which then becomes a blueprint
for managerial practice, which increases practical management knowledge.
Consequently, management research must engage with both the world of theory and
the world of practice and problems addressed should develop from the interaction
between these two worlds rather than either on their own.
Finally, it requires organisational sponsors who need to be convinced of the potential use
of the findings – this could be you, your manager or your client organisation.

What is Philosophy?
The research onion
The research onion is a conceptual model developed by Saunders et al. (2012) that
represents the different layers of research design and methodology. The onion
metaphor suggests that the research process is layered and complex, with each layer
building upon the previous one to create a coherent and comprehensive research
design.
The layers of the onion are:
Research Philosophy: The first layer of the onion represents the researcher's underlying
beliefs and assumptions about the nature of reality and how knowledge is acquired. This
layer includes ontological and epistemological considerations, such as the researcher's
stance on objectivity and subjectivity, positivism or interpretivism, and realism or
relativism.
Research Approach: The second layer of the onion represents the broad approach to
research design and methodology, such as deductive or inductive, quantitative or
qualitative, or mixed methods. This layer includes decisions about the research design,
research question, and data collection methods.
Research Strategy: The third layer of the onion represents the specific research strategy
or plan, such as case study, experiment, survey, or ethnography. This layer includes
decisions about the sampling strategy, data collection methods, and data analysis
techniques.
Data Collection: The fourth layer of the onion represents the actual collection of data,
which may involve primary or secondary sources, and may be collected through
methods such as surveys, interviews, observation, or archival research.
Data Analysis: The fifth layer of the onion represents the analysis of data, which may
involve quantitative or qualitative techniques, such as statistical analysis, content
analysis, or thematic analysis.
1
Findings: The sixth and final layer of the onion represents the interpretation of the data
and the presentation of the findings, which may involve drawing conclusions, making
recommendations, or proposing further research.
Overall, the research onion provides a structured and systematic approach to research
design and methodology, helping researchers to ensure the validity and reliability of

Positivist
With regard to Ontology: the positivist approach is based on the ontological assumption
that there is "a measurable and objective reality that determines a universal truth"
(Andrews et al 2005:6).
Positivists argue that 'scientific' knowledge, based on 'facts' and 'laws', is the only true
form of knowledge and the only way to study human behaviour therefore is by using
scientific principles and methods.
The positivist Epistemology (which if you remember refers to how knowledge is
acquired) is "centred on data collection, objective distance between the researcher and
the subject, quantitative measurement, hypothesis testing and statistical analysis to
prove causality" (Andrews et al, 2005:6).
The positivist approach is founded on the strict avocation of objectivity. As Gratton and
Jones (2003) state. The whole research process should be objective, and no
measurements should be "subject to the influence of the researcher's values or
interpretation..the researcher has no influence on the findings, and has no personal
influence on the results" (Gratton and Jones, 2003:16). Thus, research should
be Replicable in that another individual could reach exactly the same conclusions based
on the precise evidence presented. Thus, the Truth can be objectively obtained through
the rigorous testing of hypotheses in terms of accepting or rejecting.

Interpretivist
The interpretative approach emerged fundamentally as a challenge to positivist
domination, adopting ontological and epistemological assumptions that opposed those
of positivism.
Ontological level -rather than suggesting that 'reality' or truth will be the same for
everyone (positivist ontology) -interpretative approach is founded upon the premise
that the social world is complex, that researchers and subjects are fundamentally and
subjectively attached to the world, and that people define their own realities
At the epistemological level, an interpretative approach prioritises "understanding the
particular behaviours, meanings and realities of individuals within particular social
settings" in order to acquire knowledge about the social world (Silk et al, 2005:6).
Interpretative researchers are interested in the experiences and viewpoints of their
research subjects. They seek to explore and interpret people's feelings, emotions,
behaviours and other intangible concepts which are too complex to be measured
numerically or reduced to numbers (Gratton and Jones, 2003:15).
Those adopting an interpretative approach see the merit in studying the social world in
its natural state rather than using strict, laboratory standards in an artificial and
unnatural environment (as positivists would advocate). Interpretative researchers
therefore promote qualitative methodologies "centred on observation, texts,
conversation, interpretation, narrative, writing, performance, and small-scale and local
interaction... a set of approaches to gathering knowledge of the social world that are
more fluid and flexible, and often emerge as the project unfolds" (Andrews et al, 2005:6).

Other philosophical approaches
As already mentioned, there are other philosophical approaches to research, including

Research 'paradigms'
It is worth noting that you may well come across the term research 'paradigm' in your
reading and this is a term closely associated with research philosophy. In fact some
academics refer to the philosophical approaches discussed above as the positivist
paradigm and the interpretative paradigm and the terms are often used
interchangeably, but there is a very subtle difference.
Whereas research philosophy essentially underpins the research process and refers to
underlying assumptions about how the world is perceived and how we can best
understand it, it could be argued that the research paradigm encompasses a more
holistic view of research. The concept of the paradigm was introduced to research
design by Thomas Kuhn (1962) and can be defined as "the entire constellation of beliefs,
values and techniques shared by the members of a given scientific community"
(Andrews et al., 2005:5).
Denzin and Lincoln (2000:19-22) summarise that the research paradigm involves the
interconnected issues of ontology (what is the nature of reality?), epistemology (what is
the relationship between the inquirer and the known?) and methodology (how can we
know the world or gain knowledge of it?). Similarly, Long (2007:196) explains that a
paradigm can be seen as "the framework of beliefs shared by a (research) community;
sharing through practice and writing produces common models of the world and how
things work".
1
Clearly research philosophies and research paradigms are inextricably linked, so much
so that, as mentioned, the terms are often used as substitutes. So do not be surprised if
one text discusses research philosophy with no mention of research paradigms, or vice

Purpose of Research questions
Formulating Research Questions
Formulating research questions is an essential part of any MBA business research
project, as it sets the foundation for the entire research process. Research questions help
to clarify the scope and purpose of the study, guide the selection of appropriate
research methods, and ensure that the research is focused and relevant.
Here are some tips for formulating research questions in MBA business research:
Start with a broad topic: Begin by selecting a general topic that you are interested in
exploring. This could be a problem or opportunity that you have identified in a particular
industry or business context.
Narrow down the focus: Once you have identified a broad topic, you need to narrow it
down to a specific research question. This will help to ensure that your research is
focused and manageable. Consider the following questions to help you narrow down
your focus:
• What specific aspect of the topic do you want to explore?
• What research questions are you trying to answer?
• What are the key variables or factors that you want to investigate?
Ensure the research question is relevant and important: It is important to ensure that
your research question is relevant and important to the business community. Think
about the practical implications of your research and how it could be applied in a real-
world context.
Ensure the research question is feasible: Make sure that your research question is
feasible and can be answered within the constraints of the research project. Consider
the available resources, time, and data that are needed to answer the question.
1
Use clear and concise language: Your research question should be clear and concise,
using language that is easy to understand. Avoid using technical jargon or overly
complex language that could confuse or alienate your audience.
Seek feedback: Once you have formulated your research question, seek feedback from
your peers, professors, or industry experts to ensure that it is relevant, important, and
feasible.
By following these tips, you can formulate research questions that are relevant, focused,
and feasible, and that will guide your MBA business research project towards meaningful
results.

Types of Research Questions
Again be conscious of the research philosophy and your approach. 

What is it you want to find out?
- Predicting an outcome: Does y happen under circumstances a and b?
- Explaining causes and consequences of a phenomenon: is y affected by x? What are the consequences of x?
- Evaluating a phenomenon: does y exhibit the benefits that it is claimed to have?
- Describing a phenomenon: what is y like or what forms does y assume?
- Developing good practice: how can we improve y?
- Empowerment: how can we enhance the lives of those we research?
Denscombe, 2010

Qualitative (Interpretivist ) RQ’s
- Usually start with ‘what’ or ‘how’ (avoid beginning qualitative questions with ‘why’ as this implies cause and effect).
- Identify the central phenomenon you plan to explore (tell in your question what you are going to describe, explore, generate, discover, understand).
- Avoid the use of quantitative words such as relate, influence, effect, cause.

With qualitative research, you will usually have one central question and possibly also some sub-questions to narrow the phenomenon under study further. The sub-questions will generally be more specific.
Qualitative questions often change in the research process (e.g., refined over time). 

Examples of qualitative research questions: 
- How do family business idiosyncrasies influence the formation of organisational path dependence and, in the case of a lock-in, path-breaking, as well as strategic outcomes? 
- How do couples negotiate their work and family responsibilities when they encounter a conflict between the two?
- What challenges do MBA students identify when learning qualitative research?

Quantitative (Positivism) RQ’s
Remember the positivist approach – you are testing, measuring and experimenting. 
You will usually have a set of hypothesis upon which you set out to test if they are true or not
Often informally referred to as a ‘top-down’ approach. The starting point is with a particular pre-determined theory which is narrowed down to a more specific hypothesis. Research is conducted in order to collect observations which can be used to test hypotheses. This then confirms or rejects the original theory. 

- What is the relationship between employee training and development programmes and employee retention rates?
- Does workplace diversity influence firm performance?
- What is the influence of supply chain management practices on firm performance and profitability?
- What is the relationship between entrepreneurial orientation and firm growth in small and medium-sized enterprises?

Criteria for Good Research Questions
Questions should be clear. They must be understandable to you and to others.
Questions should be researchable. They should be capable of developing into a
research design, so that data may be collected in relation to them. This means that
extremely abstract questions are unlikely to be suitable.
Questions should connect with established theory and research. This means that there
should be a literature you can draw on to help illuminate how your research questions
could be approached. Even if you find a topic that has been scarcely addressed by
business researchers, it is unlikely that there will be no relevant literature (for example, on
related or parallel topics or in other social science disciplines). Making connections to
theory and existing research will also allow you to show how you have made a
contribution to knowledge and understanding through your research.
Questions should be logically and conceptually linked to each other. This can involve
having an overall research question and two or three related subsidiary research
questions. All of the research questions posed need to be connected to a common
theoretical perspective or literature.
Questions should offer you the potential to contribute to knowledge/practice. They
should at the very least hold out the prospect of being able to make an original, novel
contribution—however small—to the topic.
Questions should be neither too broad nor too narrow. The research questions should
be neither too large (so that you would need a massive grant to study them) nor too
small (so that you cannot make a reasonably significant contribution to your area of
study).

What is MECE? And why use it
The MECE framework is a problem-solving framework that stands for "Mutually Exclusive,
Collectively Exhaustive." MECE is a principle that will sharpen your thinking and simplify complex
ideas into something that can be understood.
Mutually Exclusive – from probability theory to argue that two events cannot happen at the
same time –e.g. the flip of a coin or the role of a dice.
Collectively Exhaustive – the creation of a set of ideas that include all possible options.
Therefore one of the events must occur e.g. the role of the dice will be 1,2,3,4,5,6.
This is an entire range of possibilities that can be considered. It is commonly used in
management consulting, business strategy, and other fields to structure thinking and break
down complex problems into manageable parts.
The MECE framework works by dividing a problem into distinct categories that are mutually
exclusive (meaning they don't overlap) and collectively exhaustive (meaning they cover all
possible options). This helps to ensure that all aspects of the problem are covered, and that
there is no duplication or overlap in the analysis.
It is commonly used in management consulting, business strategy, and other fields to structure
thinking and break down complex problems into manageable parts. It allows you to take a
large amount of information simplify it into multiple groups of separate and distinct ideas. The
MECE framework works by dividing a problem into distinct categories that are mutually
exclusive (meaning they don't overlap) and collectively exhaustive (meaning they cover all
possible outcomes). This helps to ensure that all aspects of the problem are covered, and that
there are no duplications or overlap in the analysis. This tool would be useful in conducting an
investigative workshop or a variant of this approach which are discussed in a later lecturecast.
Here's an example of how the MECE framework might be applied:
Problem: A retail company is experiencing a decline in sales.
The MECE Framework would divide the issue into 3 categories
1
1)Product-related factors which would cover: Product quality 2 Product design Product pricing
Product availability
2)Marketing-related factors which would cover Advertising Promotions Social media presence
Brand reputation
3)Sales-related factors which would cover: Customer service Sales training Sales force
effectiveness Sales process efficiency
By breaking down the problem into three distinct categories, we can ensure that we have
covered all the possible factors that could be contributing to the decline in sales. We can then
further analyse each category to identify specific areas of improvement and develop a plan of
action to address the problem.
Overall, the MECE framework is a powerful tool for problem-solving and critical thinking, as it
helps to ensure that our analysis is comprehensive, structured, and focused.
Now its your turn. Let’s use a shopping list as a basic example – remember what is on the
shopping list. Don’t write anything down. You have 10 seconds.
What can you remember from the list? Write it down and see how many you can remember.
2
Using MECE as a guide we can take the list and try to create mutually exclusive groups, again
breaking this down into three mutually exclusive categories (typically) e.g.
Bakery Items: Bread, Muffin, Bagel
Frozen Food: lolly ice, ice cream, fish sticks
Fresh Fruit: strawberry, banana, grapes
This gives us 3 groups from the same list, distinctly different and separate. They are mutually
exclusive - they don’t overlap and they cover all nine items on the shopping list. This is
‘synthesis’ taking detailed information and summarising it into a higher level category.
3

Why use the MECE framework? 
It makes the information more memorable for you and the client – 3 things rather than 9 

More importantly, it is more persuasive to your client when you can present the three themes. People can process data more effectively when structured in threes. 

When presenting to a client they will not want a list, they will want ‘synthesis’. They will want to know where to focus and where they need to dig deeper. Take 1 key section at a time e.g.

Idea 1: Need to increase profit: Grow customers, Increase average spend per customer. Reduce fix costs  

It is a logical way of breaking down your organisational problem and thus finding categories upon which you can generate research questions and drive your literature searches. It can also help guide you towards the types of data and information you need to gather to build the research and or business case.

The framework offers an idea and is by no means perfect, we are human and thus we do need to use common sense.

The MECE framework can be used in a variety of situations beyond problem-solving. Here are some additional examples of how the MECE framework can be applied:

Market segmentation

Root cause analysis
–
The MECE framework can be used to identify the root causes of a problem by breaking down the problem into distinct categories and analysing each category in depth. This can help to identify the underlying factors that are contributing to the problem and develop targeted solutions to address them.

Process improvement
–
The MECE framework can be used to analyse a business process and identify areas for improvement. By breaking down the process into distinct categories, such as input, output, and controls, we can identify inefficiencies, redundancies, or other areas for improvement and develop a plan of action to streamline the process.

Decision-making
–
The MECE framework can be used to structure decision-making by breaking down the decision into distinct categories and evaluating each category separately. This can help to ensure that all factors are considered and that the decision is based on a comprehensive analysis.

Minto Pyramid Principle
The Minto Pyramid Principle is a methodology developed by Barbara Minto, a former McKinsey
consultant, for organizing and communicating ideas in a clear and structured manner. The
Minto Pyramid Principle is based on the idea that ideas should be presented in a logical and
hierarchical order, with the most important ideas at the top of the pyramid and supporting
ideas presented in a structured and organized manner below.
The Minto Pyramid Principle consists of three parts:
The Situation: The first part of the pyramid is the situation, which provides context for the ideas
being presented. This section should be concise and focused on the key facts and background
information that are relevant to the ideas being presented.
The Complication: The second part of the pyramid is the complication, which identifies the
problem or challenge that the ideas are designed to address. This section should be clear and
concise, and should clearly articulate the problem that needs to be solved.
The Solution: The third and final part of the pyramid is the solution, which presents the ideas
and recommendations for addressing the problem or challenge identified in the complication.
The solution should be structured in a logical and hierarchical order, with the most important
ideas presented first and supporting ideas presented in a structured and organized manner
below.
The Minto Pyramid Principle is designed to help communicators structure their ideas in a clear
and effective manner. By organizing ideas in a logical and hierarchical order, the Minto
Pyramid Principle can help to ensure that ideas are presented in a way that is easy to
understand, and that the key points are highlighted in a clear and concise manner. The Minto
Pyramid Principle is widely used in management consulting, business strategy, and other fields
where clear and effective communication is critical. This principal would be useful when
presenting your key findings to a senior board.

CLEAN AND INTUITIVE

The second benefit is that if your framework is MECE you can't miss any causes of the issue your client is experiencing because you will cover EVERYTHING. Clients are often worried that you will "miss something" and being MECE reassures them.

NO DUPLICATION OF WORK

In team situations it means that John can cover the US market, Julie can focus on Europe, Mike on Asia, etc. You can organise in a clear and transparent way which once again clients appreciate.

Inductive or Deductive 

INDUCTIVE

Inductive reasoning is a type of logical reasoning that involves drawing general conclusions based on specific observations or evidence. In this method of reasoning, the conclusions are not necessarily certain, but are based on the probability or likelihood of the observations being true.

Inductive reasoning involves moving from specific observations to broader generalizations or theories. It involves collecting data, analysing it, and then making conclusions based on patterns or trends that emerge. The conclusions reached through inductive reasoning are not necessarily true, but are considered likely based on the evidence gathered.

For example, if you observe several employees arriving late to work and then calling in sick later that day, you might make the generalization that these employees are taking advantage of the sick leave policy. This conclusion is not necessarily certain, but is based on the probability or likelihood of the observations being true.

Inductive reasoning is often used in scientific research, where researchers collect data and make observations in order to develop theories or hypotheses. This method of reasoning is also commonly used in everyday life, where people make generalisations based on their experiences and observations.

DEDUCTIVE

Deductive reasoning is a type of logical reasoning that involves drawing specific conclusions based on a general premise or set of premises. In this method of reasoning, the conclusions reached are certain, given that the premises are true.

Deductive reasoning involves moving from general principles or theories to specific conclusions. It involves starting with a general premise or set of premises, and then using logic and reasoning to draw specific conclusions from them.

For example, if the general premise is that "All men are mortal," and the specific premise is that "Socrates is a man," then the conclusion that can be deduced is that "Socrates is mortal." This conclusion is certain, given that the premise is true.

Deductive reasoning is often used in mathematics and logic, where it is used to prove or disprove a hypothesis. It is also commonly used in scientific research, where researchers use deductive reasoning to test hypotheses and draw conclusions based on the results of experiments or observations.

A literature review is a critical analysis and synthesis of the existing research and scholarly literature on a particular topic. It involves examining, summarising, and evaluating the key ideas and findings from a range of sources, such as academic journals, books, and other relevant publications.

An academic literature review is an analytical summary of published research studies, scholarly articles, books, and other sources relevant to a particular topic or research question.

The purpose of a literature review is to provide a summary and evaluation of the existing research and knowledge in a particular field or subject area.
A literature review typically involves a systematic and thorough search of databases and other sources to identify relevant publications. The review then involves reading and critically analysing the selected publications, with the goal of identifying key findings, trends, gaps in knowledge, and areas for further research.

The structure of a literature review can vary depending on the specific research question or topic, but it typically includes an introduction that explains the research question and purpose of the review, a section that describes the search strategy and selection criteria for the publications, a synthesis section that summarises the key findings and themes, and a conclusion that discusses the implications of the review for future research and practice.


Literature reviews are commonly found in academic research papers, dissertations, and theses, and are important for establishing the context and significance of the research being conducted.

Grey literature 
In some cases, given your topic, you may need to explore non-academic sources for the literature review. 

Grey literature refers to information that is not published commercially or indexed in traditional academic databases. This can include reports, working papers, policy documents, conference proceedings, and other materials that are produced by government agencies, non-profit organizations, and other non-commercial sources.

Grey literature can be an important source of information for researchers, as it often contains valuable data and analysis that is not available through traditional academic sources. However, because grey literature is not typically peer-reviewed or subject to the same quality control standards as published academic research, it is important to critically evaluate the sources and methods used in grey literature.
Some examples of grey literature sources include government reports, conference proceedings, technical reports, white papers, preprints, dissertations, and policy briefs. Criteria for evaluating sources of information
There are four criteria suggested by Easterby-Smith et al, (2015). 

Purpose: What is the purpose of this source? What are the motivations or interests that led to its creation? This is usually set out in the abstract of the paper or could be found on the organisations website

Authorship: Who created it? Who is responsible? Are they authoritative in this area?

Credibility and Accuracy: Why is it credible? Do they give enough information on the methods used and data upon which they make claims. Has the work been peer reviewed and taken up in academic journals? If not why might that be? Could there be an element of bias in the information given and how accurate is that information?

Timeliness: When was the information created? When was it last updated? Has anything changed within the field that means the work is no longer valid?

Types of literature review
There are several types of literature reviews that can be conducted in academic
research, depending on the research question and purpose of the review. Here are some
common types of literature reviews:
Systematic literature review: This type of review involves a thorough and comprehensive
search of published literature, using specific inclusion and exclusion criteria to identify
relevant studies. The studies are then critically evaluated and synthesized, with the goal
of identifying key themes, trends, and gaps in knowledge.
Narrative literature review: A narrative review is a more general overview of the
existing literature on a particular topic. Unlike a systematic review, a narrative review
is not based on a specific search strategy or set of inclusion and exclusion criteria.
Instead, it provides a broad overview of the literature on a particular topic, often with a
more subjective and interpretive approach.
Meta-analysis: A meta-analysis is a type of systematic review that uses statistical
methods to synthesize the results of multiple studies. The goal of a meta-analysis is to
identify patterns or trends across studies, and to provide a more quantitative
understanding of the findings.
Scoping review: A scoping review is a type of review that aims to identify and map the
existing literature on a particular topic. It is often used when a topic is new or under-
researched, or when there is a need to identify gaps in the existing literature.
Rapid review: A rapid review is a streamlined version of a systematic review, designed to
be completed quickly in response to a pressing research question or policy need. A rapid
review often involves fewer studies than a full systematic review, and may use less
rigorous inclusion and exclusion criteria.
Overall, the type of literature review chosen will depend on the research question, the
available resources, and the purpose of the review.

Venn Diagram – Key Words
A Venn diagram is a useful tool for visually representing the overlap and differences
between two or more sets of data. In the context of a literature review, a Venn diagram
can be used to compare and contrast the findings or ideas of different sources.
To create a Venn diagram for a literature review, follow these steps:
Determine the sources you want to compare: This may include two or more authors or
studies that you want to compare and contrast.
Identify the key themes or ideas in each source: As you read each source, identify the
main themes or ideas that are relevant to your research question.
Group the themes or ideas: Based on the key themes or ideas you have identified, group
them into categories that overlap or differ between the sources.
Create the Venn diagram: Using a diagramming tool, such as Microsoft PowerPoint or
Google Slides, create a Venn diagram with overlapping circles. Label each circle with the
name of the source you are comparing. Place the overlapping themes or ideas in the
area where the circles overlap, and the unique themes or ideas in the areas that do not
overlap.
Interpret the results: Analyse the Venn diagram to identify similarities and differences
between the sources. This will help you to develop a more nuanced understanding of the
topic and to identify gaps or areas of agreement in the existing literature.
Using a Venn diagram can help you to visually represent complex ideas and to identify
patterns and relationships between different sources. It can also be a useful tool for
organising your literature review and presenting your findings to others.

General steps to follow
Here are some general steps for conducting a literature review:
1. Define the research question: Before beginning the literature review, it is important to
define the research question or topic that you will be exploring. This will help to guide
your search and ensure that you are focused on relevant literature.
2. Develop a search strategy: Identify the databases and sources that you will use to
search for relevant literature. This may include academic databases, search engines,
and other sources such as grey literature repositories. Consider using keywords and
phrases that are relevant to your research question, and use Boolean operators (such as
AND, OR, and NOT) to refine your search.
3. Conduct the search: Use your search strategy to identify relevant literature and record
your search terms and sources. It is often helpful to use a reference management tool
(such as Sotero or EndNote) to organise and track your search results.
4. Evaluate the literature: Review the titles and abstracts of the literature that you have
identified and select those that are relevant to your research question. Read the full text
of these articles, taking notes on key findings, methods, and limitations.
5. Synthesise the literature: Analyse and synthesise the literature that you have reviewed,
identifying key themes, trends, and gaps in knowledge. Consider using a systematic
approach to organise your findings (such as a matrix or thematic map) and identify
areas where further research is needed.
6. Write the literature review: Summarise and discuss your findings in a clear and
organised manner, drawing conclusions and making recommendations based on the
literature that you have reviewed.
7. Revise and edit: Review and revise your literature review, ensuring that it is well-
organised, clearly written, and free from errors and inconsistencies. You may come back
to this several times over the lifecycle of the project to refine it and make it sharper and
better linked to the project.

Overall, conducting a literature review requires careful planning, thorough research, and
critical analysis of the literature. By following these steps, you can ensure that your literature
review is comprehensive, rigorous, and relevant to your research question.

Why is it difficult and time consuming?
Academic papers can be challenging to read for several reasons:
1. Technical language: Academic papers often use specialized technical language that
can be difficult for readers who are not familiar with the subject matter to understand.
2. Dense content: Academic papers are typically dense and packed with information,
making them challenging to read and comprehend.
3. Lengthy and complex sentences: Many academic papers use lengthy and complex
sentences that can be difficult to follow and understand.
4. Jargon and abbreviations: Academic papers often use jargon and abbreviations
specific to the field of study, which can be confusing for readers who are not familiar
with them.
5. Formal structure: Academic papers typically follow a formal structure and use
conventions such as abstracts, introductions, methods, results, and conclusions. This
structure can be difficult to navigate for readers who are not familiar with it.
6. Limited context: Academic papers often assume a certain level of prior knowledge and
provide limited context or background information on the topic, which can make it
challenging for readers to understand the significance and relevance of the research.
It is important to note that reading academic papers is a skill that can be developed
through practice and familiarity with the conventions and language of the particular
field of study. Researchers and educators can also take steps to make academic papers
more accessible to a wider audience by using plain language, clear and concise
sentences, and providing context and background information where necessary.

Characteristics of quantitative research methods
Arguably, quantitative methods have dominated management research and they are
generally associated with experimental and survey methods and data analysis
procedures that generates numerical data and uses statistical techniques. Saunders
(2019) provides a useful summary of the characteristics of quantitative research
methods, and we will explore these in more detail during this lecturecast.
However, you will see from this list that quantitative methods tend to relate to a positivist
philosophy of research which believes that there is an external, objective social reality
and the researcher is generally seen as independent from those being researched. It
tends to take a deductive approach where a theory is used to generate specific
propositions or hypotheses (Bryman and Bell 2008) and focuses on the 'what?', 'when?'
and 'how many?' questions., For example, often an aim of quantitative research is to
determine the relationship between one thing (an independent variable) and another (a
dependent variable) in a population. Thus, the theory may be that there are gender
differences with regard to participation in sport and the hypothesis generated is that
women are less likely to participate in sport than men.
In order to test this hypothesis quantitative date would then be collected through a
large-scale survey, the data from which would then be analysed using statistics and the
results would be used to support or reject the hypothesis or proposition.
1

Key Terms in Quantitative Research
Before we go on to the quantitative research design process there are a number of key terms that underpin this that it is important to be familiar with.  

- Variables: A measure of characteristics or behaviour patterns which may vary from one individual to another (e.g. age, place of birth, social class, strength, sex).
- Independent Variable: Presumed to explain the effect that is being researched.
- Dependent Variable: Can be explained by the effect of the independent variable. For example, the dependent variable could be sport participation and this is affected by the independent variable of age.
- Population: Can be very general (all human beings) or very narrow (all male, left-hand, vegetarians called Bob) but in either case, it's rare to have access to every member of a population.
- Sample: A small subset of the population. Used to infer things about the population as a whole. The bigger the sample the less likely it is to have sampling errors that mean that it does not reflect the behaviour of the population as a whole.
- Hypothesis: Statement of the relationship between two variables that can be empirically tested.

Hypothesis/Research Question(s)/Research Objective(s) 
Hypotheses tend to be used most in experimental methods and are defined by Smith (2010: 201) as an "informed speculation, which is set up to be tested, about the relationship between two or more variables".

 They are more common in the natural sciences than in the social sciences. In the social sciences research questions or objectives tend to be used, these are the overarching questions that focus on the topic and define the scope, scale and conduct of the research study. As highlighted in earlier sessions, it is crucial to have a research question or objective in order to ensure that your research is focused. 
You must have a clear and agreed research aim and objectives before you decide on a method or anything else for that matter. 

These can be slightly refined throughout the project but you need a direction!

Operationalisation of Concepts

Operationalisation involves defining concepts into variables that are measurable. This can be done in two ways: 
- first, using measures that have been developed in previous research, common ones include gender and social class; 
- second, we have to develop our own which may be based on informants from the particular group being researched and/or literature. 

There may well be debate and conflict about how a concept has been operationalised which results in review papers that attempt to pull together the research to try and identify how the concept can be conceptualised. It is important that you discuss such debates within your review of the literature in order to provide a justification for your measures.

This tends to be used when the population is a homogeneous group, and you have
access to them all. For example, if you were to undertake a survey on frequency of
participation of students at a university sport centre you could undertake a random
sample using the list of students attending the university. In order to do this, you would
use the following steps.
Steps in devising a simple random sample
• Define the population (N) - All students enrolled at the university.
• Select or devise a comprehensive sampling frame - The list of students enrolled
on university courses.
• Decide your sample size - In order to generalise beyond your sample group you
are likely to want to use statistical tests (which will be covered in session 5). At a
simple level, the larger the sample the more confident we can be that it is
representative of the population from which it is drawn. It is also important to take
into account the likelihood of non responses (see the next section for more
discussion on this).
• List all the individuals in the population and assign them consecutive numbers
from 1 to N
• Using a table of random numbers, or a computer programme that can generate
random numbers, select n different random numbers that lie between 1 and N
• The students to which the n random number refers constitute the sample
Systematic Sampling
This is a more commonly used method. Everyone in the sample frame is again allocated
numbers, the first person is generated randomly and then the subsequent participants
are chosen at a constant interval for example every 9th person.

• If the number 20 were generated select the individual who is allocated number 20

Research Design
A research design is the framework for the collections and analysis of data. There are a
number of different research designs that relate to quantitative research; prior to
outlining the different approaches it is important to examine two key criteria that are
used to assess if the research design is appropriate: reliability and validity.
Reliability is the extent to which a test or procedure produces similar results under
constant conditions on all occasions. In other words, is the research repeatable?
Validity refers to the extent to which the measure actually reflects the phenomenon
being researched. There are four key types of validity:
Measurement/construct validity relates to the extent to which a measure that is
devised to represent a concept really does reflect that concept, as many constructs that
we are investigating in research are not directly observable we use a hypothetical
construct to approximate what we are trying to observe. For example, occupations are
commonly used to indicate social class.
Internal validity: the level of confidence that the independent variable caused a change
in the dependent variable.
External validity: the extent to which the results of the study can be generalised beyond
the sample studied.
Ecological validity: the extent to which the findings are applicable to peoples' everyday
lives and natural social settings.
1

Classic Experimental Design (Bell et al, 2022) 
It is extremely unlikely that your MBA Project would be based on a classic experimental design but it is worth just providing a brief overview.

It is used to investigate whether a change in an independent variable has an effect on another dependent variable as shown in the diagram where two groups are created and in the experiment the independent variable is manipulated. The subjects are assigned randomly to their respective groups which means there would be confidence that any difference between the two groups is attributable to manipulation of the independent variable.

Experimental Group
So the experimental group receives the treatment, for example if looking at the impact of exercise on health then the experimental group would receive an exercise programme and it is compared against the control group, which does not.

Dependent Variable
The dependent variable of health is measured before and after the treatment
and a before-and-after analysis would be undertaken

Limitations
There are limitations to this approach in the social sciences: for example if experimental methods were used to examine the impact of sport on reducing crime then it would be difficult to set up treatment and control groups and it would be particularly difficult with regard to control for the other different variables that may also be impacting (Long,2017). Consequently scientific research designs are not common in management and business studies of recent.

Surveys as a research tool
Surveys are a common quantitative method in management research and could be an
appropriate tool for your project depending on your research aims and objectives. The
two main techniques are questionnaires and interviews. Questionnaires are usually
paper-and-pencil/online instruments that the respondent completes whilst Interview
based surveys are completed by the interviewer based on what the respondent says.
The advantages of questionnaires are that they are usually cheaper, quicker and
relatively anonymous. The disadvantages are that you can have a patchy response,
they might be incomplete or there is a frivolous response, therefore, more care is needed
in design. With regard to interviews the advantages are that there is more accuracy,
higher response rates, fuller and more complete answers and the design can be less
user-friendly. However, they can be more costly and there is less anonymity
Before deciding to undertake a survey there are a number of issues that you need to
consider. First, is it the best way to collect data with regard to the aims /research
questions of the study? Second, on a more practical level, it is important to identify
whether the population can be specified in order for you to then identify your sample.
You also need to think about whether or not the population is literate or if there are any
language issues, for example when researching children. Other practical administrative
issues include the cost of the survey in relation to the resources you have and also any
time constraints.
After considering any issues you need to consider how you will administer the survey as
identified in the introduction the main tools are respondent completion surveys, in the
form of questionnaires, and interviewer completion in the form of structured interviews.

Structured interviews
Structured interviews, hereafter termed interviews in this section, involve the
administration of an interview schedule by an interviewer.
They enable the interviewer to establish a rapport with the respondent which addresses
the issue of individuals who ignore self-completion questionnaires or who may not
answer sensitive questions. It also enables a trained interviewer to employ appropriate
techniques to help a respondent who has problems understanding or interpreting a
question without affecting the integrity of the interview. For example, research with
children. It also has the advantage of enabling standardisation both when asking
questions and recording the answers, this means that any variation in people's replies
will be 'true' and not caused by the interview context (Bryman and Bell 2007). The
interview context refers to all respondents being given exactly the same stimulus, in that
interviewees have to read out questions in exactly the same way and in the same order.
Thus, any variation between respondents occurs not because of how the question was
asked or how the answers were recorded but because of a difference in the respondents'
behaviour, attitude or whatever the question is trying to find out about.
Most structured interviews are similar to questionnaires in that they contain closed,
closed-ended, pre-coded or fixed-choice questions (Bell et al, 2022). Question types will
be discussed in a separate section. Open questions could be used where the respondent
has more scope in their reply; however, the problem with this in face-to-face interviews
is that the interviewer has to interpret the respondent's reply when writing it down.
There are three different approaches to structured interviews:
In-person: this is the most expensive and time-intensive option but does enable direct
face-to-face conversation enabling you to respond to both verbal and non-verbal cues.
Also, more complex questions may be asked in a face-to-face interview than in a
telephone interview as respondents can be shown cards with the complete set of
possible responses, making it easier for them to remember and consider all the choices.
In addition, more questions can be asked, 20 to 30 minutes is the usual limit for
telephone interviews whilst face-to-face interviews can be up to an hour.
1
Telephone interviews: these are obviously a lot cheaper and quicker to administer as it
saves the interviewer travelling between respondents. In addition, Bell et al(2022)
suggest that in personal interviews respondents' replies can be affected by the
characteristics of the interviewer or by them just being there.
There are several disadvantages to telephone interviews -
Not everyone has access to a telephone, this may be most likely in people from lower
socioeconomic households which could create a potential for sampling bias.
People may choose to be ex-directory.
It is not possible to see the body language of those undertaking the survey, eg if they
look confused.
Visual aids can't be used.
Computer-assisted interviewing (CAI): this could be used to support face-to-face
interviewing or over the telephone. In CATI, the interview is stored in a computer with the
questions displayed on the computer screen during the interview, and the interviewer
directly enters the responses into the computer.
2

Conducting Interviews (Bryman, 2016)
How to conduct Interviews?

Know the Schedule of Questions
You should be fully conversant with the schedule, if you are not confident in what you are doing this could affect interviewee variability in terms of how you ask the questions. 

Introducing the research
Prospective respondents are giving up valuable time to be involved in the research so you need to have a credible rationale in order to persuade them to take part. This is an often overlooked but is critical part of the research in terms of ensuring an appropriate response rate. The introduction is usually verbal and written and should include the following: 
- The identity of the interviewer
- The organisation that is overseeing the research
- What is research is broadly about
- Why the respondent has been selected
- Confidentiality issues
- How anonymity is ensured
- Opportunity for questions. 

Rapport
A relationship needs to be established that ensures that the respondent will continue with the interview, however this should not unduly lengthen the interview or affecting the respondent in terms of wanting to please the interviewer.

Asking Questions
It is extremely important that interviewers keep exactly to the wording and order of the questions to reduce variation.

Recording Answers
As with asking the questions the answers need to be accurately recorded. This is a key issue with more open questions as it requires the interviewer to provide some interpretation of the response.

Probing
This could cause problems as it introduces variability but it may be the respondent doesn't understand the question or gives an incomplete answer in which case a standardized probe could be used such as - 'could you say a little more about that?'

Prompts
All respondents should receive the same prompts. A show card with a list of prompts on is often used to reduce variability.

Structured interviews
Issues with structured interviewing
Compared to questionnaires, even using telephone interviews, structured interviewing
can be time consuming if the sample group is very large.
The format of structured interviews means it is difficult for the researcher to examine
complex issues and opinions. Even where open-ended questions are used, the depth of
answers the respondent can provide tend to be limited.
The presence of the researcher may influence the way a respondent answers various
questions, thereby biasing the responses. For example, Bell et al (2022) identified that the
ethnicity or gender of the interviewer could affect respondents in respect of certain topic
areas.
A problem common to both postal questionnaires and structured interviews is the fact
that by designing a "list of questions", a researcher has effectively decided - in advance
of collecting any data - the things they consider to be important and unimportant.
Social desirability - respondents may be affected in their response by the social
desirability of their answers. For example, respondents may be affected by the desire to
be helpful and friendly towards the interviewer, so in interviews about participation they
may exaggerate their interest or involvement. A key issue when considering carrying out
structured interviews for your own research project is whether you have the social skills
in terms of undertaking the interviewing.

Closed or Open questions?
Survey questions can be divided into closed and open questions. Closed questions
require people to choose from a set of provided response alternatives whilst open
questions invite people to respond in their own words.
Types of closed questions:
Likert scales This is where a statement is provided, and people are asked to indicate
how strongly they agree or disagree
Semantic Differential formats: Adjectives are chosen to represent the two extremes of a
continuum,
Checklists: Consist of a list of items and respondents are asked to circle each relevant
item. For example:
• Which skills did you develop on your degree (please circle all those appropriate):
• Written communication IT Skills
• Oral communication Research Skills
• Problem Solving Teamwork
Ranking: Respondents are again given a list of items, but they have to rank their
importance.
Attitude choices: This is where respondents are provided with a list of alternative views,
and they have to select that which is closest to their own. For example:
• Tick the option you favour?
• Students should pay full fees for university courses
• Students should pay some fee contribution for university courses
• Students should not pay any fee contribution for university courses
• Students should receive cost of living grants and pay no fees for university
courses.

Language and wording of questions
Questions need to be clear and unambiguous and put respondents at ease rather than
challenge or intimidate them (Brace 2008).
An example of an ambiguous question is:
How often do you take public transport?
• Often
• Sometimes
• Never
First, there may be confusion over what is meant by public transport, second what is
considered as often - every day, once a week?
Another problem to avoid when designing questions is double barrelled questions that
ask about two things, for example:
“How would you rate the facility's cleanliness and customer service?”
Clearly it would be difficult to tease out whether the respondent is thinking about
cleanliness or customer service when answering - one could be very good and the other
poor but you wouldn't know, also this would frustrate the respondent. It may seem
obvious not to include these types of questions but it is tempting when you are running
out of space on a questionnaire.
Leading or loaded questions can be another issue with poorly designed questionnaire;
they question that are phrased in such a way that they suggest to the respondent that
the researcher expects a certain answer.
Another common problem is using questions that include negatives as the respondent
might miss the fact that it is written in the negative or at least it can be confusing, an
example would be: “Do you think that cleaning staff should not get paid more than
receptionists?”
1
Question Order - it is important to be sensitive to the implications of how earlier
questions may affect the answers to later questions.
Early questions should be directly related to the research topic - thus age, social
background and other such questions should be left until the end.
The questions that are going to be of more interest to the respondent are best asked at
the start to gain their attention.
Questions that may be of a sensitive nature should be asked towards the end of the
interview.
Think about the flow of the questions, try to group those covering a similar topic area
and ask general questions before specific ones.

It is important to think about how you are going to analyse your questionnaire during the design phase. The first step in analysing the questionnaire is coding it, which basically means having a system to collate the answers together. You need to go through your questionnaire and assign a code for each question for example in the student questionnaire, with closed questions this can be done prior to distribution and you can unobtrusively include the code on the questionnaire. 

If it is an open question you will need to allocate the codes as they arise in the questionnaires when they are returned, you would do this by identifying the key themes that emerge and coding them appropriately. What are statistics?
In this lecturecast, we will examine some of the basic techniques for analysing
quantitative data, such as that obtained from surveys.
The first point is that you need to think about how you are going to analyse your data in
the planning stage of your project, not after you have completed your data collection,
this is because the decisions you make on the type and amount of data you collect will
have implications for the types of analysis that you use. A lot of people worry about
quantitative data analysis because of statistics, it is important to start with the point that
statistics are simply tools and techniques that are used to organise, summarise and
describe data that are quantitative or can be measure numerically; this includes
methods of drawing inferences and generalisations. Consequently they are there to turn
the data collected into information.
The key point is understanding which tests are most appropriate for your data, why they
are used and any limitations with the data and results. In terms of the mathematical
calculations there are packages such as the Statistical Package for the Social Sciences
(SPSS) that can perform the calculations.
There are two branches of Statistics, which we mention in your data analytics:
Descriptive Statistics: which are the methods used to describe general trends and
patterns in a data set including measures of central tendency and dispersion. This type
of analysis is termed univariate analysis as it involves the analysis of one variable at a
time
Inferential Statistics: Where ‘inferences’ are made about the population from a sample.
Can we rely on our findings? (Or how certain can we be that they are real!)
We will be covering both branches in this lecturecast.

Types of Variables
One of the things we need to be aware of before identifying what statistical tests we need to use and what are the types of variables we have. For example, has our questionnaire collected responses that are real numbers, such as age, or are they rank-ordered dichotomies in terms of yes/no or categories such as "every day" or "once a week"?  

In order to make sense of these and decide on the appropriate statistics that we would use we need to categorise them into a type of variable. 

Nominal variables
–
Comprise of categories that cannot be rank ordered (also known as categorical variables) e.g. sex (male/female), place of birth, course type, and work experience. Such variables must be mutually exclusive and the numbers assigned to them during coding have no numerical meaning

Ordinal variables
–
These are variables whose categories can be rank ordered but the distances between the categories are not equal across the range. for example Likert Scale ( completely disagree to completely agree)

Interval/ratio variables
–
These variables can measure the difference between values, for example, age if collected in the form of actual age rather than categories or actual income. This is the highest level of measurement which means that a number of different techniques can be applied. The difference between interval and ratio variables is that a ratio scale has a logical Zero point for example age or income, whilst interval scales do not have an absolute zero point - for example, temperature in f. 

Dichotomous variables
–
These variables contain data that only has two  categories such as yes/no

Colour is ‘Nominal’ data and to think of using a mean is nonsense! We know this because we are familiar with means. The next descriptive analysis is the Measure of Central Tendency
Very easy to understand and commonly used, but very useful in describing data
succinctly. This is a measure of the most typical value or central value in a frequency
distribution, there are three types:
Arithmetic mean: the sum of the scores divided by the total number of scores. This can
only be used with interval/ratio data and can be distorted by extreme values.
Median: the mid-point in a distribution of values and is not affected by extreme values. It
is found by arranging all the values in a distribution from the smallest to the largest and
finding the middle:
2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 14, 16, 18, 20, 21
The Mode: The value that occurs most frequently in a distribution, it isn't used very often
but is the only appropriate measure of central tendency for nominal data.
Why used any other than the mean?? The mean is vulnerable to outliers, so think of
income distribution in female executives. If you used the mean, there are now a number
of women in top executive roles that get paid a lot more than other women so that
would exert upwards pressure so it might be better to use the median or the mode.
There is still evidence women are paid less than men so if you put all executive pay of
men and women together you will skew the mean average greatly.

This measures the amount of variation or spread in a sample, which can be just as interesting as the typical value of a distribution; for example, two sets of data may have the same central tendency but have a different distribution of individual items. Inferential statistics are used to make inferences from our data to the population that we are studying that is the reasoning moves from specific facts about your sample to inferring more general, but tentative, conclusions about the population from which your sample was selected. An example would be drawing conclusions on the levels of participation in training and development by employees in call centres from a sample of 100 employees, from different sites across the companies UK footprint. Inferential statistics are based on the assumption that the sample has been obtained through probability sampling which if undertaken appropriately means that the likelihood the outcome observed was not as a result of chance but is a real observation. Inferential statistics determine the level of confidence that the result is due to the relationship/effect of one variable with another rather than chance and that the results of the test are real and repeatable and not just random.

Inferential Statistics
Hypothesis Testing
This process of assessing the statistical significance of findings from a sample is
significance testing or generally known as hypothesis testing. The purpose of testing a
hypothesis is to determine the accuracy of the statement.
Statistics enable us to judge whether the differences/relationships found in the data are
statistically significant or due to random fluctuations in the data.
"A hypothesis is a statement of, or a speculation about, the relationships among the
variables that you intend to study" (Saunders and Berman Brown 2007:73).
The first stage in the process is to develop a null hypothesis, Ho which says there is no
association between the characteristics, and an alternative hypothesis, H1 which states
that there is an association between the two. Statistics can then be used to accept or
reject the null hypothesis
Hypothesis testing can be directional or non-directional.
Non-directional means that the hypothesis simply states that there is a statistical
difference or association - these are called two-tailed tests.
Directional tests mean that the hypothesis has stated if the direction of association is
positive or negative - these are termed one-tailed tests
An example of a one-tailed test is that women are more likely to be paid less than men
in the IT sector.
Need to know if you need a one or two-tailed test for some stats tests.

Statistical Significance
Important in hypothesis testing is the notion of statistical significance which relates to whether a research finding is probably true and is not due to chance. When reporting statistics we turn this around, thus significance level indicates the probability of a result being due to chance.

It is normally accepted that a finding is real if the probability of it occurring by chance is less than 5 in 100. In other words, there is a 95% chance of it being true. 

To explain further: if you were to take a sample from a population 100 times and see a relationship/difference, by chance you would find the same ‘strength’ relationship/difference 5 times when there was actually NO relationship/difference.

Statistical significance is expressed by showing how likely something is of not being true. E.g. p<0.05

Relationships and Associations
So, let's start with relationships and associations, a key area for investigation for business
management research is the relationship between different variables. For example, is
there a relationship between the availability of capital and entrepreneurial start-ups?
Measures of association/correlation enable the investigation of relationships between
variables: variables are said to be associated/related if one variable differs in
accordance with another. However, it is important to note that measures of
association/correlation techniques will only show if there appears to be a relationship
not if there is causality, i.e. you cannot infer that one variable causes another.
There are a variety of statistical tests of association/correlation but, as with all statistical
tests, their use depends on the nature of the two variables being analysed in terms of
whether they are nominal, ordinal, interval/ratio or dichotomous and whether the data
has a normal distribution
Chi-Square: Non-parametric, nominal level variables.
Chi-square is a statistical test that is frequently used with questionnaire data and is
applied to contingency tables when both variables are nominal where it enables a
judgement to be made on the level of confidence we can have about there being a
relationship between the two variables. The premise behind Chi-Square is to compare
the table with what would be the expected results due to chance or probability. Sapsford
(2007:168) describes it as model-fitting "working out what the table would look like if
there was no association between the variables (if the null hypothesis were true) and
then assessing how different the observed figures are from the 'expected' ones". If there
is a sufficiently large difference this would suggest that there is a real difference in the
sample being studied.
Chi-square only tests whether or not there is a relationship or association between
categorical variables. In order to test the strength of the relationships correlation-like
measures such as the Phi coefficient or Cramer's V are used. In both cases, the strength
of the association ranges in value from 0 to +1.

A value of 0.5 indicates a moderate association
A value of zero indicates no association

Tests of Difference
Social science research often involves analysing differences or changes. This may be in
the form of trying to identify if two groups are different (differences in well-being
initiatives participation between men and women) or whether an intervention may have
caused a change (reduction in customer complaints after staff have undergone
customer service training). The purpose of tests of difference is to determine whether the
difference between the two groups or the difference in groups pre and post-intervention
is small enough to be due to chance or large enough to apply to the whole population. If
there is a large difference then it can be argued that the independent variable has
affected the dependent variable, although as with measures of association we can
never be 100% certain. As discussed in the introduction to this session the choice of
statistical test will depend on the nature of the data.
T Tests: parametric, interval/ratio data.
The simplest form of analysis of difference is to compare the means of the two groups to
determine if they are significantly different, the T test. There are two types of T test:
Independent samples T test - this is used to establish the difference between two groups
- for example, the level of participation in coaching by men and women
Paired sample T tests usually work with one sample to consider if there is a difference in
their behaviour in relation to different things (for example comparing the average
amount spent on hobbies compared to holidays) or a change in behaviour, attitude or
performance following an intervention. For example, whether there was a change in
customer complaints following staff customer care training.

As outlined by Gratton and Jones (2010) there are several different types of interview structure: 

STRUCTURED
Structured interviews are essentially a questionnaire read out to respondents, and are akin to that which we covered in the session on quantitative methods. However the interviewer is able to seek clarification of responses although they need to adhere rigidly to predetermined questions. Thus this approach is often used when there are multiple interviewers on a larger research project and is usually based on a deductive research design. 

SEMI-STRUCTURED
Semi-structured interviews use a standard set of questions or schedule, however, there is a flexible approach in that the interviewer can alter the sequence of the interview or probe with subsidiary questions in response to the answers that the participant might give, so it feels more like a conversation, but does require the interviewer to have good listening skills and by the end of the interview the schedule of topics or questions needs to be covered. Generally, the more experienced the interviewer the less structured the interview schedule is, whilst an inexperienced interviewer is likely to have the actual questions written down. This emphasises why it is important to pilot your interview as this is also about developing your own skills and confidence in interviewing. Semi structure interviews are often based on a research design that is a balance between deductive and inductive, in that theory has informed the approach but other key themes may be uncovered during the interview process.

UNSTRUCTURED
Unstructured interviews consist of very general ideas of topics or themes and the participant tends to lead the direction of the interview with further questions developing as the interview progresses so the participant is developing the areas that are important to them. These are usually based on an inductive research approach.

Investigative Workshops 
An investigative workshop is a collaborative event designed to explore a specific topic or problem through the collective efforts of a diverse group of participants. The primary goal of an investigative workshop is to generate new ideas, insights, and knowledge through active engagement and collaboration among the participants. 

Investigative workshops can take many forms, but they typically involve a mix of presentations, discussions, and small-group activities focused on a particular topic or problem. They may be organised by academic or research institutions, non-profit organisations, or private companies, and may include participants from a wide range of disciplines and backgrounds.

During an investigative workshop, participants may engage in activities such as brainstorming, problem-solving, data analysis, and prototyping. The goal is to encourage open dialogue and exchange of ideas, and to foster a collaborative and innovative environment where new ideas can emerge.

Overall, investigative workshops are an effective way to bring together diverse perspectives and expertise to address complex problems and generate new knowledge. They can be used in a variety of settings, from academic research to industry innovation, and can help to catalyse progress and change in a particular field.

Other varieties of this approach
There are several other terms that are used to describe the type of research that involves
collaborative problem-solving and innovation, such as:
1. Action research: This is a collaborative research method that involves participants working
together to identify and solve practical problems in a particular context.

data collection, analysis, and interpretation.
3. Design thinking: This is a problem-solving approach that involves a collaborative and
iterative process of empathising with users, defining problems, ideating potential solutions,
prototyping and testing those solutions, and implementing the best one.
4. User-centered design: This is an approach to design that involves understanding the needs
and experiences of users and designing solutions that meet their needs.
5. Co-creation: This is a process of collaborative creation in which stakeholders work together
to develop new ideas, products, or services.

are often used in business, social sciences, and other fields where practical problem-solving

Criteria for evaluating qualitative research

In the session on quantitative research, we highlighted how you can assess the quality of the research using the notion of reliability and validity. Although the approach is different, the terms are still applied by some in qualitative research. However, Guba and Lincoln (1994) two of the foremost writers on qualitative research proposed more specific criteria to assess quality – trustworthiness and authenticity – it is trustworthiness that is most widely used and we will focus on that. Trustworthiness is made up of four criteria: 

Credibility
–
Parallels internal validity and relates to acceptability to others. Therefore, it focuses on ensuring that the research is conducted in accordance with what is considered good practices and that the findings are shared with the participants to confirm the accurate understanding of their social world, which is termed respondent validation. Another approach to credibility is to be reflexive such as considering how your own beliefs and assumptions may have affected your work. 

Transferability
–
The extent to which the results can be transferred to other contexts or settings with other respondents. Guba and Lincoln (1994) suggest that a way of establishing transferability is to provide a dense description of the population studied by describing the demographics and geographic boundaries of the study. 

Dependability
–
Dependability relates to reliability and suggests that researchers should keep an audit trail of complete records of all phases of the research process and the decisions made with regard to: 
- The specific purpose of the study
- How and why the participants were selected for the study
- How the data was collected and how long collection lasted
- How the data was reduced or transformed for analysis
- Interpretation and presentation of the findings
- The techniques used to determine the credibility of the data

Confirmability
–
Confirmability relates to objectivity. It is recognised that achieving complete objectivity is impossible, as discussed earlier in the module within interpretivist research philosophies. However, the research should show that the researchers acted in good faith and made efforts to minimise the influence of personal values on the research and findings as much as possible. This involves being self-reflective. For example, taking notes on personal feelings, biases, and insights immediately after an interview.

Mixed Methods

The final approach we will look at is mixed methods which combines quantitative and qualitative research. Given the discussions regarding research philosophy that we covered in the second week of the module, you will probably realise that this approach can be seen as controversial by those who take a purely positivist or interpretivist perspective, however, we did note that a mixed methods approach is often used by pragmatists. The argument for using mixed methods is often based on the view that quantitative and qualitative methods have individual weaknesses and it makes sense to combine the two. Plana Clark and Ivankova define mixed methods as a research design in which researchers mix quantitative and qualitative methods in specific ways to address a research purpose” Plano Clark & Ivankova (2016).

It is important to note that there are truly mixed designs and quasi-mixed designs. Quasi designs are where researchers collect and analyse both quantitative and qualitative data but make no attempt to meaningfully mix the methods or results to address the research Objectives. Truly mixed methods involve the researcher mixing quantitative and qualitative methods to generate inferences grounded in both sets of results. (Plano Clark and Ivankova).

Mixed methods can be a Fixed design that is planned from the start or emergent in that the research does not produce the necessary information or there may be unexpected findings so another method is added. Given the word count, time and resource constraints, you are advised to avoid using mixed methods for your MBA Project.

Three Basic Mixed Methods Design Logic (Plano Clark &

The concurrent Quan + Qual design is where quantitative and qualitative strands are
undertaken concurrently or independently from each other with the purpose of comparing or
merging quantitative and qualitative results to produce more complete and validated
conclusions. Neither method has any priority over the other. Plano and Ivankova note that the
advantage of the concurrent Quan + Qual design is that it can produce well-validated and
substantiated findings, because concurrent strand implementation allows for obtaining
“different but complementary data on the same topic. However, it would be a lot to manage for
an MBA project as you are the sole researcher.
The sequential Quan → Qual design involves the undertaking of quantitative and qualitative
strands in sequence where the follow-up qualitative data is used to elaborate, explain, or
confirm the initial quantitative results. The results from the quantitative survey can also be
used to identify individuals for follow-up qualitative interviews and then the two sets of results
can be interpreted together so that the qualitative findings can provide a better understanding
of the initial quantitative result. The design of this approach means it is easier for one
researcher to undertake but it will take longer due to the sequential nature of the method.
The sequential Qual → Quan design involves the undertaking of qualitative and quantitative
strands in the sequence where the follow-up quantitative data is used to generalise, test, or
confirm initial qualitative results. Researchers can use the results from qualitative interviews to
inform the development and administration of a survey and then interpret the two sets of
results together the quantitative results can verify, confirm, or generalise the initial exploratory
qualitative findings. As with design 2, this approach means it is easier for one researcher to
undertake but it will take longer due to the sequential nature of the method.

Crafting a Conclusion
Crafting a strong conclusion is an important part of any written or spoken communication.
Here are some tips to help you craft a strong conclusion:
1. Summarise key points: A good conclusion should summarise the key points or ideas
presented in the communication. This helps the reader or audience remember the most
important aspects of your research message.
2. Provide closure: A conclusion should provide a sense of closure to the communication,
bringing the ideas or arguments to a logical end. This can help the reader or audience feel
satisfied with the communication and understand its overall purpose.
3. Offer insights: A conclusion should offer insights or perspectives on the topic, drawing from
the main points or ideas presented in the communication. This can help the reader or audience
understand the broader implications of the topic and why it matters.
4. Encourage action: A conclusion may encourage the reader or audience to take action based
on the insights or perspectives presented. This can help to motivate the reader or audience to
do something with the information they have received.
5. Be concise: A good conclusion should be concise and to the point. It should avoid repeating
information that has already been presented and focus on the most important insights or
perspectives.
6. Use language that is appropriate for the audience: The language used in the conclusion
should be appropriate for the audience and communication context. It should be clear,
concise, and free of jargon or technical terms that may be unfamiliar to the reader or
audience.

Stages of Crafting Recommendations

Click through the interaction below to see stages of crafting recommendations.

1 - Define the Problem
The first step in crafting recommendations is to clearly define the problem or issue you are addressing. What is the problem that needs to be solved? What are the underlying causes of the problem? What are the consequences of not addressing the problem? The better you can define the problem, the more targeted and effective your recommendations will be.

2 - Analyse the Data
Once you have defined the problem, you need to analyse the relevant data. This might include quantitative data, such as statistics or surveys, or qualitative data, such as interviews or case studies. You need to use the data to identify patterns, trends or insights that can inform your recommendations.

3 - Consider Alternatives
Before you make any recommendations, it's important to consider alternatives. What are the possible solutions to the problem? What are the pros and cons of each solution? By considering alternatives, you can ensure that your recommendations are well-informed and that you have considered all the available options.

4 - Prioritise Recommendations
Once you have identified potential solutions, you need to prioritise your recommendations. Which solutions are most likely to be effective? Which solutions are feasible given the resources available?
Which solutions are most important in addressing the underlying problem? By prioritising your recommendations, you can ensure that the recipient knows which actions to take first.

5 - Communicate Clearly
The final step in crafting recommendations is to communicate them clearly.
Recommendations should be written in clear, concise language that is easy to understand. Use bullet points or numbered lists to make the recommendations stand out. Provide concrete examples or case studies to illustrate your recommendations and make sure to explain why you are making each recommendation so that the recipient can understand the reasoning behind your suggestions.

Conclusion

In conclusion, crafting recommendations requires a systematic and analytical approach. By defining the problem, analysing the data, considering alternatives, prioritising recommendations and communicating clearly, you can create effective recommendations that can help others make better-informed decisions, solve problems or improve their performance.

Recommendations and Conclusions
Recommendations and conclusions are closely linked as both are important elements of any
analytical or research report.
Conclusions are the final thoughts or insights drawn from the analysis presented in the report.
They summarise the key findings and insights and provide an overall perspective on the
problem or issue at hand. Conclusions are typically presented at the end of the report and are
intended to provide closure and help the reader understand the significance of the analysis
presented.
Recommendations, on the other hand, are specific actions or steps that should be taken based
on the conclusions and analysis presented in the report. Recommendations are intended to
provide guidance and direction to the reader, and to help them apply the insights and findings
from the analysis in a practical way. Recommendations are typically presented after the
conclusions and are often structured as a set of actionable steps or suggestions that the
reader can implement.
In other words, conclusions are the "what" of the report, summarising the key findings and
insights, while recommendations are the "so what" of the report, providing guidance on what
actions should be taken based on those findings and insights.
While conclusions and recommendations are distinct elements of a report, they are closely
linked and should be developed in tandem. The conclusions provide the foundation for the
recommendations, and the recommendations should be based on the insights and findings
presented in the conclusions. By developing strong conclusions and recommendations that
are closely linked, you can provide a clear and actionable perspective on the problem or issue
at hand.

For example, a strong conclusion in a business research report can:

1 - Summarise key findings
A conclusion can summarise the most important findings of the research in a concise and clear manner. This can help business leaders understand the most critical aspects of the research and how it can be applied to their organisation.

2 - Offer Insights and Recommendations
A conclusion can offer insights and recommendations based on the research findings. This can help business leaders understand the potential implications of the research and make informed decisions
on how to proceed.

3 - Provide Guidance on Next Steps
A conclusion can provide guidance on what the business should do next based on
the research findings. This can help business leaders make decisions and take
actions that can lead to positive outcomes for the organisation.

4 - Encourage Action
A conclusion can encourage business leaders to take action based on the research findings. This can help ensure that the insights and recommendations presented in the research are implemented in a timely and effective manner.

Overall, a well-crafted conclusion in business research can provide valuable insights and guidance that can help organisations make informed decisions and achieve their goals.

Minto Pyramid
As a reminder, the Minto Pyramid Principle is a methodology developed by Barbara Minto, a
former McKinsey consultant for organising and communicating ideas in a clear and structured
manner. The Minto Pyramid Principle is based on the idea that ideas should be presented in a
logical and hierarchical order with the most important ideas at the top of the pyramid and
supporting ideas presented in a structured and organised manner below.

The Minto Pyramid Principle consists of three parts:

1 - The Situation:
The first part of the pyramid is the situation, which provides context for the ideas being presented. This section should be concise and focused on the key facts and background information that are relevant to the ideas being presented.

2 - The Complication:
The second part of the pyramid is the complication, which identifies the problem or challenge that the ideas are designed to address. This section should be clear and concise, and should clearly articulate the problem that needs to be solved.

3 - The Solution:
The third and final part of the pyramid is the solution, which presents the ideas and recommendations for addressing the problem or challenge identified in the complication. The solution should be structured in a logical and hierarchical order, with the most important ideas presented first and supporting ideas presented in a structured and organised manner below.
