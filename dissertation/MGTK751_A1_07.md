## 1. Introduction

In knowledge-based firms (KBFs), individual expertise constitutes the primary source of competitive advantage (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992). The strategic value of KBFs  derives from the voluntary exchange of tacit professional knowledge, rendering knowledge management (KM) a governance priority rather than a peripheral function.

Tall Poppy Syndrome (TPS), the social phenomenon whereby high-performing individuals  face peer marginalisation in response to their standout performance (Feather, 1989), represents  a direct threat to this priority. In KBFs, where knowledge sharing is an operational necessity,  a marginalised Tall Poppy (TP) may withdraw from collective knowledge contribution, converting interpersonal friction into a boardroom-level strategic concern and latent KM governance risk, whilst team cohesion erodes under performance disparity tensions that management rarely observes directly. Survey evidence confirms 87.3% of high-performing professionals report  peers undermining their achievements (Billan, 2023); in knowledge-intensive organisations, employee departures result in measurable losses of institutional knowledge, productivity,  and talent continuity (Banerjee et al., 2023), whilst disengagement costs approximately  US$10 trillion annually (Gallup, 2026).

Despite extensive literature on TPS in cultural psychology (Feather, 1989, 1994) and its documented presence in professional workplaces (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017;  Mancl and Penington, 2011), management-oriented application within KBF contexts remains  a literature gap. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon driven by group dynamics that transcend individual firm boundaries; studying it within a single organisation risks conflating findings with that firm’s idiosyncratic culture, limiting possibilities to isolate TPS as the  explanatory variable. A cross-firm design is, therefore, more methodologically appropriate, consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025).

The research aim is to investigate how TPS dynamics in KBFs generate team cohesion and knowledge governance challenges, surfacing management implications for practitioners. Four research objectives guide this project:
RO1. Critically review existing literature on TPS, team dynamics, and KM in KBFs
RO2. Explore how TPPs in KBFs perceive and experience TPS dynamics
RO3. Evaluate the organisational and interpersonal conditions that exacerbate or alleviate TPS
RO4. Formulate management recommendations addressing TPS-related KM and team cohesion risks

Two research questions frame the investigation:
RQ1. What conditions within KBFs exacerbate or alleviate TPS?
RQ2. How do team members in KBFs experience TPS-related team dynamics?

This study contributes academically by extending TPS studies beyond cultural psychology into management and KM domains, and practically by equipping managers with workforce-derived insights to address an under-recognised governance challenge.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1. TPS Origins & Workplace Manifestation

TPS was theorised by Feather (1989, 1994) as a culturally rooted disposition towards marginalising high achievers, manifesting as an ambivalent attitudinal pattern that simultaneously admires and socially distances the standout performer. Feather’s foundational studies are, however, attitudinal and culturally specific to Australian and New Zealand contexts; TPS-to-KBF extension hence constitutes theoretical inference this study is designed to test. TPS operates as a live professional constraint (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), expressed through informal exclusion, gossip, and systematic undermining. In KBFs, where individual intellectual performance constitutes the organisation’s core commercial offering, the structural conditions for TPS are amplified precisely because excellence is simultaneously  required and inherently comparative.
(110 words)

### 2.2. Strategic Stakes of Knowledge Management

The KBF construct draws from the Knowledge-Based View (KBV) of the firm  (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992), which positions knowledge as the primary source  of sustained competitive advantage. Grant (1996) identifies mutual recognition of individual  knowledge domains as a structural prerequisite for knowledge integration; peer marginalisation  is definitionally a withdrawal of such recognition, severing integration at its source. Management cannot diagnose TPS-driven KM erosion from above since the relevant knowledge is tacit  and laterally embedded, directly warranting peer-primary participant design (Grant, 1996);  peers occupy the only vantage point from which TPS dynamics are fully visible. For KBFs,  where tacit knowledge is both the primary product and the least coercible organisational asset, TPS converts interpersonal friction into a capability governance risk.
(121 words)

### 2.3. Team Dynamics & Peer Reactions to High Performance

Social Comparison Theory (SCT; Festinger, 1954) explains peer responses; since competence is  a group’s defining activity, performance disparity generates chronic uniformity pressure, such that  “a state of social quiescence is never reached” (Festinger, 1954, p.125). Whilst comparison with TP becomes threatening, peers redirect this tension as hostility or derogation, confirmed empirically as peer victimisation (Kim and Glomb, 2014). Meta-analytic evidence further establishes that upward social comparison produces envy and peer withdrawal (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021).  Team cohesion is a well-established antecedent of team performance (Grossman et al., 2022);  in KBFs where professional competence anchors both task interdependence and the primary  “realm of relevance” (Festinger, 1954, p.132), this mechanism is structurally predicted  and carries disproportionate capability consequences.
(119 words)

### 2.4. Knowledge-Sharing Behaviour & Conditions for Withdrawal

Social Exchange Theory (SET; Blau, 1964) frames knowledge sharing as a reciprocal exchange governed by unspecified obligations and trust. Whilst TPS manifests as social devaluation, perceived reciprocity breaks down and voluntary KM contribution may be reactively withdrawn. Cabrera and Cabrera (2002) establish that supportive social environments are the most critical  non-technological precondition for sustained voluntary knowledge sharing;  TPS systematically degrades this precondition.

Sung et al. (2024) note upward comparison typically promotes knowledge sharing  through benign envy; withdrawal becomes more probable amidst hostile marginalisation, activating a malicious envy pathway that makes contribution recalibration conditional but theoretically grounded. This study thus explores whether and under what conditions  TPS-driven hostile marginalisation reshapes knowledge-sharing behaviour in ways  that pose latent governance risks.
(118 words)

### 2.5. Literature Gap & Conceptual Framework

Existing TPS literature is concentrated in psychology1, with management-oriented application in KBF contexts largely absent. Peer-level informal dynamics remain an under-theorised frontier of knowledge governance; peer-level competitive dynamics are explicitly regarded as under-studied moderators of knowledge-sharing behaviour (Foss, Husted and Michailova, 2010; Wang and Noe, 2010). This study occupies the intersection of these gaps.

The provisional conceptual framework (Figure 1) synthesises four thematic domains.  Within this structure, SCT’s cessation-of-comparison mechanism creates the social conditions  under which SET’s reciprocity logic predicts withdrawal is more probable, with KBV establishing  the strategic stakes that render this dynamic governance-critical. RQ1 and RQ2 map onto 
the conditions and peer experience domains respectively, informing both data collection  and interpretation. Domains are connected through relational associations rather than  directional causation, consistent with interpretivist epistemology (Braun and Clarke, 2006). 
(131 words)

## 3. Research Methodology

### 3.1. Research Philosophy & Approach

This study adopts an interpretivist philosophy, grounded in a constructivist ontology and subjectivist epistemology. TPS dynamics, characterised by perception, social attribution,  and lived experience, cannot be meaningfully reduced to objective measurement alone.  An inductive, qualitative approach is hence appropriate, allowing investigation of peer experience without imposing confirmation biases on a phenomenon that is inherently socially constructed  and contextually variable (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2023).
(64 words)

### 3.2. Data Collection

Semi-structured interviews via Teams constitute the primary method, offering flexibility to  probe emergent themes whilst maintaining sufficient structure for cross-participant comparability.  TPS is embedded in interpersonal and contextual experience; semi-structured interviews permit participants to articulate observations within their own frames of reference, a design consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025).
(54 words)

### 3.3. Sampling

At least six TPPs will be recruited through purposive sampling. Adequacy is determined by  data saturation rather than headcount; Guest, Bunce and Johnson (2006) confirm empirically that core themes can emerge by six homogeneous interviews, whilst adequacy reflects data depth rather than participant count (Fusch and Ness, 2015; Braun and Clarke, 2006, p.87).

Participants must have worked alongside standout performers in KBFs and directly observed shifts in team dynamics, excluding individuals without direct exposure. Rigorous purposive screening inherently yields a smaller qualifying pool, reflecting methodological precision.

Supplementary interviews with TPs and managers will be attempted prior to analysis, aiming to total ten participants, though TPPs remain primary since peer accounts reveal behavioural patterns  not observable from vantage points as hierarchy limits disclosure. One-participant-per-organisation design will be practiced to isolate TPS dynamics from single-firm cultural idiosyncrasies.
(136 words)

### 3.4. Data Analysis

Thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke’s (2006) six-phase framework will be applied.  Coding will commence after the third interview and continue iteratively until theoretical saturation is reached. A dedicated coding node for disconfirming evidence, capturing instances of continued or increased knowledge sharing despite TPS conditions, will be maintained throughout analysis, providing analytical balance and guarding against asymmetric interpretation of  the TPS-to-KM withdrawal proposition.
(63 words)

### 3.5. Trustworthiness

Qualitative rigour will be governed through Shenton’s (2004) four trustworthiness criteria. Credibility will be maintained through systematic coding and member checking where achievable. Dependability will be supported through an audit trail of analytical decisions. Transferability is addressed through cross-firm sampling and rich contextual description; Shenton (2004) indicates that researchers can describe only the sending context, with applicability to other settings assessed by readers. Confirmability is supported by reflexivity, where the researcher’s sustained professional exposure to high-performer team environments is acknowledged as  an interpretive lens and disclosed throughout this section.
(89 words)

### 3.6. Feasibility & Limitations

The researcher’s professional network spans legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, technology, and public sector entities across 10⁺ countries, providing feasible access to  a diverse KBF sample. The cross-firm, one-participant-per-organisation design removes management-level consent requirements, with individual participant consent being sufficient.  The primary limitation is the small qualitative sample; analytical generalisation to theory  constitutes the appropriate standard for interpretivist enquiry.
(58 words)

### 3.7. Ethics

Standard UoL ethics approval will be sought prior to any data collection. Semi-structured  interview questions will operate at the professional observation level, examining team behaviour and collaboration patterns rather than personal emotional disclosure. Individual participant consent forms will be signed in advance, and all data will be stored on UoL OneDrive.  Data collection will not commence until written ethical approval has been received.
(63 words)
