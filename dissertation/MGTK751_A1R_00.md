## 1. Introduction

In knowledge-based firms (KBFs), individual expertise constitutes the primary source of competitive advantage (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992). The strategic architecture of KBFs (spanning mainly legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, IT, and R&D sectors) depends on the continuous integration of specialist knowledge distributed across individuals, rendering knowledge management (KM) a governance priority rather than a peripheral function. Any mechanism that disrupts this integration is thus not a mere interpersonal matter, but an organisation-level risk with measurable commercial consequences.

CYC is a management consulting firm (MCF) specialising in KM, advising KBF clients on the governance conditions that sustain or inhibit effective knowledge contribution[1]. Across its client engagements, CYC has identified a recurring phenomenon carrying direct implications for KM performance, namely Tall Poppy Syndrome (TPS). Theorised by Feather (1989, 1994) as the social disposition whereby high-performing individuals face peer marginalisation in response to their standout performance, TPS represents a threat to the social conditions on which voluntary knowledge sharing (KS) depends. In KBFs, where individual intellectual performance constitutes the organisation’s core commercial offering, TPS is amplified precisely because excellence is simultaneously required and inherently comparative. When a Tall Poppy (TP) is subjected to peer marginalisation, a reactive recalibration of knowledge contribution follows (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021); KS becomes a vulnerability rather than a reciprocal exchange, and the social trust underpinning effective KM erodes progressively. This mechanism converts peer-level interpersonal friction into a boardroom-level strategic concern and latent KM governance risk, particularly for KBFs whose competitive position is inextricable from the depth and availability of their practitioners’ knowledge.

The scale of this risk is evidenced across industry research. Survey data indicate that 87.3% of high-performing professionals report colleagues actively undermining their achievements (Billan, 2023), whilst disengagement costs approximately US$10 trillion[2] annually (Gallup, 2026). In knowledge-intensive organisations, employee departure results in measurable losses of institutional knowledge, productivity, and talent continuity (Banerjee et al., 2023). Internal knowledge is the single highest-rated innovation source across organisations, outranking any single external factor, including clients, suppliers, and competitors, yet employees routinely withhold innovative input when they feel disengaged or perceive their managerial environment as unreceptive (Beatson, 2024). Only 51% employees deem their managers as “good at seeking their views”; only 37% say they are “allowed to influence final decisions” (Young and Tong, 2025, p.29), exposing the management vacuum in which TPS dynamics operate without intervention. The Australian context compounds these conditions, with workplace stress as a driver of “unscheduled absence” cited by 57% employers, up from 42% in 2023; and 33% of employers reporting increased conflict issues in 2024 (AHRI, 2025, p.12). Additionally, 35% of organisations indicate their leaders as “not proficient” in team development and people management (AHRI, 2026, p.6). For KBFs, these deficiencies translate into an elevated exposure to TPS.

Notwithstanding extensive literature on TPS in cultural psychology (Feather, 1989, 1994) and its documented presence in professional workplaces (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), management-oriented application within KBF contexts remains a literature gap. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon driven by group dynamics that transcend individual firm boundaries; studying it within a single organisation risks conflating findings with that firm’s idiosyncratic culture, limiting possibilities to isolate TPS as the explanatory variable. A cross-firm sampling design is, therefore, more methodologically appropriate, consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025). CYC’s primary client base spans KBFs broadly across multiple sectors; the research participant pool is drawn from within this base, providing the cross-firm diversity this study requires.

Research Aim
To investigate how TPS dynamics in KBFs generate team cohesion and knowledge governance challenges, surfacing management implications for practitioners.

Research Questions
RQ1. What conditions within KBFs exacerbate or alleviate TPS?
RQ2. How do team members in KBFs experience TPS-related team dynamics?

Research Objectives
RO1. Critically review existing literature on TPS, team dynamics, and KM in KBFs.
RO2. Explore how TPs’ peers (TPPs) in KBFs perceive and experience TPS dynamics.
RO3. Evaluate the organisational and interpersonal conditions that exacerbate or alleviate TPS.
RO4. Formulate management recommendations addressing TPS-related KM and team cohesion risks.

This study contributes academically by extending TPS studies beyond cultural psychology into management and KM domains, and practically by equipping managers with workforce-derived insights to address an under-recognised governance challenge. For CYC, the findings will inform a cross-sector advisory framework supporting KBF clients in diagnosing and mitigating the knowledge governance risks associated with TPS.

## 2. Literature Review

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 TPS Foundation

TPS was theorised by Feather (1989) as a culturally rooted disposition towards marginalising high achievers, manifesting as an ambivalent attitudinal pattern in which admiration for standout performance coexists with a drive to devalue and socially distance the individual who embodies it. Empirically operationalised through the Tall Poppy Scale, Feather (1989) confirmed that satisfaction at a TP’s fall strengthens amongst individuals with higher conformity and security values, establishing value-conditioning as the structural substrate of TPS expression. Extending this framework, Feather (1994) positioned TPS as an equity-restoration mechanism, activated episodically by contextual pretexts rather than expressed as continuous overt hostility.

The social-psychological mechanism underpinning peer marginalisation is grounded in Social Comparison Theory (SCT; Festinger, 1954). Because professional competence constitutes the primary domain of comparison in workplace settings, performance disparity generates chronic uniformity pressure; in ability-based comparison, “a state of social quiescence is never reached” (Festinger, 1954, p. 125). When peer comparison becomes threatening, hostility and derogation replace direct evaluation, a mechanism documented across contemporary organisational settings (Matthews and Kelemen, 2025). Meta-analytic evidence further establishes that upward comparison reliably produces counterproductive behaviour, ostracism, and peer undermining (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021), whilst multilevel field research documents a direct pathway from high performance to peer envy to victimisation (Kim and Glomb, 2014). In professional workplaces, these dynamics are observable as a live constraint, expressed through gossip, informal exclusion, and systematic undermining of standout colleagues (Mancl and Penington, 2011). In Australian professional contexts, TPS attitudes are empirically associated with negative outcomes for leader effectiveness and subordinate commitment, motivation, and satisfaction, operating as an embedded cultural feature of organisational life rather than an individual-level aberration (Meng, Ashkanasy and Härtel, 2003).

---

### 2.2 TPS in KM

The knowledge-based view (KBV) of the firm holds that voluntary knowledge integration constitutes the primary source of sustained competitive advantage, as firms function to synthesise specialist knowledge distributed across individuals rather than to produce it independently (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992). Grant (1996) identifies mutual recognition of individual knowledge domains as the foundational precondition for knowledge integration; Kogut and Zander (1992) locate competitive advantage in the tacit know-how embedded within social cooperation regularities. Both mechanisms depend on the social conditions that TPS systematically degrades. Recent empirical evidence confirms these KBV propositions remain operative in contemporary knowledge-intensive business services, with knowledge sharing measurably predicting innovativeness and market efficiency and its suppression translating directly into competitive underperformance (Danko and Crhová, 2024).

Cabrera and Cabrera (2002, p. 704) identify as the more consequential prerequisite for voluntary knowledge sharing “a social environment which encourages or even enforces knowledge sharing”; TPS degrades exactly this precondition. Social Exchange Theory (SET; Blau, 1964) frames voluntary knowledge contribution as a reciprocal exchange governed by trust and unspecified obligation. When TPS manifests as social devaluation of a TP’s contributions, perceived reciprocity breaks down and voluntary knowledge contribution may be reactively withdrawn. Voluntary knowledge sharing, as a form of organisational citizenship behaviour, is further sensitive to perceived fairness; under perceived inequity, discretionary contributions are withheld (Podsakoff et al., 2000). The degree of withdrawal is not, however, predetermined. Upward social comparison primarily promotes knowledge sharing through benign envy; withdrawal becomes more probable when hostile peer marginalisation activates a malicious envy pathway, making contribution recalibration conditional rather than deterministic (Sung et al., 2024). Because the relevant dynamics are laterally embedded and inaccessible to managers by virtue of their hierarchical position, peer accounts provide the only vantage point from which TPS-driven KM erosion is fully visible (Grant, 1996).

### 2.3 TPS in KBF

KBFs, operating across legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, IT, and R&D sectors, are structurally distinguished by the centrality of individual expert performance to commercial output. This characteristic amplifies TPS risk in a distinctive way. For KBF teams, professional competence constitutes the primary axis of peer comparison, and Festinger’s (1954) Corollary VII B establishes that where an ability is “necessary or important for the life of the group”, the pressure towards uniformity is maximised and the derogation of standout performers carries disproportionate force. Matthews and Kelemen (2025) confirm that social dynamics comparisons in organisational settings produce outcomes as damaging as compensation or capability comparisons, with the acuity through which knowledge workers define identity via expertise rendering social comparison especially consequential. In knowledge-intensive workplaces, creative and proactive performance systematically triggers peer ostracism, with team-level envy functioning as the moderating condition that determines whether standout contribution produces exclusion (Breidenthal et al., 2020).

Team cohesion, understood as the interpersonal bonds sustaining collective performance, is a well-established antecedent of team outcomes (Grossman et al., 2022). In high-interdependence KBF environments, cohesion disruption carries amplified consequences, since task interdependence strengthens the cohesion-performance relationship such that its erosion produces proportionally greater performance degradation (Gully et al., 2012). TPS-driven marginalisation does not produce a single-period cohesion cost; rather, it initiates a self-reinforcing cycle in which reduced cohesion diminishes collective performance, intensifying the comparative pressure that drives further TPS behaviour (Mathieu et al., 2015). This self-reinforcing nature renders TPS-induced cohesion erosion a compounding governance risk that escalates without managerial intervention. The tacit knowledge most critical to KBF competitive positioning, embedded within informal social routines and collective problem-solving (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992), is precisely the knowledge most vulnerable to this spiral.

### 2.4 TPS in AU KBF

The academic foundations of TPS research are distinctly Australian. Feather’s (1989, 1994) foundational studies drew on South Australian samples, situating TPS within Australia’s historically egalitarian cultural norms, where collective equality standards constrain the social acceptability of standout individual achievement. Subsequent research has confirmed TPS as one of four distinctively Australian cultural dimensions of organisational life, with empirically documented effects on leader effectiveness and subordinate commitment, motivation, and satisfaction in Australian professional contexts (Meng, Ashkanasy and Härtel, 2003). TPS is, in other words, not merely culturally attributed in Australia but organisationally operative within it. For KBFs operating in this cultural context, this embeddedness translates into an elevated baseline exposure to the KM and team cohesion risks identified in §2.2 and §2.3, making the governance challenge examined in this study particularly acute across the Australian professional services sector, a context well represented within CYC’s broader KBF client base.

### 2.5 Literature Gap & Conceptual Framework

Existing TPS literature is concentrated in psychology[4], with management-oriented application in KBF contexts largely absent. Feather (1989) himself identified the need for “studies of tall poppies in actual situations” and cross-group replication, a call this study directly answers. Peer-level competitive dynamics are explicitly regarded as under-studied moderators of knowledge-sharing behaviour (Foss, Husted and Michailova, 2010; Wang and Noe, 2010). This study occupies the intersection of these gaps.

The provisional conceptual framework (Figure 1) synthesises four thematic domains. Within this structure, SCT’s cessation-of-comparison mechanism creates the social conditions under which SET’s reciprocity logic predicts withdrawal is more probable, with KBV establishing the strategic stakes that render this a governance-critical concern. RQ1 and RQ2 map onto the conditions and peer experience domains respectively, informing both data collection and interpretation. Domains connect through relational associations, not directional causation, consistent with interpretivist epistemology (Braun and Clarke, 2006).
