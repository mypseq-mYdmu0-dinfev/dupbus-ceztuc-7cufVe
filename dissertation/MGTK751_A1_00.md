## 1. Introduction

In knowledge-based firms (KBFs), including legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, information technology, and research and development organisations, individual expertise constitutes the primary source of competitive advantage (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992). The strategic value of KBFs derives from the voluntary exchange of tacit professional knowledge, making knowledge management (KM) a governance imperative rather than a peripheral function.

Tall Poppy Syndrome (TPS), the social phenomenon whereby high-performing individuals face peer marginalisation in response to their standout performance (Feather, 1989), represents a direct threat to this imperative. In KBFs, where knowledge sharing is an operational necessity, a marginalised Tall Poppy (TP) may withdraw from collective knowledge contribution, converting interpersonal friction into a latent KM governance risk; simultaneously, team cohesion erodes as collaborative norms break down under performance disparity tensions that management rarely observes directly. Survey evidence confirms that 87.3% of high-performing professionals report peers undermining their achievements (Billan, 2023); in knowledge-intensive organisations, employee departures result in measurable losses of institutional knowledge, productivity, and talent pipeline continuity (Banerjee et al., 2023), while disengagement is estimated to cost approximately US$10 trillion in lost productivity annually (Gallup, 2026).

Despite extensive scholarship on TPS in cultural psychology (Feather, 1989, 1994) and its documented presence in professional workplaces (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), its application as a management challenge within KBF contexts remains a literature gap. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon driven by group dynamics that transcend individual firm boundaries; studying it within a single organisation risks conflating findings with that firm's idiosyncratic culture, making it impossible to isolate TPS as the explanatory variable. A cross-firm design is therefore the only approach capable of addressing the management challenge authentically, and this rationale is consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025).

The research aim is to investigate how TPS dynamics in KBFs generate team cohesion and KM governance challenges, and to identify management implications for practitioners. Four objectives guide this project: to critically review literature on TPS, team dynamics, and KM in KBFs; to explore how TP peers (TPPs) perceive and experience TPS dynamics; to evaluate the conditions that exacerbate or alleviate TPS in KBF teams; and to develop management recommendations addressing TPS-related risks. RQ1 asks what conditions within KBFs exacerbate or alleviate TPS. RQ2 asks how team members in KBFs experience TPS-related team dynamics. This study contributes academically by extending TPS into management and KM research, and practically by equipping managers with peer-derived insight to address an under-recognised governance challenge.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1. TPS Origins and Workplace Manifestation

TPS was formally theorised by Feather (1989, 1994), who identified a culturally rooted disposition towards marginalising high achievers whose success appears disproportionate to perceived deservingness. Rooted in a tension between collectivist equality norms and individual achievement values, TPS manifests as an ambivalent attitudinal pattern that simultaneously admires and socially distances the standout performer. Subsequent organisational research confirms TPS operates as a live professional constraint (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), expressed through informal exclusion, gossip, and systematic undermining of high achievers' contributions. In professional service environments, where individual intellectual performance constitutes the organisation's core commercial offering, the structural conditions for TPS are amplified and its organisational costs are most acute.

*(113 words)*

### 2.2. Knowledge-Based Firms and the Strategic Stakes of Knowledge Management

The KBF construct draws from the Knowledge-Based View of the firm, advanced by Grant (1996) and Kogut and Zander (1992), which positions knowledge as the primary source of sustained competitive advantage. In KBFs, value creation depends on expertise application, professional collaboration, and the voluntary sharing of tacit knowledge. Grant (1996) identifies mutual recognition of individual knowledge domains as a structural prerequisite for knowledge integration; peer marginalisation of a TP is definitionally a withdrawal of this recognition, severing integration at its source. TPS therefore elevates from a team relations matter to a knowledge governance concern, converting interpersonal friction into a strategic capability risk.

*(102 words)*

### 2.3. Team Dynamics and Peer Reactions to High Performance

Social Comparison Theory (Festinger, 1954) provides a foundational account of peer responses to outstanding performance. In professional contexts, where competence is both the comparison currency and the group's defining activity, performance disparity generates chronic rather than episodic pressure towards uniformity; Festinger (1954, p. 125) observes that a state of social quiescence is never reached. Empirical research confirms this mechanism produces peer victimisation in high-performance settings (Kim and Glomb, 2014), with meta-analytic evidence establishing that upward social comparison reliably generates envy and peer withdrawal across organisational settings (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021). Team cohesion is a robust antecedent of team performance (Grossman et al., 2022); TPS-driven marginalisation directly degrades this antecedent in KBF environments where task interdependence is especially high.

*(109 words)*

### 2.4. Knowledge-Sharing Behaviour and Conditions for Withdrawal

Social Exchange Theory (Blau, 1964) frames knowledge sharing as a reciprocal exchange governed by unspecified obligations and trust. TPS disrupts perceived reciprocity through exclusion and peer hostility, creating conditions under which TPs may recalibrate knowledge contribution downward. Cabrera and Cabrera (2002) establish that a supportive social environment is the most critical non-technological precondition for sustained voluntary knowledge sharing; TPS degrades precisely this precondition.

Not all upward social comparison produces knowledge withdrawal, and this study does not assume withdrawal as a confirmed outcome. Sung et al. (2024) find that upward comparison typically promotes knowledge sharing through benign envy and learning-oriented goal responses; withdrawal becomes more probable only where TPS takes the form of hostile marginalisation, activating a malicious envy pathway that makes voluntary contribution recalibration a rational response. This study therefore proceeds from the position that TPS-driven hostile marginalisation may reshape knowledge-sharing behaviour in ways that generate latent governance risks, treating this as a conditional but theoretically grounded proposition warranting systematic empirical investigation.

*(162 words)*

### 2.5. Literature Gap and Conceptual Framework

Existing TPS scholarship is concentrated in cultural psychology and attitudinal research (Feather, 1989, 1994), with management-oriented applications in KBF contexts absent from the literature. Research on knowledge sharing identifies peer-level informal dynamics as an under-theorised frontier of knowledge governance (Foss, Husted and Michailova, 2010), while Wang and Noe (2010) explicitly identify peer-level competitive dynamics as an under-studied moderator of knowledge-sharing behaviour. This study occupies the intersection of these identified gaps.

The provisional conceptual framework (Figure 1) synthesises four thematic domains: TPS conditions within KBF environments; peer team experience of TPS dynamics; KM sharing behaviour; and team cohesion outcomes. RQ1 targets the conditions domain and RQ2 targets the peer experience domain; both will generate interview themes that populate the KM behaviour and cohesion domains following analysis, ensuring the framework directly informs both data collection and interpretation. Domains are connected through relational associations rather than directional causation, consistent with interpretivist epistemology and the qualitative convention of non-directional conceptual frameworks (Braun and Clarke, 2006). The framework will be refined following data analysis to reflect empirical patterns.

## 3. Research Methodology

### 3.1. Research Philosophy and Approach

This study adopts an interpretivist philosophy, grounded in a constructivist ontology and subjectivist epistemology. TPS dynamics, characterised by perception, social attribution, and lived professional experience, cannot be meaningfully reduced to objective measurement alone. An inductive, qualitative approach is therefore most appropriate, enabling investigation of peer experience within team environments without imposing predetermined hypotheses on a phenomenon that is inherently socially constructed and contextually variable (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2023).

*(69 words)*

### 3.2. Data Collection

Semi-structured interviews constitute the primary data collection method, affording flexibility to probe emergent themes whilst maintaining sufficient structure for cross-participant comparability. TPS dynamics are embedded in interpersonal and contextual experience; semi-structured interviews allow participants to articulate observations within their own professional frames of reference, a design aligned with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025). Interviews and transcription will be conducted via Microsoft Teams in accordance with University of Liverpool research integrity requirements.

*(74 words)*

### 3.3. Sampling

A minimum of six participants will be recruited through purposive sampling, targeting TPPs: non-managerial professionals in KBFs who have directly observed TPS dynamics within their teams. Adequacy is determined by data saturation rather than headcount. Guest, Bunce and Johnson (2006) identify saturation as the appropriate adequacy criterion for purposive qualitative sampling, confirming empirically that core themes can emerge by six interviews in purposive samples where participants share common selection criteria. Fusch and Ness (2015) observe that adequacy depends not on participant count per se but on data depth; a small sample does not preclude saturation, as what constitutes the sample matters more than its size. Braun and Clarke (2006, p. 87) further note that qualitative research uses smaller samples precisely because reading and re-reading data is time-intensive.

Participants must have worked alongside a standout performer and directly observed shifts in team dynamics or collaboration in that context. This rigorous selection criterion produces a purposive and relatively homogeneous participant profile with respect to the research domain, satisfying the conditions under which small-n saturation is warranted (Guest, Bunce and Johnson, 2006). Where access permits, supplementary interviews with TPs or managers will be sought as triangulation enrichment (Shenton, 2004). One participant per organisation will be recruited, avoiding organisational consent bottlenecks and isolating TPS dynamics from any single firm's cultural idiosyncrasies.

*(217 words)*

### 3.4. Data Analysis

Thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-phase framework will be applied. Coding will commence after the second or third interview and continue iteratively until theoretical saturation is reached. A dedicated coding node for disconfirming evidence, capturing instances of continued or increased knowledge sharing despite TPS conditions, will be maintained throughout analysis, providing analytical balance and guarding against asymmetric interpretation of the TPS-to-KM withdrawal proposition.

*(65 words)*

### 3.5. Trustworthiness

Qualitative rigour will be addressed through Shenton's (2004) four trustworthiness criteria: credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability. Credibility will be maintained through systematic coding and member checking where achievable. Dependability will be supported through an audit trail of analytical decisions. Transferability is addressed through cross-firm sampling and rich contextual description; Shenton (2004) notes that the researcher can describe only the sending context, with applicability to other settings assessed by the reader. Confirmability is addressed through researcher reflexivity: the researcher's sustained professional exposure to high-performer team environments is acknowledged as an interpretive lens and disclosed throughout this section.

*(96 words)*

### 3.6. Feasibility and Limitations

The researcher's professional network spans legal, accounting, consulting, technology, and public sector organisations across more than ten countries, providing realistic access to a diverse KBF sample. The cross-firm, one-participant-per-organisation design avoids consent bottlenecks, with individual participant consent being sufficient. The primary limitation is the small qualitative sample, which precludes statistical generalisation; analytical generalisation to theory constitutes the appropriate standard for interpretivist inquiry.

*(62 words)*

### 3.7. Ethics

Standard University of Liverpool ethics approval will be sought prior to any data collection. Interview questions will operate at the professional observation level, examining team behaviour and collaboration patterns rather than personal emotional disclosure. Individual participant consent forms will be completed in advance, and all data stored exclusively on University of Liverpool OneDrive. Data collection will not commence until written ethical approval has been received.
