## 1. Introduction

In knowledge-based firms (KBFs), including legal, accounting, consulting, engineering, information technology (IT), and research and development (R&D) organisations, individual expertise constitutes the primary source of competitive advantage (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992). The strategic value of KBFs derives from the voluntary exchange of tacit professional knowledge, rendering knowledge management (KM) a governance priority rather than a peripheral function.

Tall Poppy Syndrome (TPS), the social phenomenon whereby high-performing individuals face peer marginalisation in response to their standout performance (Feather, 1989), represents a direct threat to this priority. In KBFs, where knowledge sharing is an operational necessity, a marginalised Tall Poppy (TP) may withdraw from collective knowledge contribution, converting interpersonal friction into a latent KM governance risk, while team cohesion erodes under performance disparity tensions that management rarely observes directly. Survey evidence confirms 87.3% of high-performing professionals report peers undermining their achievements (Billan, 2023); in knowledge-intensive organisations, employee departures result in measurable losses of institutional knowledge, productivity, and talent continuity (Banerjee et al., 2023), while disengagement costs approximately US$10 trillion annually (Gallup, 2026).

Despite extensive literature on TPS in cultural psychology (Feather, 1989, 1994) and its documented presence in professional workplaces (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), management-oriented application within KBF contexts remains a literature gap. TPS is a supra-organisational phenomenon driven by group dynamics that transcend individual firm boundaries; studying it within a single organisation risks conflating findings with that firm’s idiosyncratic culture, making it impossible to isolate TPS as the explanatory variable. A cross-firm design is therefore a more methodologically appropriate approach, consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025).

The research aim is to investigate how TPS dynamics in KBFs generate team cohesion and knowledge governance challenges, exposing management implications for practitioners. Four research objectives guide this project:
RO1. Critically review existing literature on TPS, team dynamics, and KM in KBFs
RO2. Explore how TPPs in KBFs perceive and experience TPS dynamics
RO3. Evaluate the organisational and interpersonal conditions that exacerbate or alleviate TPS
RO4 - Develop management recommendations addressing TPS-related KM  and team cohesion risks

Two research questions frame the investigation:
RQ1. What conditions within KBFs exacerbate or alleviate TPS?
RQ2. How do team members in KBFs experience TPS-related team dynamics?

This study contributes academically by extending TPS into management and KM research, and practically by equipping managers with peer-derived insight to address an under-recognised governance challenge.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1. TPS Origins and Workplace Manifestation

TPS was formally theorised by Feather (1989, 1994) as a culturally rooted disposition towards marginalising high achievers, manifesting as an ambivalent attitudinal pattern that simultaneously admires and socially distances the standout performer. Feather’s foundational studies are, however, attitudinal and culturally specific to Australian and New Zealand contexts; extending TPS to KBF professional environments therefore constitutes a theoretical inference this study is designed to test. Subsequent research confirms TPS operates as a live professional constraint (Holmes, Marra and Lazzaro-Salazar, 2017; Mancl and Penington, 2011), expressed through informal exclusion, gossip, and systematic undermining. In KBFs, where individual intellectual performance constitutes the organisation’s core commercial offering, the structural conditions for TPS are amplified precisely because excellence is simultaneously required and inherently comparative.

(119 words)

### 2.2. Knowledge-Based Firms and the Strategic Stakes of Knowledge Management

The KBF construct draws from the Knowledge-Based View (KBV) of the firm (Grant, 1996; Kogut and Zander, 1992), which positions knowledge as the primary source of sustained competitive advantage. In KBFs, value creation depends on expertise application, professional collaboration, and the voluntary sharing of tacit knowledge. Grant (1996) identifies mutual recognition of individual knowledge domains as a structural prerequisite for knowledge integration; peer marginalisation of a TP is definitionally a withdrawal of that recognition, severing integration at its source. Grant (1996) further observes that management cannot diagnose TPS-driven KM erosion from above since the relevant knowledge is tacit and laterally embedded, directly warranting the peer-primary participant design; peers occupy the only vantage point from which TPS dynamics are fully visible. For KBFs, where tacit knowledge is both the primary product and the least coercible organisational asset, TPS converts interpersonal friction into a capability governance risk.

(145 words)

### 2.3. Team Dynamics and Peer Reactions to High Performance

Social Comparison Theory (SCT; Festinger, 1954) explains peer responses to outstanding performance; where competence is a group’s defining activity, performance disparity generates chronic uniformity pressure, such that a state of social quiescence is never reached (Festinger, 1954, p. 125). Where comparison with a TP becomes too threatening to sustain, peers redirect this tension as hostility or derogation, confirmed empirically as peer victimisation in high-performance settings (Kim and Glomb, 2014); meta-analytic evidence further establishes that upward social comparison reliably produces envy and peer withdrawal across organisational contexts (Li, Xu and Kwan, 2021). Team cohesion is a well-established antecedent of team performance (Grossman et al., 2022); in KBFs where professional competence anchors both task interdependence and the primary realm of comparison, this mechanism is structurally predicted and carries disproportionate capability consequences.

(129 words)

### 2.4. Knowledge-Sharing Behaviour and Conditions for Withdrawal

Social Exchange Theory (SET; Blau, 1964) frames knowledge sharing as a reciprocal exchange governed by unspecified obligations and trust; where TPS manifests as peer hostility and social devaluation, perceived reciprocity breaks down and voluntary KM contribution may be rationally withdrawn. Cabrera and Cabrera (2002) establish that a supportive social environment is the most critical non-technological precondition for sustained voluntary knowledge sharing; TPS systematically degrades this precondition.

Sung et al. (2024) find that upward comparison typically promotes knowledge sharing through benign envy; withdrawal becomes more probable specifically where TPS takes the form of hostile marginalisation, activating a malicious envy pathway. This study therefore investigates whether and under what conditions TPS-driven hostile marginalisation reshapes knowledge-sharing behaviour in ways that generate latent governance risks.

(121 words)

### 2.5. Literature Gap and Conceptual Framework

Existing TPS literature is concentrated in cultural psychology and attitudinal research (Feather, 1989, 1994), with management-oriented application in KBF contexts absent from the literature. Peer-level informal dynamics remain an under-theorised frontier of knowledge governance, and peer-level competitive dynamics are explicitly identified as under-studied moderators of knowledge-sharing behaviour (Foss, Husted and Michailova, 2010; Wang and Noe, 2010). This study occupies the intersection of these gaps.

The provisional conceptual framework (Figure 1) synthesises four thematic domains: TPS conditions within KBF environments; peer team experience of TPS dynamics; KM sharing behaviour; and team cohesion outcomes. Within this structure, SCT’s cessation-of-comparison mechanism creates the social conditions under which SET’s reciprocity logic predicts withdrawal is more probable, with KBV establishing the strategic stakes that render this dynamic governance-critical. RQ1 and RQ2 map onto the conditions and peer experience domains respectively, informing both data collection and interpretation. Domains are connected through relational associations rather than directional causation, consistent with interpretivist epistemology (Braun and Clarke, 2006). The framework will be refined following analysis to reflect empirical patterns.

## 3. Research Methodology

### 3.1. Research Philosophy and Approach

This study adopts an interpretivist philosophy, grounded in a constructivist ontology and subjectivist epistemology. TPS dynamics, characterised by perception, social attribution, and lived professional experience, cannot be meaningfully reduced to objective measurement alone. An inductive, qualitative approach is therefore most appropriate, allowing investigation of peer experience within team environments without imposing predetermined hypotheses on a phenomenon that is inherently socially constructed and contextually variable (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2023).

(69 words)

### 3.2. Data Collection

Semi-structured interviews constitute the primary data collection method, affording flexibility to probe emergent themes whilst maintaining sufficient structure for cross-participant comparability. TPS dynamics are embedded in interpersonal and contextual experience; semi-structured interviews allow participants to articulate observations within their own professional frames of reference, a design consistent with qualitative practice in knowledge-intensive organisational research (Thomas, 2025). Interviews and transcription will be conducted via Microsoft Teams in accordance with UoL research integrity requirements.

(72 words)

### 3.3. Sampling

A minimum of six TPPs will be recruited through purposive sampling as the primary participant group. Adequacy is determined by data saturation rather than headcount; Guest, Bunce and Johnson (2006) confirm empirically that core themes can emerge by six interviews in homogeneous purposive samples, whilst Fusch and Ness (2015) and Braun and Clarke (2006, p. 87) jointly observe that adequacy reflects data depth rather than participant count.

Participants must have worked alongside a standout performer in a KBF and directly observed shifts in team dynamics or collaboration in that context; those without direct team-level exposure do not meet the eligibility criterion. [Rigorous purposive screening inherently yields a smaller qualifying pool than convenience sampling would, reflecting methodological precision rather than limited access.]

Where access permits, supplementary interviews with TPs and managers will be sought prior to analysis, aiming to expand total participants towards approximately ten; TPPs will remain the primary analytical group throughout, since peer accounts reveal behavioural patterns not observable from management vantage points as hierarchical distance limits disclosure. One participant per organisation will be recruited, isolating TPS dynamics from single-firm cultural idiosyncrasies and removing the requirement for organisational consent.

(190 words)

### 3.4. Data Analysis

Thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke’s (2006) six-phase framework will be applied. Coding will commence after the second or third interview and continue iteratively until theoretical saturation is reached. A dedicated coding node for disconfirming evidence, capturing instances of continued or increased knowledge sharing despite TPS conditions, will be maintained throughout analysis, providing analytical balance and guarding against asymmetric interpretation of the TPS-to-KM withdrawal proposition.

(65 words)

### 3.5. Trustworthiness

Qualitative rigour will be addressed through Shenton’s (2004) four trustworthiness criteria. Credibility will be maintained through systematic coding and member checking where achievable. Dependability will be supported through an audit trail of analytical decisions. Transferability is addressed through cross-firm sampling and rich contextual description; Shenton (2004) notes that the researcher can describe only the sending context, with applicability to other settings assessed by the reader. Confirmability is addressed through researcher reflexivity; the researcher’s sustained professional exposure to high-performer team environments is acknowledged as an interpretive lens and disclosed throughout this section.

(91 words)

### 3.6. Feasibility and Limitations

The researcher’s professional network spans legal, accounting, consulting, technology, and public sector organisations across 10⁺ countries, providing realistic access to a diverse KBF sample. The cross-firm, one-participant-per-organisation design removes consent bottlenecks, with individual participant consent being sufficient. The primary limitation is the small qualitative sample; analytical generalisation to theory constitutes the appropriate standard for interpretivist inquiry.

(56 words)

### 3.7. Ethics

Standard UoL ethics approval will be sought prior to any data collection. Interview questions will operate at the professional observation level, examining team behaviour and collaboration patterns rather than personal emotional disclosure. Individual participant consent forms will be completed in advance, and all data stored exclusively on UoL OneDrive. Data collection will not commence until written ethical approval has been received.
