#### Core Argument

Positivist validity/reliability concepts cannot be applied directly to qualitative work. Shenton operationalises Guba's four trustworthiness criteria — credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability — into concrete researcher provisions, drawn from his own qualitative PhD. Paper is practitioner-facing, not theoretically novel; its value for us is procedural legitimation of our design choices.

---

#### The Four Criteria

**Credibility** (≈ internal validity; p. 64) — demonstrates findings represent a true picture of the phenomenon. Lincoln & Guba consider it the most important criterion. 14 provisions listed; those most relevant to A1–3:

- *Member checks* (p. 68) — Guba & Lincoln: "the single most important provision"; spot verification during/after data collection; participants verify transcripts and emerging theories; Van Maanen: analysis occurs in the field, not after
- *Negative case analysis* (p. 67) — refine hypothesis until all data instances, including outliers, are accounted for; directly maps to our disconfirming evidence coding discipline
- *Triangulation* (p. 65–66) — methods, informant types, sites; site triangulation across orgs reduces local idiosyncratic effects; methodological legitimation for cross-firm design as a credibility provision
- *Researcher background and experience* (p. 68) — Patton: researcher is the primary instrument; scrutineer trust in researcher equals procedural adequacy (Alkin et al.); relevant to positionality disclosure
- *Reflective commentary* (p. 68) — enables "progressive subjectivity" monitoring (Guba & Lincoln); researcher's developing constructions tracked throughout; informs results and methods analysis
- *Thick description* (p. 69) — contextual detail enables reader to assess whether findings "ring true"; supports typology construction

Less relevant provisions (honesty tactics, random sampling, iterative questioning, peer scrutiny, debriefing) noted but not extracted — re-upload if needed.

**Transferability** (≈ external validity; pp. 69–71) — not statistical generalisation. Researcher describes only the "sending context" (Lincoln & Guba; Firestone); reader determines applicability to their own "receiving context." Shenton explicitly questions whether "truly transferable results from a single study" is realistic — context is inherently particular. Required disclosure: org count/location, participant restrictions, sample size, methods, session count/length, data collection period (p. 70).

**Dependability** (≈ reliability; pp. 71–72) — exact replication is not the standard; phenomena change. Lincoln & Guba: credibility and dependability are closely tied — demonstrating credibility goes "some distance" toward ensuring the latter. Achieved via overlapping methods and in-depth methodological description enabling the study to serve as a "prototype model."

**Confirmability** (≈ objectivity; p. 72) — findings must emerge from informants, not researcher predispositions. Requires: disclosure of beliefs behind decisions; explanation of method choices; acknowledgement of weaknesses; discussion of preliminary theories not borne out by data. **Audit trail** — data-oriented (how data was gathered and processed) and theoretical (how research question gave rise to the study design); both may be represented diagrammatically.

---

#### Key Constructs

- **Guba's four criteria** — credibility/transferability/dependability/confirmability as qualitative equivalents to positivist standards (Guba 1981; Lincoln & Guba 1985)
- **Progressive subjectivity** (Guba & Lincoln 1989) — monitoring researcher's developing constructions; critical for credibility
- **Audit trail** — diagrammatic trace of research decisions; two types: data-oriented and theoretical
- **Member checks** — verification of data accuracy and emerging theories with participants
- **Negative case analysis** — hypotheses refined to account for all instances including disconfirming ones
- **"Sending context"** (Lincoln & Guba; Firestone) — researcher can describe only their own fieldwork context; transferability inferred by the reader

### 02 — Kim, E. and Glomb, T.M. (2014) 'Victimization of high performers: The roles of envy and work group identification', *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 99(4), pp. 619–634. DOI: 10.1037/a0035789.

#### Core Argument

High task performance is a **victim precipitation factor**: by triggering unfavourable upward social comparison among fellow group members, it generates **envy** — an affective state characterised by inferiority, hostility, and resentment — which in turn produces peer **victimisation** (interpersonal harming behaviours directed at the high performer). This mechanism is empirically confirmed across two studies in two cultural contexts. **Work group identification (WGI)** — the degree to which members merge self-concept with the group — moderates both stages of this envy mechanism, reducing the likelihood that performance disparity translates into peer harm.

The paper integrates three theoretical traditions: (1) **victim precipitation theory** (Curtis 1974; Schafer 1968), which holds that individual characteristics or behaviours can inadvertently instigate harmful responses; (2) **Social Comparison Theory / SCT** (Festinger 1954), which explains the evaluative threat that high performance poses to peers; and (3) **identity theory** (Brewer & Gardner 1996; Hogg 2000), which explains how group-level self-categorisation alters comparison processes and moral scope.

The paper explicitly frames findings as consistent with "tall poppy" syndrome (Feather 1994) — p.619 — making it one of the few peer-reviewed empirical studies that directly bridges SCT-based peer marginalisation research to TPS literature.

---

#### Theoretical Mechanisms — Detailed

**SCT → Envy pathway (p.620–621)**
- High performers attract upward comparison because: (a) performance is a self-relevant domain (competence = fundamental human need per Deci & Ryan 2002); (b) org reward systems make HP visibility high; (c) Festinger's "upward drive" means peers tend toward comparison with those close to but better than themselves
- Self-evaluation maintenance model (Tesser 1988): when another's success is in a self-relevant domain (task competence), *comparison* rather than *reflection* occurs → lowered self-evaluation
- Relative deprivation model (Crosby 1976, 1984): perception of what others have that one lacks motivates behaviour to reduce deprivation
- Envy selected over other negative affect states (depressive feelings, shame, hostility) because it uniquely combines *self-lacking* and *other-possessing* components (Smith 2000; Smith & Kim 2007, p.46) — making it the direct motivator of harming to restore self-evaluation

**Envy → Victimisation pathway (p.621)**
- Envious persons restore self-evaluation by *reducing the envied person's advantages* (Duffy et al. 2008; Vecchio 1995) — harming decreases envy-induced frustration (Fox & Spector 1999) and restores damaged self-esteem (Fein & Spencer 1997)
- Victimisation operationalised as self-perceived target of interpersonal aggression from coworkers (Aquino & Thau 2009)

**WGI moderates via two mechanisms (p.621–622)**
1. *Prevents envy from arising*: WGI shifts self-evaluation source from individual capability to group capability; high performers are reframed as contributors to shared group success → upward comparison becomes positive (intergroup rather than intragroup); experimental support from Brewer & Weber (1994) and Gardner et al. (2002)
2. *Deactivates harming even when envy does occur*: WGI expands the "circle of morality" (Opotow 1995) — high WGI members feel moral obligation toward all group members including HPs; perpetrators experience more guilt; Duffy et al. (2012) confirm WGI deactivates threat-oriented action tendencies by preventing moral disengagement

#### Key Results

**H1 — Task performance → Victimisation (both studies)**
- Study 1: task performance β=.11, p<.01; salary β=.03, p<.05; task performance statistically stronger predictor than salary
- Study 2: task performance γ=.28, p<.01 (Model 6, Table 4)
- H1 supported across US and Korean samples; preliminary cross-cultural generalisability

**H2 — Envy mediates task performance → Victimisation (Study 2)**
- Task performance → Envy: γ=.20, p<.01 (Model 2, Table 4)
- Envy → Victimisation: γ=.22, p<.01 (Model 7, Table 4)
- Task performance coefficient reduced from γ=.28 to γ=.24 after envy entered → *partial* mediation
- Bootstrapped 95% CI of indirect effect via envy: [.01, .07] — excludes zero → H2 supported

**H3 — WGI moderates envy mechanism (Study 2, Table 5)**
- First stage (task performance → envy): WGI interaction γ=−.09, p=.07 (first stage weakened at high WGI)
- Second stage (envy → victimisation): WGI interaction γ=−.11, p=.05 (second stage weakened at high WGI)
- Simple paths at *low* WGI: task performance→envy=.26**, envy→victimisation=.32*, indirect effect=.08*
- Simple paths at *high* WGI: task performance→envy=.08, envy→victimisation=.10†, indirect effect=.01† (effectively attenuated)
- Difference in indirect effects: −.08, p<.05 → H3 supported
- Note: WGI moderation confirmed in Korean collectivist sample; authors caution against assuming replication in individualistic cultures (p.629)

#### Named Constructs / Measures

- **Victim precipitation factor** — individual characteristic or behaviour that inadvertently instigates harmful reactions (Curtis 1974; Schafer 1968); task performance operationalised as such here
- **In-degree envy centrality** — sociomatrix-based aggregate of peer-reported envy directed at a focal actor; multisource, controls for group size differences (Wasserman & Faust 1994)
- **Work group identification (WGI)** — group-level construct; shared perception that members merge self with group; shifts frame of reference from individual to collective (Mael & Ashforth 1992 adapted)
- **Circle of morality** (Opotow 1995) — scope of moral obligation; WGI expands it to include high performers, inhibiting harming behaviours
- **Self-evaluation maintenance model** (Tesser 1988) — comparison vs. reflection processes; performance in self-relevant domains triggers comparison and lowered self-evaluation
- **Relative deprivation** (Crosby 1976, 1984) — perception of other-possession motivates behaviour to reduce perceived gap; converges with SCT in predicting envy

#### Flags

**(a) TPS→KM withdrawal link**
Kim & Glomb establish the micro-level mechanism (SCT → envy → victimisation) through which TPS-equivalent peer marginalisation occurs. They do NOT address KM outcomes. However, the paper is the closest quantitative empirical anchor for the claim that high performance precipitates peer harming, and it explicitly references TPS/Feather (1994). Critically, the paper establishes envy as *partial* mediator — the direct task performance → victimisation path remains significant — meaning envy alone does not fully explain marginalisation. This is important for our TPS framing: TPS involves additional attribution processes (perceived undeservingness, norm-violation perception) beyond envy per se; Kim & Glomb's envy mechanism is a necessary but not sufficient account of TPS. For the TPS→KM withdrawal argument: the victimisation established here creates the social exclusion context in which KM withdrawal becomes rational — use as the *mechanism preceding withdrawal*, not as evidence of withdrawal itself.

Counterargument/balance note for Enis' critical balance requirement: the paper shows victimisation effects are small-to-moderate (β=.11 in Study 1 — modest effect size); most variance in victimisation resides *within* work groups (95% in Study 1), not between them — suggesting strong situational and individual variation. WGI substantially attenuates the mechanism, meaning peer marginalisation of HPs is far from inevitable. Use to hedge deterministic TPS→KM withdrawal framing.

**(b) Sampling and saturation**
Study 2 multisource design (supervisor-rated performance, peer-rated envy via sociomatrix, self-reported victimisation) from 3 organisations, 67 work groups. Not directly relevant to justifying our n=6 qualitative sample. However, the multi-org design (3 organisations) is modest and validates cross-org comparative logic at small scale. The study also demonstrates that group-level constructs (WGI) can be meaningfully studied with small group sizes (M=3.67 members). These precedents do not directly defend qualitative saturation but establish that multi-org designs with limited units per source are defensible in this literature.

**(c) Conceptual framework**
**Direct contribution**: WGI as a group-level moderating condition maps precisely to our "conditions that alleviate TPS" domain (RQ1). The two-mechanism WGI model (prevents envy; deactivates harming) provides theoretically grounded mechanisms for recommendation development. **Framework linkage**: the task performance → envy → victimisation chain is the micro-level process underpinning TPS in our conceptual framework; it connects the TPS conditions domain to team cohesion outcomes. WGI-equivalent constructs (team identity strength, collective vs. individual orientation) should appear in the framework's "conditions" cluster. **For interview design**: WGI-related probes (how cohesive was the team? did the team identify more as "we" or as individual contributors?) are legitimate interview themes for Guide A.

#### Core Argument

TA is a method for identifying, analysing, and reporting patterns (themes) within data (p. 79). B&C argue TA is under-acknowledged as a named method despite being widely used, and that existing accounts either claim it as something else (grounded theory, DA) or report it without naming it. The paper's primary contribution is to demarcate TA clearly while preserving its flexibility — providing a vocabulary and process without constraining the method.

Key positioning claims:
- TA is theoretically independent: compatible with both essentialist/realist and constructionist paradigms (p. 81); can be inductive (data-driven) or theoretical (analyst-driven) (p. 83); can operate at semantic or latent levels (p. 84)
- TA should be considered a method in its own right, not merely a tool within other methods such as grounded theory (p. 78)
- TA is the recommended first qualitative method for researchers to learn — foundational skill applicable across other qualitative approaches (p. 78; citing Holloway & Todres, 2003)
- Themes do not "emerge" passively from data; the researcher actively constructs them: "if themes 'reside' anywhere, they reside in our heads from our thinking about our data and creating links as we understand them" (Ely et al., 1997: 205–6, cited p. 80)

The paper's primary practical output is the 6-phase framework (Table 1, p. 87) and the 15-point quality checklist (Table 2, p. 96).

---

#### Design Dimensions & Key Decisions

B&C identify three orthogonal design axes; our study's position on each must be stated in A3 Ch3:

**1. Inductive vs theoretical (pp. 83–84)**
- *Inductive*: themes emerge from data without a pre-existing coding frame; data-driven; closer to grounded theory; researcher does not code for theoretically anticipated patterns
- *Theoretical*: driven by researcher's analytic interest; more deductive; tends toward detailed analysis of one aspect rather than rich description across whole dataset
- **Our position:** Primarily theoretical — interview questions are guided by SET, SCT, TPS constructs — but with inductive openness to emergent themes; this is consistent with an interpretivist design that allows the data to surface unexpected patterns (including disconfirming evidence re KM withdrawal)

**2. Semantic vs latent (p. 84)**
- *Semantic*: themes at explicit/surface level; description progresses to interpretation; what participants say is the analytic starting point
- *Latent*: goes beyond semantic content to underlying ideas, assumptions, ideologies shaping the data; more constructionist; overlaps with DA
- **Our position:** Primarily semantic, with latent sensitivity for the constructionist dimension of how KBF norms shape what TPPs are willing to articulate about peer dynamics

**3. Essentialist/realist vs constructionist (pp. 85–86)**
- *Realist*: theorise motivations, experience, meaning straightforwardly; language reflects reality
- *Constructionist*: meaning is socially produced; TA theorises sociocultural contexts enabling individual accounts; cannot focus on individual psychology
- **Our position:** Interpretivist/constructionist — we are not treating TPP accounts as transparent reports of objective TPS events but as socially constructed narratives about peer dynamics within KBF contexts; consistent with our ontology (Concept Brief §1.4)

#### The 6-Phase Framework (Table 1, p. 87)

Canonical procedural reference for A1 §3.4 and A3 Ch3:

| Phase | Description |
|---|---|
| 1. Familiarise with data | Transcribe (if needed); read and re-read entire dataset; note initial ideas. Active, not passive reading. |
| 2. Generate initial codes | Code interesting features systematically across entire dataset; collate data relevant to each code. Code as many potential themes as possible; retain surrounding context; single extract can carry multiple codes. |
| 3. Search for themes | Sort codes into potential themes; collate all relevant coded extracts per candidate theme; use visual tools (mind maps, tables). |
| 4. Review themes | Level 1: check candidate themes against coded extracts for coherence. Level 2: check against entire dataset. Refine, merge, split, or discard. Patton's (1990) dual criteria: internal homogeneity + external heterogeneity. Re-read dataset for missed data. |
| 5. Define and name themes | Identify "essence" of each theme; write detailed analysis per theme; generate clear, concise, punchy theme names; check sub-themes; ensure no excessive overlap between themes. |
| 6. Produce the report | Final analysis and write-up; select vivid, compelling extracts; embed in analytic narrative that goes beyond description to argument; relate back to RQ and literature. |

Critical process notes from paper:
- Analysis is **recursive**, not linear — movement back and forth between phases is expected and necessary (p. 86)
- Coding begins when data collection begins and continues throughout — it is part of analysis, not prior to it (p. 88)
- Writing is integral to analysis, beginning at Phase 1 (p. 86) — do not defer write-up to Phase 6
- Phase 1 explicitly notes that qualitative research uses "far smaller samples" precisely because reading and re-reading data is time-consuming; skipping Phase 1 is strongly advised against (p. 87) — **directly usable for small-n sample defence**
- Avoiding "anecdotalism" (Bryman, 1988, cited p. 95): do not reify one or two instances into a theme; adequate evidence from across the dataset must support each theme
- Themes do not need quantified prevalence thresholds but prevalence must be considered consistently within any one analysis (p. 82–83)
- "Keyness" of a theme is not determined by frequency alone but by whether it captures something important in relation to the RQ (p. 82)

#### Pitfalls — Directly Relevant to Our Analysis Design (pp. 94–95)

Five named pitfalls; three are relevant operational risks for our study:

#### Core Argument

Firms exist not to minimise transaction costs (contra TCE/opportunism premise) but because they are superior to markets at sharing and transferring individual and social knowledge. The firm is a **social community** structured by higher-order **organising principles** — the recipes by which people coordinate, transfer technology, and create new knowledge. This positions the firm as a repository of capabilities determined by socially embedded knowledge, not a bundle of contracts.

Central claim: "what firms do better than markets is the sharing and transfer of the knowledge of individuals and groups within an organization" (p. 383). This knowledge consists of **information** (transmissible, declarative) and **know-how** (procedural, accumulated, embedded in social cooperation).

The paper introduces **combinative capability** — the firm's capacity to synthesise and apply current and acquired knowledge to generate new applications. This is the dynamic mechanism of innovation and growth, and the resource most at risk from social disruption.

---

#### Knowledge Taxonomy

**Information vs. Know-How** (pp. 386–387)

| Type | Character | Analogy | Transferability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Information | Declarative; facts, symbols, propositions; transmissible if syntactical rules known | Blueprint | Easier — codifiable, alienable from the individual |
| Know-how | Procedural; accumulated practical skill; defines current practice | Recipe | Harder — embedded in social interaction, partially tacit |

The distinction maps onto AI's declarative/procedural divide. Know-how is the content of a firm's competitive edge; information alone (org chart, accounting data) does not capture how the firm actually functions.

**Codifiability and Complexity** (pp. 387–388) — two dimensions governing knowledge transferability and imitability:
- *Codifiability*: degree to which knowledge can be structured into identifiable, communicable rules; coded knowledge becomes alienable from the holder but also more imitable
- *Complexity*: number of parameters required to define the system; inversely related to ease of transfer

Not all knowledge is amenable to codification — tacit know-how (Polanyi 1966) resists formal expression precisely because it consists of heuristic search rules that precede the articulable solution.

**Individual → Group → Organisation → Network** (Figure 2, p. 388) — knowledge operates at four nested levels:
- Individual: facts, skill, problem-solving heuristics
- Group: shared coding schemes, recipes (Taylorist methods, craft production), "who knows what"
- Organisation: higher-order organising principles that coordinate groups and enable knowledge transfer across functions
- Network: inter-firm cooperation protocols, transactional codes

This hierarchy is critical: when TPS operates, it disrupts the *group-level* layer — shared coding schemes, reciprocal knowledge flows, informal cooperation norms — which degrades upward integration into organisational and network knowledge.

**Tacit Knowledge** (pp. 388–389) — Polanyi's (1966, pp. 23–24) puzzle: individuals know more than they can express. Tacit knowledge consists of search rules and heuristics; the formal solution expression cannot fully capture the procedural know-how that generated it. In KBFs, the most strategically valuable knowledge (e.g., how to handle a complex client engagement, how to structure advice) is predominantly tacit and know-how-based.

**Combinative Capability** (pp. 391–392) — defined as "the intersection of the capability of the firm to exploit its knowledge and the unexplored potential of the technology" (p. 391). New learning is not created in abstraction but as recombinations of existing knowledge guided by current organising principles. Growth is path-dependent: what a firm has done before predicts what it can do next because knowledge advances through localised search within stable heuristics.

**The Replication Paradox** (pp. 390–391) — codifying knowledge for internal transfer simultaneously lowers barriers to external imitation. Firms face a structural dilemma: accelerating internal knowledge diffusion exposes them to competitive erosion. For KBFs, this is especially acute because know-how is the product itself.

**Inertness of Knowledge** (pp. 387, 396) — competitive differentials persist because knowledge is embedded in social relationships and organising principles, not in individuals alone. Hiring new workers does not transfer the firm's capabilities because know-how resides in the *regularities of cooperation*, not in individual heads. Switching capabilities is costly: "neither the knowledge embedded in the current relationships and principles is well understood, nor the social fabric required to support the new learning known" (p. 396).

---

#### TPS / KBF Connection (For A1/A3 Drafting)

K&Z do not discuss TPS or peer dynamics — this connection is constructed for our use, not cited from K&Z directly.

The strategic link: K&Z establish that the firm's competitive capability resides in the "regularities by which members cooperate in a social community" (p. 383). TPS erodes precisely these regularities — it disrupts group-level cooperation norms, degrades shared coding schemes, and breaks down the informal social fabric through which know-how is transmitted. The consequence is not merely interpersonal friction but degradation of **combinative capability**: the firm loses its ability to generate new knowledge from existing expertise, because the social cooperation that enables that recombination has been fractured.

This reframe elevates TPS from a "people not getting along" problem to a strategic capability-destruction mechanism — the boardroom-level framing required by the MBA management challenge angle.

Pairing with Nonaka (1994): K&Z provide the *static taxonomy* (what knowledge is; how it is held) and the *strategic stakes* (why social knowledge loss matters to the firm's competitive position). Nonaka provides the *dynamic creation model* (SECI: how knowledge moves between tacit and explicit, individual and collective). Together they constitute the full KM theoretical scaffold for A3 Ch2.

Pairing with Grant (1996): Grant argues knowledge is the primary source of sustained competitive advantage. K&Z explains *why*: because it is socially embedded, hard to imitate, and dependent on organising principles that competitors cannot easily replicate. TPS attacks the social embeddedness that makes knowledge competitively inert — turning an asset into a liability.
