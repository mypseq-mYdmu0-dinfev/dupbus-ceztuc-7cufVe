### RefRepo entry

### [Meng, Y.K., Ashkanasy, N.M. and Härtel, C.E.J. (2003) 'The effects of Australian tall poppy attitudes on American value based leadership theory', *International Journal of Value-Based Management*, 16(1), pp. 53–65. doi: 10.1023/A:1021984005070]
**Full text accessed (PDF)** | ABS ranking: Not listed (journal discontinued c.2004–05; niche management journal, Springer/Kluwer)
- Survey of 22 Queensland CEOs (from Queensland 400 largest businesses) and 149 CEO–subordinate relationships found that subordinate tall poppy attitudes were **significantly negatively related** to perceptions of value-based leader behaviour (B=−0.37, R²=0.14, p<0.10) and subordinate CEMS — commitment, effectiveness, motivation and satisfaction (B=−0.42, R²=0.18, p<0.10); even after accounting for leader motives and leadership style, TPS attitudes acted as a structural drag on core organisational outcomes in a professional setting.
- Drawing on Ashkanasy and Falkus (1997), the paper identifies TPS as one of four uniquely Australian cultural dimensions of leadership alongside mateship, 'one of us,' and underdog orientation — framing it as endemic to AU organisational culture rather than an individual-level attitude; argues that AU egalitarianism (low power distance, cf. Hofstede 1980) structurally conditions TPS emergence and constrains value-based leadership effectiveness.
- Limitation: N=22 CEOs is small and Queensland-specific (20 Brisbane, 2 Sydney); cross-sectional design limits causal inference; predominantly male sample (1 female CEO); IJVBM not ABS-ranked.
- **Placement:** §2.4 (primary) — provides the critical org/professional complement to Feather's attitudinal research: where Feather (1989, 1994) maps TPS as an attitudinal phenomenon, Meng et al. demonstrates it operating as a measurable negative force on professional org outcomes (CEMS) in an AU CEO context, grounding the KBF risk claim. Also §2.1 (secondary) — see Target 3.

### §2 usage note
Meng et al. (2003) belongs in §2.4 immediately after the Feather anchors. This directly bridges §2.4 to §2.2 (KBV/KM governance) by showing TPS is not merely a cultural curiosity but an active constraint on professional org performance.

## Target 2 — Recent Empirical KBV Pairing for §2.2

### Research notes
**Danko & Crhová (2024):** Full text read (open access). Explicitly adopts Grant (1996) KBV as theoretical frame. Empirically tests knowledge sharing → organisational performance in KIBS (n=237 Czech for-profit KIBS executives, PLS-SEM, two-round questionnaire). Tomas Bata University in Zlín. Three performance dimensions: innovativeness, market efficiency, financial performance. Knowledge sharing significantly predicts innovativeness (internal communication β=0.250, p<0.05; sharing as work duty β=0.147, p<0.05) and market efficiency (internal communication β=0.362, p<0.05); no significant effect on financial performance (R²=0.091). Weak overall R² (0.091–0.237). JKE = not in ABS 2021 list (unranked). **Confirmed as Target 2 entry.**

**Rehman et al. (2023):** Full text accessed (UoL access). Uses RBV (Barney 1991) not KBV (Grant 1996) as primary theoretical lens. Focuses on HPWS → Intellectual Capital development in PSFs via qualitative conceptual framework with empirical testing. Does not directly test tacit knowledge / knowledge integration → competitive advantage chain required by §2.2. Weaker fit than Danko & Crhová. **Rejected.**

**ABS ranking search:** Three independent G Scholar searches conducted in prior session ("knowledge-based view" "professional services" OR "knowledge-intensive" empirical 2018–2025; "knowledge-based view" "tacit knowledge" "competitive advantage" "professional services" OR "consulting" empirical 2015–2025; "knowledge sharing" "firm performance" "professional service" empirical 2018–2025). Neither a closer fit than Danko & Crhová for the KBV→KM→competitive advantage argument. JKE unranked noted as limitation per prompt instruction.

### RefRepo entry

### [Danko, L. and Crhová, Z. (2024) 'Rethinking the role of knowledge sharing on organisational performance in knowledge-intensive business services', *Journal of the Knowledge Economy*, 16, pp. 13873–13893. doi: 10.1007/s13132-024-02354-5]
**Open access, full text read** | ABS ranking: Not listed in ABS 2021 (JKE unranked — no ABS 2+ alternative found with equivalent KBV fit; noted as limitation)
- Explicitly grounded in Grant (1996) KBV — knowledge as the primary source of competitive advantage — and tests this empirically in KIBS (n=237, Czech Republic, PLS-SEM): knowledge sharing significantly enhances innovativeness (internal communication β=0.250, T=2.893; sharing as work duty β=0.147, T=2.106) and market efficiency (β=0.362, T=4.121), operationalising Grant's claim that knowledge integration and distribution drive competitive performance outcomes.
- Knowledge sharing between managers and employees (vertical + horizontal information flow) is the dominant competitive performance pathway; however, knowledge sharing has no significant effect on financial performance (R²=0.091), indicating that the KBV → competitive advantage chain is dimension-dependent — innovativeness and market positioning respond to knowledge flows whereas direct financial returns are mediated by additional organisational factors.
- Limitation: Weak overall R² (0.091–0.237); Czech Republic KIBS only — geographically limited; cross-sectional design; JKE not ABS-listed.
- **Placement:** §2.2 — pairs with Grant (1996) and Kogut & Zander (1992) as recent empirical evidence of continued KBV relevance: demonstrates that knowledge sharing is an active driver of competitive outcomes in KBFs (KIBS), substantiating the theoretical claim that KM governance failures (e.g. TPS-induced withdrawal from knowledge sharing) have measurable competitive consequences.

---

### §2 usage note
Danko & Crhová (2024) belongs in §2.2 immediately following the Grant (1996) / Kogut & Zander (1992) theoretical anchors, fulfilling markers' expectation of recent empirical validation. It demonstrates that Grant's KBV propositions remain empirically operative in contemporary KIBS: knowledge sharing measurably drives innovativeness and market efficiency. This positions the TPS → knowledge-sharing withdrawal chain in §2.4 not as a cultural observation but as a KBV-documented competitive risk — if TPS suppresses knowledge-sharing willingness (established via Meng et al. 2003 and Mancl & Penington 2011), the Grant (1996) framework predicts direct competitive performance degradation.
