**[Festinger, L., 1954]**
Title: A Theory of Social Comparison Processes
Journal / Publisher: Human Relations, 7(2), 117–140
DOI: 10.1177/001872675400700202
Access: ✅ FULL TEXT ACCESSIBLE — PDF/EPUB and Download PDF confirmed via UoL SAGE institutional proxy (journals-sagepub-com.liverpool.idm.oclc.org), April 2026. No abstract in the 1954 HTML page (pre-abstract era); full article in PDF.
Summary (2–4 sentences): Festinger's foundational paper proposes that individuals have a fundamental drive to evaluate their opinions and abilities; when objective physical standards are unavailable, they compare themselves to other people. Comparison is directed towards similar others; when a discrepancy exists, there is social pressure to reduce it — either through changing one's own position, changing others' positions, or ceasing comparison with the deviant. Nine hypotheses are derived, including that when a range of abilities/opinions exists in a group, social forces emerge to reduce the spread toward uniformity; deviation from the norm generates pressure toward conformity or rejection of the deviant. For the dissertation, this is the theoretical bedrock of social comparison theory (SCT): the "pressure toward uniformity" mechanism directly explains TPS peer dynamics — high performers' standout performance creates a discrepancy that peers experience as threatening, triggering social pressure to bring the outlier back to group level.
A1 section: lit theme 2 (SCT foundational)
Flag: FULL TEXT CONFIRMED ✅ via UoL SAGE proxy. 13,901 Crossref citations. Theoretical paper — no structured abstract (1954); 9 hypotheses derived from social-psychological theory. Note: the original SCT paper focuses on opinion/ability comparison in general social groups — subsequent work by Wood (1989), Suls & Wheeler (2000), and Matthews & Kelemen (2025) applies this to org contexts; cite Festinger for the theoretical foundation only.

## ACCURACY AUDIT — Full Text Access Status

**Why CrossRef API was used instead of publisher full-text sites:**
CrossRef API (api.crossref.org) returns structured metadata — title, authors, DOI, abstract, citation count, journal details — via a single URL fetch, with no paywalls, no bot detection, and no page-rendering delays. It was used as the primary verification method because: (a) it confirms DOI existence and bibliographic accuracy 100%; (b) abstracts are present for most post-1990 papers; (c) it avoids UoL library's SPA rendering issues and session timeouts; and (d) it is faster and more reliable than browser automation for metadata verification.

**What CrossRef/PsycNET provides vs. full text:**
- CrossRef: Title, authors, DOI, journal, volume/issue/page, publication year, citation count, abstract (where deposited). DOES NOT provide: full methodology, detailed findings, tables/figures, or full reference lists.
- PsycNET: Same as CrossRef but with APA record page and abstract. DOES NOT provide full text unless institutional access is confirmed.
- Publisher pages (ScienceDirect, SAGE, Wiley, INFORMS): Abstract page visible without login. Full text requires institutional subscription.

**The distinction:** Accuracy of bibliographic metadata (DOI, title, journal, year, authors) = 100% confirmed from CrossRef. Accuracy of the dissertation-relevant summary = based on abstract only, not full paper. Full-text-level accuracy requires accessing the complete paper via UoL library.
