- Two main stances: positivist vs. interpretivist (full comparison table)
- Research paradigm concept (Kuhn 1962)
- Pragmatism as third stance
- Ethics overview: standard approval, avoid vulnerable groups, LOREC = topic change signal
- Philosophy consistency rule (cannot mix stances arbitrarily)

## W1.3 — Forming a Research Aim and Objectives
- Topic selection criteria (Saunders: Appropriateness, Capability, Fulfilment)
- Research types: descriptive/exploratory, explanatory, evaluative (evaluative = most likely for MBA)
- Research aim and objectives (structure, SMART-ish criteria, Saunders' 6-point checklist)
- Research questions: purpose, formulation tips, types (qual vs. quant)
- ⭐ Qual RQ rule: start with "what"/"how" — never "why"
- Most common MBA approach = qualitative/interpretivist (with rationale)
- Job-sharing example showing how RQs are selected from broader questions
- Golden thread: aim → objectives → lit review → RQs → data → findings → recommendations

## W2.1 — Diagnosing Organisational Problems
- MECE framework: definition, Mutually Exclusive / Collectively Exhaustive
- MECE examples (restaurant profitability tree, Unilever, shopping list)
- MECE: ≤5 categories at L2; L2 ideas must summarise L3; can be applied to methods too
- Minto Pyramid Principle: Situation → Complication → Solution; top-down hierarchy
- Inductive vs. deductive reasoning (with examples)
- Research types mapped to problem certainty: exploratory (unaware) → descriptive (aware) → explanatory/prescriptive (clearly defined)
- 5-step client problem diagnosis process
- McKinsey case interview video (optional; MECE applied to consulting)

## W3.1 — Literature Reviews
- Definition and purpose of lit review (6 functions listed)
- Sources: academic journals, books, grey literature; Wikipedia never
- 4 evaluation criteria: purpose, authorship, credibility, timeliness (Easterby-Smith et al. 2015)
- Types of lit review: systematic, narrative, meta-analysis, scoping, rapid
- ⭐ Narrative lit review = most common for MBA final project
- Three-stage framework (Smallbone & Quinton 2011): search → deconstruct → reconstruct/synthesise
- Venn diagram for keyword search strategy
- Literature Review Framework table template (source, author, year, focus, method, country, findings)
- Hart's deconstruction checklist (6 areas for assessing articles)
- Bad lit review signs: poor search, limited scope, no critical analysis, inadequate synthesis, poor writing
- Structuring lit review: themes from MECE + objectives; each theme = sub-section
- Cyclical review process (Easterby-Smith diagram)

## W3.2 — Reading Academic Papers Workshop
- Hubbard & Dunbar (2017): career stage affects reading strategies; undergrads read linearly; seniors use targeted strategies
- Abstract + intro + conclusion first; then selective sections
- Section headings and opening sentences as navigation shortcuts
- Reading is a skill that improves with practice

## W4.1 — Quantitative Research Methods
- Characteristics of quant research (Bryman & Bell 2008): positivist, deductive, numerical, statistical
- Key terms: variables (independent/dependent), population, sample, hypothesis
- Research process for quant (Bryman & Cramer 2011 cyclical diagram)
- Operationalisation of concepts
- Sampling methods: probability (simple random, systematic, stratified, cluster) vs. non-probability (convenience, snowball, key informant, quota)
- ⭐ Sample size: min 30 for descriptive stats; 50+ preferred; 100+ ideal for inferential
- Response rates: 30% non-response = "quite good" (De Vaus 2014)
- Research designs: cross-sectional (most common), time series, longitudinal
- Survey tools: questionnaires vs. structured interviews (advantages/disadvantages)
- Questionnaire design: steps, layout, closed vs. open questions, Likert scales, coding
- Pilot testing: 2-stage process; critical before full survey

## W4.2 — Quantitative Analysis
- Descriptive statistics: frequency distribution, mean/median/mode, range, standard deviation
- Normal distribution and standard deviation (68-95-99 rule)
- Bivariate analysis: cross-tabulation for nominal/ordinal data
- Variable types: nominal, ordinal, interval/ratio, dichotomous (decision flowchart)
- Inferential statistics: making population inferences from sample
- Hypothesis testing: null (H0) vs. alternative (H1); significance level p<0.05
- Statistical tests: chi-square (nominal), Pearson/Spearman correlation (interval/ratio/ordinal), T-tests (parametric difference tests)
- Parametric vs. non-parametric distinction
- Tools: SPSS (UoL-licensed), Excel for basics; LinkedIn Learning SPSS training available

## W5.1 — Qualitative Research and Analysis (Part 1)
- Definition: exploring meaning, experience, behaviour, motivations (Creswell 2017)
- Methods covered: interviews, focus groups, investigative workshops, observation, discourse analysis
- Interviews: structured / semi-structured / unstructured (+ when to use each)
- 9 question types (Kvale 1996): introductory, follow-up, probing, specifying, direct, indirect, structuring, silence, interpreting
- Criteria for successful interviewer (Kvale + Bryman 2016 update)
- Varieties of literature on interviewing: elites, vulnerable groups, power/positionality considerations
- Interview advantages (9 listed) and disadvantages (5 listed)
- Focus groups: 5`~`10 ppts; group dynamics; 4 advantages, 6 disadvantages
- Investigative workshops: collaborative problem-solving; Van Aken et al. (2018); 5 advantages, 5 disadvantages
- Related methods: action research, participatory research, design thinking, co-creation

## W5.2 — Qualitative Data Analysis (Part 2)
- Stages of qual analysis (Miles & Huberman 1994): data reduction → display → conclusions/verification
- ⭐ Thematic analysis — Braun & Clarke (2006) 6 steps: familiarise → initial codes → search themes → review → define → write-up
- Data saturation: stop when no new themes emerge; must justify per literature
- NVivo: available at UoL; not mandatory; manual equally accepted
- ⭐ Only UoL-sanctioned software for transcription: MS Teams / MS Office / MS Copilot
- Trustworthiness criteria (Guba & Lincoln 1994): credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability
- Researcher reflexivity: acknowledge and manage bias; not a disqualifier
- Mixed methods: technically possible; 3 designs (concurrent Qual+Quant, sequential Quan→Qual, sequential Qual→Quan)
- ⭐ Module directive: avoid mixed methods; not enough time; supervisor will almost certainly push back

## W6.1 — Crafting Recommendations and Conclusions
- Recommendation = specific actionable advice based on evidence; not generic
- 5-step crafting process: define problem → analyse data → consider alternatives → prioritise → communicate clearly
- Why recommendations matter (5 reasons listed)
- Impact of poor recommendations (4 negative consequences)
- Conclusions = "what" (summary of findings); recommendations = "so what" (actionable steps)
- ⭐ Recommendations must be SMART; mix of short/medium/long-term
- Organisational research impact: positive (decision-making, efficiency, employee satisfaction) and negative (resistance, unintended consequences)

## W6.2 — Final Report, Presentation and Storytelling
- Full research process overview (Creswell & Plano-Clark 2010 cyclical diagram)
- Final report structure (executive summary ≤350w; Ch1`~`6 + references + appendices; word counts advisory exc. exec summary)
- Style requirements: Times New Roman or Arial 11`~`12pt; 1.5 spacing; Harvard referencing
- Minto Pyramid revisited: Situation → Complication → Solution; slide structure mapped to pyramid levels
- ⭐ A2 audience = senior management of client org, NOT academic audience; still Harvard referenced
- Storytelling in management: building rapport, communicating values, facilitating change
- Storytelling must be authentic, engaging, relevant; ethical implications acknowledged
