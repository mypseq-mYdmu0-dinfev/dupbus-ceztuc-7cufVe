## TPS in Professional & Org Settings

---

### Feather, N.T. (1994) 'Attitudes toward high achievers and reactions to their fall: Theory and research concerning tall poppies', in Zanna, M.P. (ed.) *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, vol. 26. San Diego: Academic Press, pp. 1–73. doi: 10.1016/S0065-2601(08)60151-3.

**Publisher summary only (pre-digitisation chapter; not digitally available)**

- 73-page authoritative synthesis of full TPS research programme to 1994; covers vignette/scenario studies, Tall Poppy Scale, and attitudes toward public figures; 199 Scopus citations
- Distinguishes TPS as response to poppies "viewed impersonally from a distance rather than personal rivals"; identifies deservingness, values (conformity/security), and self-esteem as moderators
- Preferred over Feather (1989) [CoreRef] for general TPS framing when full-text access to 1989 is not available; covers and extends the 1989 three-study design
- Note: Feather (1989) [CoreRef] = original three-study empirical design; Feather (1994) BJSP [CoreRef] = value/conformity basis; this chapter = full synthesis — three distinct roles across A1/A3
- A1 S2.1; A3 Ch2: supplementary TPS foundation; cite for broad TPS theoretical synthesis where word count permits additional citation beyond CoreRef entries

---

### Mancl, A.C. and Penington, B. (2011) 'Tall poppies in the workplace: Communication strategies used by envious others in response to successful women', *Qualitative Research Reports in Communication*, 12(1), pp. 79–86. doi: 10.1080/17459435.2011.601701.

**Full text confirmed**

- Focus group study (40 women); TPS behavioural expression via gossip, eye-rolling, undermining (passive-aggressive); "poppy clippers" use indirect tactics; high achievers self-deprecate to avoid targeting
- TPS as "mediocrity-maintaining mechanism" — bridges Feather's attitudinal theory to observable workplace behaviour
- Gender-specific; Midwest US; limited generalisability to mixed-gender KBF settings — use for TPS expression mechanisms only, not as primary empirical grounding
- A1 S2.1; A3 Ch2: empirical evidence of TPS behavioural expression in professional settings; cite alongside Holmes et al. (2017) for workplace TPS evidence cluster; note gender limitation

---

### Holmes, J., Marra, M. and Lazzaro-Salazar, M. (2017) 'Negotiating the tall poppy syndrome in New Zealand workplaces: Women leaders managing the challenge', *Gender and Language*, 11(1), pp. 1–29. doi: 10.1558/genl.31236.

**Full text confirmed**

- Sociolinguistic study; women leaders in NZ workplaces navigating TPS professionally; egalitarianism and gender order as structural constraints; professional women strategically manage identity to avoid targeting
- One of few peer-reviewed studies explicitly situating TPS within professional organisational contexts (not experimental/attitudinal) — confirms TPS operates as a live professional constraint
- Gender and leadership focus; NZ egalitarian cultural context; note limitations when citing in cross-cultural KBF framing
- A1 S2.1; A3 Ch2: professional-context TPS evidence; pair with Mancl & Penington (2011); use to establish TPS as an observable workplace phenomenon before pivoting to KBF-specific gap

---

### Sanchez, J.A.R. (2026) 'Effects of tall poppy syndrome on work performance: A multivariate regression analysis', *SEISENSE Business Review*, 6(1), pp. 1–19. doi: 10.33215/2dhagf18.

**Open access**

- Quantitative; multivariate regression (n=204, Philippine HEI); TPS measured against decision-making, problem-solving, creativity, internal service quality
- CONTRADICTS framing: TPS did not negatively affect most performance indicators; positive relationship with creativity found in this academic context
- Use as gap/tension material: TPS effects are context-dependent — strengthens the case for KBF-specific investigation where conditions differ from academic HEI settings
- SEISENSE not in ABS rankings; use as supplementary empirical illustration only; clearly flag Philippine HEI context when citing
- A1 S2.5 gap statement; A3 Ch2: disconfirming evidence node supporting context-dependence argument

---

### Dediu, I. (2015) *Tall Poppy Syndrome and Its Effect on Work Performance*. MSc dissertation, University of Canterbury. doi: 10.26021/8370.

**Open access (UC Research Repository)**

- Online survey (n=229); regression; 3 of 7 hypotheses confirmed; TPS environments → lower decision-making dependability and higher avoidance; internal service quality negatively affected
- NZ context; psychology discipline; MSc dissertation — not peer-reviewed journal article
- Borderline: currently superseded by Sanchez (2026) and Kim & Glomb (2014) [CoreRef] for empirical TPS-performance evidence; include only if no stronger source available
- A1/A3: supplementary background only if needed; do not cite as primary evidence

---

### Radike, M. (2025) 'Power dynamics or a race to the bottom? Bullying, harassment and tall poppy syndrome in radiology', *British Journal of Radiology*, Online First.

**Full text confirmed (library)**

- TPS observed in radiology professional setting; contextualises TPS as operating in professional workplace environments beyond Australasia
- Borderline: healthcare not KBF; narrow applicability; use only if additional professional-context TPS evidence needed beyond Holmes et al. (2017) and Mancl & Penington (2011)
- A3 Ch2: contextual background only; not recommended for A1

## High Performer Peer Dynamics & Envy

---

### Breidenthal, A.P., Liu, D., Bai, Y. and Mao, Y. (2020) 'The dark side of creativity: Coworker envy and ostracism as a response to employee creativity', *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 161, pp. 242–254. doi: 10.1016/j.obhdp.2020.08.001.

**Full text confirmed**

- Multi-phase time-lagged field study (engineers, high-tech firm, China); relative creativity → coworker envy (T2) → coworker ostracism (T3); SCT-grounded
- LMX moderates pathway: higher LMX accentuates envy; higher CWX attenuates it — relational context shapes whether standout performance triggers peer exclusion
- Closest empirical analogue to TPS peer-marginalisation mechanism in knowledge work; forms coherent empirical chain with Mao et al. (2021) and Liu et al. (2024)
- Limitation: China-based manufacturing; generalisability to professional services KBFs requires explicit acknowledgement
- A1 S2.1/S2.2; A3 Ch2: primary OB empirical anchor for envy → peer marginalisation chain; cite alongside Kim & Glomb (2014) [CoreRef]

---

### Mao, Y., He, J. and Yang, D. (2021) 'The dark sides of engaging in creative processes: Coworker envy, workplace ostracism, and incivility', *Asia Pacific Journal of Management*, 38, pp. 1261–1281. doi: 10.1007/s10490-020-09707-z.

**Full text confirmed**

- Three-wave supervisor-subordinate matched study (China); creative process engagement → coworker envy → workplace ostracism and incivility; SCT-grounded
- Key moderator: helping behaviour attenuates the creativity → envy link at high levels — prosocial engagement can interrupt the TPS-equivalent mechanism; actionable for managerial recommendations
- Directly complements Breidenthal et al. (2020); together form two-study empirical chain for envy-driven peer exclusion
- China-based; creative-process context rather than broad knowledge-work performance
- A1 S2.2; A3 Ch2/Ch5: pair with Breidenthal et al. (2020); use moderator finding for prosocial buffering in recommendations

---

### Liu, C., Peng, Y., Xu, S. and Azeem, M.U. (2024) 'Proactive employees perceive coworker ostracism: The moderating effect of team envy and the behavioral outcome of production deviance', *Journal of Occupational Health Psychology*, 29(6), pp. 445–459. doi: 10.1037/ocp0000389.

**Full text confirmed**

- Multilevel field study (n=630, 131 teams, multi-industry including consulting, healthcare, finance; China); task proactivity → coworker ostracism moderated by team-level envy; ostracism → production deviance (work withdrawal)
- Team envy is critical moderator: proactivity → ostracism significantly stronger in high-envy teams; indirect path to production deviance significant only in high-envy teams
- Production deviance (frustrated withdrawal from positive contribution) = closest empirical parallel to KM withdrawal hypothesis — note: analogue not direct test of knowledge hiding
- Collectivist ("nail that sticks out") context invoked by authors; strengthens TPS cultural argument but warrants cross-cultural caution
- A1 S2.2: supports plausibility of KM withdrawal hypothesis via production deviance analogue; A3 Ch2/Ch5: frame explicitly as analogue, not direct evidence

## KBF & Knowledge Work Context

---

### Alvesson, M. (2001) 'Knowledge work: Ambiguity, image and identity', *Human Relations*, 54(7), pp. 863–886. doi: 10.1177/0018726701547004.

**Full text confirmed**

- Conceptual/interpretive; "knowledge" in KIFs is inherently ambiguous — value constructed through rhetoric, image and social interaction rather than measurable output
- Because competence is hard to evaluate objectively in KBFs, social standing, impression and identity management become central; identity is insecure and actively regulated
- Amplifies TPS salience in KBFs: if performance is socially constructed, peer comparison is more consequential; marginalisation carries higher identity stakes than in measurable-output settings
- Use alongside Swart & Kinnie (2003) and Nonaka (1994) for KBF-specific context; provides critical/interpretive dimension to complement KBV's strategic framing
- A1 S2.2; A3 Ch2: cite for why TPS operates with heightened force in KBFs — socially constructed competence makes standout performance especially threatening to peers

---

### Swart, J. and Kinnie, N. (2003) 'Sharing knowledge in knowledge-intensive firms', *Human Resource Management Journal*, 13(2), pp. 60–75. doi: 10.1111/j.1748-8583.2003.tb00091.x.

**Full text confirmed**

- Single-case qualitative study (grounded theory; KIF); HR policies build social capital to enable knowledge sharing across distributed specialist knowledge; Nahapiet & Ghoshal social capital + Polanyi tacit knowledge as key constructs
- Shows the social architecture that TPS disrupts: relational trust and social capital are the mechanisms making KBFs function — TPS erodes exactly these
- Methodological precedent: qualitative single-case in KIF; confirms qual approach is established in this context
- A1 S2.2; A3 Ch2/Ch3: cite to operationalise KBV at HR-practice level; use to show what TPS threatens (the social capital pathway) rather than just asserting it

---

### Nonaka, I. (1994) 'A dynamic theory of organizational knowledge creation', *Organization Science*, 5(1), pp. 14–37. doi: 10.1287/orsc.5.1.14.

**Full text confirmed**

- Theoretical; SECI model (Socialisation, Externalisation, Combination, Internalisation); new knowledge originates in individuals; org amplifies through knowledge spiral
- Individual knowledge suppression breaks Socialisation and Externalisation modes — direct mechanism by which TPS-driven withdrawal degrades org-level KM performance
- 10,869 Crossref citations; foundational KM theory; complements KBV by explaining the process (not just the strategic value) of knowledge integration
- Japanese firm examples; theoretical/conceptual; no primary empirical sample
- A1 S2.2; A3 Ch2: combine with Grant (1996) [CoreRef] for "value + process" dual KM argument; SECI model = most accessible visual frame for how TPS breaks KM

## Social Comparison Theory — Org Applications

---

### Matthews, M.J. and Kelemen, T.K. (2025) 'To compare is human: A review of social comparison theory in organizational settings', *Journal of Management*, 51(1). doi: 10.1177/01492063241266157.

**Open access**

- Comprehensive review of 70+ years of SCT in org settings; upward comparison leads to envy, counterproductive behaviour, turnover; social dynamics comparisons most subjective and idiosyncratic
- Equifinality finding: comparisons on compensation, capabilities, and social dynamics all produce similar negative outcomes — contextual framing determines which pathway dominates
- Most current authoritative SCT synthesis in management; ABS 4 (Journal of Management); replaces older SCT review literature
- A1 S2.3; A3 Ch2: primary SCT theoretical anchor for org settings; use to bridge Festinger (1954) [CoreRef] to contemporary management application

---

### Sung, S.Y., Li, Y.X. and Choi, J.N. (2024) 'Upward social comparison toward proactive and reactive knowledge sharing: The roles of envy and goal orientations', *Journal of Business Research*, 170, p. 114314. doi: 10.1016/j.jbusres.2023.114314.

**Full text confirmed**

- Two-study empirical (176 employee-supervisor dyads + online simulation); upward SCT → proactive/reactive KS via benign/malicious envy respectively; moderated by goal orientation
- PARTIAL CONTRADICTION: upward comparison mostly promotes KS (via benign envy + learning goal orientation); malicious envy pathway → reactive-only sharing (withdrawal from proactive donation)
- For us: TPS-specific hostile marginalisation is more likely to activate malicious envy than general upward comparison — withdrawal is plausible but conditional, not deterministic
- Use to add critical balance Enis requested in feedback: "TPS dynamics, through hostile marginalisation, are proposed to activate the malicious envy pathway, making knowledge withdrawal more likely"
- A1 S2.3/S2.4: critical nuance for TPS→KM withdrawal argument; A3 Ch2/Ch5: disconfirming evidence node; boundary condition framing

---

### Li, M., Xu, X. and Kwan, H.K. (2021) 'The antecedents and consequences of workplace envy: A meta-analytic review', *Asia Pacific Journal of Management*, 40(1), pp. 1–35. doi: 10.1007/s10490-021-09772-y.

**Open access**

- Meta-analytic review; upward SCT = primary antecedent of workplace envy; envy reliably produces counterproductive behaviour, ostracism, undermining, reduced helping/sharing across dozens of studies
- Distinguishes episodic, dispositional, and general envy; narcissism, neuroticism, competition, and position as predictors
- Meta-analytic authority provides stronger support than any single empirical study for SCT → envy → peer marginalisation chain
- A1 S2.3; A3 Ch2: cite alongside Breidenthal et al. (2020) and Liu et al. (2024) to anchor SCT → envy → counterproductive behaviour chain with meta-analytic weight

## KM Behaviour & Social Exchange

---

### Zhang, Z., Takahashi, Y. and Rezwan, R.B. (2025) 'Knowledge hiding and social exchange theory: A systematic review and meta-analysis', *Frontiers in Psychology*, 15, p. 1516815. doi: 10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1516815.

**Open access (PMC)**

- Systematic review and meta-analysis; SET-grounded map of knowledge hiding antecedents and consequences; reciprocity violation as primary trigger
- Confirms SET is the appropriate theoretical lens for knowledge hiding behaviour across fragmented prior literature
- Frontiers in Psychology: open-access, peer-reviewed, lower prestige than ABS 3–4 — use as supporting/contextual, not primary SET citation
- A1 S2.4 (supporting); A3 Ch2: cite alongside Cabrera & Cabrera (2002) [CoreRef] and Blau (1964) [CoreRef] for SET foundation; bridges foundational theory to recent meta-analytic evidence

---

### Bari, M.W., Khan, Q. and Di Virgilio, F. (2025) 'Knowledge donation, hoarding, and hiding behaviors of high performers: Mediation role of deviant silence', *European Research on Management and Business Economics*, 31(2), p. 100276. doi: 10.1016/j.iedeen.2025.100276.

**Open access**

- Two-study SET-grounded investigation; high-performance punishment (HPP: disproportionate task loading without reward) → knowledge hoarding and hiding via deviant silence; knowledge donation NOT significantly affected (intrinsically motivated)
- HPP = top-down structural mechanism; TPS = horizontal peer mechanism — distinct but parallel; both predict KM withdrawal via different pathways
- Donation persistence despite hoarding/hiding = relevant to boundary condition framing (§2.5 Concept Brief): even under adverse conditions some knowledge contribution is maintained
- AEDEM/Elsevier journal; open access confirmed
- A1 S2.4: empirical analogue for high-performer KM withdrawal via SET; explicitly note HPP ≠ TPS when citing; A3 Ch5: boundary condition discussion

---

### Cheng, Q., Yan, Q. and Nie, T. (2024) 'Beyond diversity: The impact mechanism of organizational inclusion on employee knowledge hoarding', *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*, published 15 November 2024. doi: 10.1057/s41599-024-04085-z.

**Open access (Nature/Palgrave)**

- Survey (n=366 knowledge employees, China); organisational inclusion → reduced knowledge hoarding via psychological security and perceived cohesion as mediators; learning goal orientation as negative moderator
- Inverse implication: TPS-driven exclusion → reduced cohesion and psychological security → increased hoarding — indirect support for KM withdrawal hypothesis via inclusion-exclusion mechanism
- Cross-cluster: connects team cohesion (Cluster 7) to KM behaviour through social environment; reinforces Foss et al. (2010) on informal social conditions driving KS
- China-based; knowledge workers broadly, not KBF-specific
- A1 S2.4/S2.5; A3 Ch2/Ch5: cite for inclusion-KM-cohesion mechanism; frame as inverse evidence; do not overstate direct connection to TPS

## OCB & Voluntary Knowledge Sharing

---

### Podsakoff, P.M., MacKenzie, S.B., Paine, J.B. and Bachrach, D.G. (2000) 'Organizational citizenship behaviors: A critical review of the theoretical and empirical literature and suggestions for future research', *Journal of Management*, 26(3), pp. 513–563. doi: 10.1177/014920630002600307.

**Full text confirmed**

- Definitive OCB review; primary anchors are SET (Blau 1964 [CoreRef]) and justice/fairness perceptions; OCBs are sensitive to perceived fairness, trust, and LMX quality
- Voluntary KS treated as OCB: discretionary, beyond formal role requirements, governed by SET reciprocity — same conditions directly threatened by TPS-driven peer marginalisation
- OCBs are withheld under perceived unfairness and SET imbalance — TPS as a peer-level fairness violation maps directly onto these antecedent conditions
- 3,021 Crossref citations; ABS 4 (Journal of Management)
- A1 S2.3; A3 Ch2: foundational for treating voluntary KS as OCB subject to SET-governed withdrawal; establishes why TPS-driven fairness violations reduce KS

---

### Wang, S. and Noe, R.A. (2010) 'Knowledge sharing: A review and directions for future research', *Human Resource Management Review*, 20(2), pp. 115–131. doi: 10.1016/j.hrmr.2009.10.001.

**Full text confirmed**

- Narrative review; five areas of KS antecedents: org context, interpersonal/team characteristics, cultural, individual, motivational; primary lenses are SET, social capital, and theory of reasoned action
- Interpersonal/team characteristics section: trust, network ties, and social capital as primary relational antecedents; peer-level competitive dynamics (envy) noted as under-studied moderators
- Directly marks our research contribution space: peer-level envy as KS moderator is explicitly identified as under-studied
- 2,290 Scopus citations
- A1 S2.4; A3 Ch2: cite for comprehensive individual-level KS framework; the under-studied envy moderator note specifically supports our research gap justification

---

### Foss, N.J., Husted, K. and Michailova, S. (2010) 'Governing knowledge sharing in organizations: Levels of analysis, governance mechanisms, and research directions', *Journal of Management Studies*, 47(3), pp. 455–482. doi: 10.1111/j.1467-6486.2009.00870.x.

**Full text confirmed**

- Systematic review of KS literature across 13 leading journals (1996–2006); identifies two critical neglected gaps: (1) macro-level dominance — individual-level KS antecedents under-theorised; (2) formal governance dominant — informal peer dynamics as KS drivers under-studied
- Introduces "knowledge governance" as distinct concept: choosing org structures and mechanisms to influence KS in preferred directions
- Explicitly marks the theoretical gap our study fills: TPS operates at the individual peer-dynamics level through informal social norms — precisely what Foss et al. identify as the frontier
- A1 S2.5 gap statement; A3 Ch2: provides precise, citable articulation of why micro-level peer dynamics as KM governance is a literature gap; use to elevate the contribution claim beyond "TPS not studied in KBFs"

## Team Cohesion

---

### Grossman, R., Nolan, K., Rosch, Z., Mazer, D. and Salas, E. (2022) 'The team cohesion-performance relationship: A meta-analysis exploring measurement approaches and the changing team landscape', *Organizational Psychology Review*, 12(2), pp. 181–238. doi: 10.1177/20413866211041157.

**Full text confirmed (library PDF)**

- Meta-analysis; team cohesion robust antecedent of team performance; task vs social cohesion dimensions have different performance effects; social cohesion (interpersonal bonds) more directly disrupted by TPS-driven peer marginalisation
- Note: journal is *Organizational Psychology Review*, NOT Small Group Research — WCIC prompt misidentified; DOI confirms correct journal
- Most comprehensive recent meta-analysis on cohesion-performance; provides empirical authority for the cohesion → performance claim
- A1 S2.3; A3 Ch2: primary meta-analytic anchor for team cohesion-performance relationship; use to establish stakes of TPS-induced cohesion erosion in KBFs

---

### Mathieu, J.E., Kukenberger, M.R., D'Innocenzo, L. and Reilly, G. (2015) 'Modeling reciprocal team cohesion-performance relationships, as impacted by shared leadership and members' competence', *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 100(3), pp. 713–734. doi: 10.1037/a0038898.

**Full text confirmed (library PDF)**

- Longitudinal study; reciprocal cohesion-performance relationship over time; shared leadership and member competence as moderators; disruption to cohesion initiates self-reinforcing downward spiral
- For us: TPS-driven marginalisation can initiate compounding cycle — reduced cohesion → reduced performance → further TPS behaviour; dynamic rather than static effect
- JAP (ABS 4); longitudinal design adds temporal validity
- A1 S2.3 (supporting); A3 Ch5: use for cohesion spiral framing in discussion; reinforces compounding nature of TPS in KBF teams

---

### Chiocchio, F. and Essiembre, H. (2009) 'Cohesion and performance: A meta-analytic review of disparities between project teams, production teams, and service teams', *Small Group Research*, 40(4), pp. 382–420.

**Full text confirmed (library PDF)**

- Meta-analysis; cohesion-performance correlations differ by team type: project, production, and service teams show distinct patterns
- Service teams (closest to KBF client-facing work) have specific cohesion dynamics distinct from production teams — justifies KBF-specific cohesion claims
- A3 Ch2/Ch5: secondary cohesion source; use to justify KBF-specific claims about cohesion-performance; cite alongside Grossman et al. (2022)

---

### Gully, S.M., Devine, D.J. and Whitney, D.J. (2012) 'A meta-analysis of cohesion and performance: Effects of level of analysis and task interdependence', *Small Group Research*, 43(6), pp. 702–725.

**Full text confirmed (library PDF)**

- Meta-analysis; task interdependence moderates cohesion-performance: higher interdependence → stronger cohesion-performance link
- KBFs characterised by high task interdependence (collective knowledge application) → cohesion disruption has outsized performance consequences in KBF settings vs. low-interdependence environments
- Strengthens the argument that TPS-driven cohesion erosion is especially damaging in KBFs specifically
- A3 Ch2/Ch5: cite for task interdependence moderator; supports KBF-specific cohesion argument

## Methodology Precedent

---

### Thomas, A. (2025) 'The dynamics of knowledge behaviors: Exploring drivers, triggers and paradoxes in knowledge sharing, hiding, hoarding and sabotage', *Journal of Knowledge Management*, 29(11), pp. 117–144. doi: 10.1108/JKM-03-2025-0399.

**Open access (Emerald)**

- Qualitative; 33 semi-structured interviews with professionals from knowledge-intensive organisations; thematic analysis; paradox theory lens
- Maps full spectrum of knowledge behaviours; five paradoxes identified — Ostracism and Social Exclusion is one, directly linking peer marginalisation to KM behaviour transitions
- Near-exact methodological parallel to our design: qualitative semi-structured interviews in KIOs using thematic analysis = methodological precedent
- Paradox theory is distinct from our SET/SCT lens — use as methodological precedent only, not as core theoretical framework
- A1 S3 methodology justification: cite to establish qualitative semi-structured interviews as established method in KIOs for studying KM behaviour; A3 Ch3: reinforce methodological choice
