# Module Home

Hi Culous! Welcome to your final module of the programme and many congratulations for completing the taught modules.

This module provides the opportunity for you to undertake the MBA Project, a self-managed process of systematic practitioner inquiry, to address a management challenge within your work organisation. This project enables you to integrate, apply and develop the knowledge and skills that they have acquired during the MBA programme. Exceptionally, if you are unable to explore a challenge within your own work organisation, you may select an organisation you are closely involved with, such as a charity or sports club, or an organisation where you have significant access to relevant and reliable primary or secondary data sources, such as corporate archive data.

You will explore a management challenge in significantly greater depth than during the taught modules. You will outline the nature of the challenge and provide justification for your choice of topic and process of inquiry in the initial project proposal.

You will plan and execute appropriate management research and / or management consultancy data collection methods, such as primary qualitative or quantitative research or running an investigative workshop(s), and apply a rigorous process of analysis to your evidence-base. Based on your analysis you will generate recommendations for actions to address the management challenge. On completion of the research and analysis, you will produce a final project report as well as a recorded presentation suitable for the senior management team of the organisation ('the client') where you share the findings and recommendations.

## In this module we shall

- Develop your ability to analyse and synthesise complex and ambiguous data from a wide variety of sources, in order to make reasoned judgements and recommendations underpinned by ethical values
- Enable you to integrate your knowledge of management theory and critically apply relevant theory to the analysis of management practice
- Provide you with an opportunity to undertake independent, practice-based research in a rigorous and systematic fashion using appropriate methods to investigate a management challenge facing an organisation, and to prepare and present findings, conclusions and recommendations

## On completion of this module you will be able to

- Select a management challenge facing a particular organisation (the 'client') and justify your choice and process of inquiry in a rigorous and systematic fashion.
- Develop a structured plan proposal in order to undertake independent, practice-based research using appropriate methods
- Integrate knowledge of management theory and critically apply relevant theory to the analysis of management practice
- Analyse and synthesise complex and ambiguous data from a wide variety of sources, in order to make reasoned judgements and recommendations underpinned by ethical values
- Prepare and present findings, conclusions and recommendations in a verbal and written format

# Dissertation Agreement and Guidelines for Students and Dissertation Advisors

*University of Liverpool*

## Responsibilities of the Dissertation Advisor (DA)

- a. It is the responsibility of the Dissertation Advisor to establish a professional relationship with their student(s), Dissertation Advisors should be aware of and act in accordance with University of Liverpool's Dignity at Work and Study Policy.
- b. Dissertation Advisors should ensure that all communications with their students relating to the dissertation, are recorded within the virtual learning environment. For example, if conversations or messages do occur outside the learning environment, the Dissertation Advisor will ensure that a summary is made of their content and posted into the classroom.
- c. Dissertation Advisors should ensure that students are made aware of current research related to their research project and that their choice of research method is appropriate for the subject of their dissertation.
- d. Dissertation Advisors should encourage students to question critically the existing literature around the specific subject area, the assumptions of the research project and the results they obtain.
- e. In their professional supervisory relationship with students, Dissertation Advisors should endeavour to encourage students' engagement with their research project and should remain critical and realistic about students' progress. They are required to record any concerns they have, particularly during any formal process for evaluating a student's academic progress.
- f. Dissertation Advisors should advise students to familiarise themselves with matters of academic integrity and signpost them to the University of Liverpool's Academic Integrity Policy. However, it is not the responsibility of the Dissertation Advisor to specifically assist students in avoiding poor academic practice or of unfair and/or dishonest practice.
- g. Dissertation Advisors should reinforce at an early stage to students that their research project is the students' own responsibility and that it is the examiners who will ultimately determine the final mark for the submitted dissertation.
- h. It is the responsibility of Dissertation Advisors to remind students of the detailed information in the programme's Dissertation Guidelines regarding required timescales for completion of progress reports, completed chapters, and their final dissertation.
- i. A Dissertation Advisor should normally reply to a student's query within 4 days and provide feedback on each submitted draft complete chapter within 10 days.
- j. The Dissertation Advisor will provide feedback in the form of comments on the student's draft. They will NOT provide feedback in the form of tracked changes on the student's draft. In addition, Dissertation Advisors may summarise their comments or add further indications in a post uploaded to the corresponding thread in the Dissertation Advisor/Student discussion. The Dissertation Advisor will provide a review once only for each submitted draft complete chapter and the final dissertation.

**It is *NOT* the responsibility of a Dissertation Advisor to:**

- k. Undertake grammar, formatting referencing, data presentation and spell checks for students. A Dissertation Advisor might point out if a section is particularly unclear or problematic structuring of the work, but it is the student's responsibility to act on this advice.
- l. Provide reviews of incomplete chapters. A student must provide a complete draft of each chapter for the Dissertation Advisor to review. See 1j) above for the format of the Dissertation Advisor's review that will be provided to the student.
- m. Provide feedback on more than one version of either a complete individual chapter or of the final dissertation. See 1j) above.

## Responsibilities of Students Regarding Supervision of the Dissertation by their Dissertation Advisors

- a. Students are required to take responsibility for the planning of their project with their Dissertation Advisor and to engage with their programme as per the terms and conditions outlined in the student agreement prior to and during their formal registration period.
- b. Students should work independently on their research and writing up their dissertation, taking advice when necessary. The research project is the students' own responsibility and it is the examiners who will ultimately determine the final mark for the submitted dissertation.
- c. Students should take ownership and responsibility for meeting agreed objectives (e.g. regarding sequential stages of their dissertation such as obtaining ethical approval) and deadlines and ultimately should take responsibility for the submission of their dissertation for examination within the programme deadline. This will include complying with any requirements set out in the individual programme Dissertation Guidelines regarding deadlines, for example regarding submitting draft complete chapters.
- d. Students should maintain a professional and courteous relationship with their Dissertation Advisor and should be prepared to receive critical feedback of their work. They are entitled to expect, however, that any feedback will be provided by their Dissertation Advisor in a constructive way.
- e. Students should maintain regular contact with their Dissertation Advisor during the period of research until submission of the completed dissertation via Turnitin. In particular they must comply with the requirements of the programme regarding submission of complete chapters for their Dissertation Advisor to review before the final dissertation is submitted for assessment.
- f. Students must inform their Dissertation Advisor of any changes in personal circumstances, which might affect the progress of their work, and must also inform the Student Services Team.
- g. Students must uphold and maintain academic integrity in accordance with the University of Liverpool's Academic Integrity Policy: [http://www.liv.ac.uk/media/livacuk/tqsd/code-of-practice-on-assessment/appendix_L_cop_assess.pdf](http://www.liv.ac.uk/media/livacuk/tqsd/code-of-practice-on-assessment/appendix_L_cop_assess.pdf), and take responsibility for and quality of the work submitted as their formal dissertation.
- h. Students must submit their draft full dissertation to the Dissertation Advisor at least one month before the final submission deadline. Submitting after this deadline may result in the Dissertation Advisor not being able to provide feedback to the full draft dissertation.
- i. It is the responsibility of the student to check the final version of their dissertation for grammar, formatting, referencing, data presentation and spell checks before they submit it through Turnitin for assessment.
- j. It is not permitted under any circumstances for a student to write the dissertation in another language and have it translated into English. It is also unacceptable for a student to give the text to a professional editor for corrections. The University of Liverpool will treat the use of a professional editor as unauthorised 'collusion'.

## Proposal

### Introduction *(300 words)*

- Provide a brief overview of the management challenge under investigation, including brief contextual background. What is the problem you are trying to solve and why is it important to solve it? State the research aims and objectives/research questions. Identify the potential contribution of the research to your client(s) / organisation and any wider significance for the field of business research.

### Literature Review *(600 words)*

- Provide a critical review of the key relevant literature on the topic. Identify any theoretical or empirical gaps in the literature.
- Briefly demonstrate the significance of your study by highlighting how it will contribute to the existing body of knowledge on the topic and thus add to what is already known, fill any gaps and help to resolve your organisational / management issue.
- Provide a summary at the end of your literature review in the form of a diagram (thematic map), model (conceptual framework) or written form. A visual form is useful as it can be revisited and updated as part of your final project findings. The words in any visual form must still be included in your word count.

### Research Methodology *(600 words)*

- **Research approach:** brief discussion of ontological and epistemological stance and justification of qualitative or quantitative methods.
- **Data Collection:** justify your choice of data collection methods e.g. questionnaires, interviews, focus groups.
- **Sampling Strategy:** identify who will take part in your research and provide a justification for selection, indicate the number of people you intend to involve e.g. with questionnaires (usually up to 30 respondents) or interviews (typically between 5 and 10 interviewees) and provide an academically rigorous discussion of how the selected sample is representative / robust for the particular research study.
- **Data analysis:** how will you analyse your data? Provide analytical strategy e.g. thematic analysis, statistical tests as well as justification for analytical approach and any intended tools e.g. NVivo, SPSS, Excel.
- **Reliability and validity:** a brief discussion on how will you ensure that your data are accurate and robust.
- **Feasibility and potential limitations:** e.g. time constraints, researcher knowledge and skills, highlight any risks such as securing access to relevant participants.
- **Ethics:** brief discussion of ethical considerations e.g. university approval process, informed consent.

### Milestone Plan *(not included in word count)*

- Work back from your submission date and include key activities to produce your final deliverables (MBA Project and Recorded Presentation) such as submission of a draft to your supervisor (week 32).
- It is a serious academic offence to collect data without university ethical approval and could result in module failure. Ensure data collection does start until written ethical approval has been received —— make this clear in your plan.

### References *(not included in word count)*

- List all the references cited in the proposal using a consistent Harvard citation style

## Chapter 1: Introduction *(800 words)*

This section should provide the introduction to the problem to be addressed by the research. Research Overview (background, research aims and objectives, research questions). The importance of the research for the client(s) / organisation and the significance, if appropriate, for wider business research knowledge. Structure of Chapters (brief overview of what is in each subsequent chapter).

---

## Chapter 2: Literature Review *(2500 words)*

This is a critical review of literature relevant to the research problem. It should ensure that you do not directly repeat research that has been done already but do use such research to inform your own process of inquiry. This is an extended version from your proposal that should:

- Provide a critical review of the key relevant literature on the topic. Identify any theoretical or empirical gaps in the literature.
- Demonstrate the significance of your study by highlighting how it will contribute to the existing body of knowledge on the topic and thus add to what is already known, fill any gaps and help to resolve your organisational / management issue.
- Provide a summary at the end of your literature review in the form of a diagram (thematic map), model (conceptual framework) or written form. A visual form is useful as it can be revisited and updated as part of your final project findings and analysis.

---

## Chapter 3: Methodology *(850 words)*

This builds and extends the work that you included in your research proposal although here you will be talking about what you did rather than what you intend to do. The exact content of this section may vary depending on the nature of the research but should generally include the following topics:

- **Research approach:** brief discussion of ontological and epistemological stance and justification of qualitative or quantitative methods.
- **Data Collection:** justify your choice of data collection methods e.g. surveys, interviews, focus groups. How did you test / pilot your data collection method? What did you do to help achieve a high response / participation rate?
- **Sampling Strategy:** identify who took part in your research and provide a justification for selection, indicate the number of people involved e.g. with a survey or interviews and provide an academically rigorous discussion of how the selected sample is representative / robust for the particular research study. Provide detailed descriptions e.g. why and how people (or sites) were chosen; how access was obtained; when and how often you met with people.
- **Data analysis:** how did you analyse your data? Provide justification for analytical approach e.g. thematic analysis or statistical tests, and tools used e.g. NVivo, SPSS, Excel.
- **Trustworthiness:** Discuss issues such as validity and reliability of the data and the ability to generalise.
- **Ethical Considerations:** discussion of the ethical considerations you have addressed in your research project e.g. gaining written university approval before commencing data collection, ensuring participants take part on the basis of informed consent and how data confidentiality was assured.

You may wish to use the above bold text headings as sub-section headings in your project.

---

## Chapter 4: Findings or Results *(1250 words)*

This is where you explain the data you've collected e.g. figures, tables, thematic analysis and key quotes etc. For some types of research e.g. qualitative thematic analysis you may choose to combine chapter 4 and 5.

---

## Chapter 5: Analysis / Discussion *(1250 words)*

- This is where your analysis of the data appears and where you relate what you have found to the relevant literature from your review and to your research aims and objectives showing how what you have found answers your research questions and extends, supports or challenges other academic research.
- You might summarise your findings and analysis by producing a revised version of your diagram / model from the literature review which reflects what you have found.
- It is important that your project has a "golden thread" where there is a clear logical flow from your aims and objectives such that the literature reviewed, data collected, findings and conclusions and recommendations all address the initial purpose of your research.

---

## Chapter 6: Conclusions and Recommendations *(1000 words)*

This is an important chapter where you develop the recommendations that you will seek support for in your recorded presentation. You should address these areas:

- **Conclusions:** briefly address the original research objectives / questions and summarise what you have found and why it is important in addressing the organisational / management challenge, and any wider implications for business research knowledge.
- **Recommendations for Practice:** this is key and an important distinction between a management project and a more traditional academic dissertation where recommendations for practice may not be included. This is the "so what?" of your project. So, what should your organisation / client(s) do differently? Recommendations should be SMART (specific, measurable, achievable / action-oriented, relevant / realistic, time-bound) and a mix of short, medium and long-term.
- **Research Limitations:** identify any resource or time constraints, researcher knowledge and skills that may have impacted your study and any difficulties e.g. in securing access to relevant participants that have limited your research.
- **Recommendations for Future Research:** what further research would be appropriate based on your findings e.g. a larger study, a similar study in a different empirical context e.g. industry sector, country; a similar study using a different method, or a deeper exploration of an interesting and unexpected finding from your research.
- **Personal Learning Reflections:** this is your final opportunity to reflect on your learning from the MBA Project and / or to reflect more widely on your MBA journey and future aspirations by drawing on your Career Catalyst posts.

You may wish to use the above bold text headings as sub-section headings in your project.

---

## References *(not included in the word count)*

References must be formatted using the Harvard system of referencing.

## Appendices *(not included in the word count)*

Each appendix should be 'numbered' alphabetically and labelled e.g. Appendix A Example Interview Transcript and should be referred to in the main body e.g. "see Appendix A for sample transcript".

Any participant data in the appendix must be anonymised e.g. don't include completed consent forms with names and signatures. However, these should be available should your supervisor want to see them.

Appendices should contain materials that help to explain what has been done but are too bulky or not important enough to include in the text. It is very important to demonstrate transparency, to reassure the reviewer that you have collected the data that you have described in your project, and that you have followed the process set out in your methodology. For example:

- copy of the participant information sheet
- blank consent form
- confirmation of the Ethics Approval
- questionnaire or interview schedule
- quantitative research: summary of the quantitative data
- qualitative research: single anonymised interview transcript and / or field notes
- qualitative research: summary or example of qualitative analysis / coding

You do not need to include every interview transcript, but these should be made available if requested. Your signed consent forms, ethics application form and any other relevant evidence of your research should also be made available if requested.

# Ethical Approval Process

The Ethical Approval process is outlined below:

1. Your Research Proposal is approved by your Work Based Project Supervisor and at least one other person. This includes a methodology section which must include information about any ethical issues surrounding the collection of your data or the methods related to the research.
2. If your project includes data collection or research which is within your own organisation or requires approval at a local level, this must be obtained before the University approves the ethics application. Please ensure your Work Based Project Supervisor knows that this is required. Obtaining this approval can take time, so you should begin this process as soon as possible. You will not be able to apply for extensions or extenuating circumstances due to any delay in obtaining local ethical approval.
3. The Ethics Application must include any information that will be provided to participants, including consent and information forms, questionnaires, interview questions and schedules, risk analysis and mitigation outlines and information on how you plan to collect, store and dispose of the information after your project is complete. A full list of documentation can be found in the Ethics Approval section.
4. You are responsible for completing the Ethics Application and all the associated documents, such as consent and information forms, questionnaires, schedules, etc. The Ethics Application is then considered by your Work Based Project Supervisor and at least one other person. If there are any questions about the methods you propose or the way that you plan to store and collect data, the Ethics Application may be returned to you so that you can respond to the question, and resubmit your application. You may not proceed with any research until the Ethics Application has been formally accepted. If the Ethics Application is deemed to be above a certain level of risk, it will also be considered by the Liverpool Online Research Ethics Committee (LOREC). This will take more time to approve.
5. Once the Ethics Application is approved, you will be notified in writing. **YOU MAY NOT START DATA COLLECTION UNTIL YOU HAVE RECEIVED CONFIRMATION IN WRITING.** During the time that you are waiting for approval, you may work on your literature review or other research from secondary sources.

Please now refer to the University of Liverpool Policy on Research Ethics (omitted).

The key principles listed in the Policy on research ethics are:

- Except where the nature of the research makes this impossible, research staff and participants must be fully informed about the purpose, methods, and intended possible uses of the research. On the basis of this information they must give their voluntary consent to take part in the project, free from coercion.
- The confidentiality of the information supplied by research participants and the anonymity of respondents must be respected. Wherever possible data should be anonymised and there should be full compliance with the principles within the Data Protection Act 2018. Personal data can only be disclosed when permission to disclose is part of the consent procedure.
- Risks to those involved in the research must be minimised, and harm to research participants and researchers must be avoided. Participants must be warned in advance about any potential risks of harm.
- The independence of the research must be clear, and any conflict of interest or partiality must be explicit.
- The research must conform to all relevant statutory requirements and to the requirements of the institution in which the research is undertaken.

## Ethics Checklist

All research projects which involve human participants, their tissues or their personal information should receive research ethics approval before they commence. If you are unsure whether your study requires ethical approval, please use the 'Do I need ethical approval?' Decision Tool (omitted). The following are examples of research methods that require ethical approval:

- a. Questionnaire
- b. Survey
- c. Focus groups
- d. Observational studies, including interactions with other people, technology or a set of prompts
- e. Tests of any sort, including standardised tests or psychometric tests
- f. Secondary data which includes any personal information, such as salary, gender, educational achievement, personal characteristics

If you answer 'yes' to any of these questions —— you will require Ethical Approval.

As part of your research proposal, you should also include information on the following:

1. What information do you plan to obtain for your study?
2. How do you plan to obtain this information?
3. What characteristics are you looking for in the cohort? i.e. age, interest, employer, salary scale, gender?
4. How do you plan to recruit or find the cohort?

This information will be required for your Ethics Approval documentation.

In all cases, you must avoid research which includes any of the following categories:

- A study which includes a person who can be considered 'vulnerable', such as a child, an adult who cannot be expected to give consent because of illness or impairment, or someone who is not in a position to refuse to participate due to economic or social reasons;
- A study which aims to deceive or mislead participants in any way;
- A study which requires participants to discuss sensitive topics, such as bereavement, anti-social or criminal activity, embarrassing or very personal subjects;
- A study in which the collection of data is dangerous to either the participant or the person collecting the data;
- A study which includes the imbibement of any substance;
- A study in which there is a prolonged or extensive requirement on the participant to engage;
- A study which could lead to discomfort, anxiety, stress or negative consequences to the participant in any way;
- A study which includes human tissue samples;
- A study in which participants are not informed or able to understand the full intentions and objectives of the research.

# Action Learning Set (ALS) Guidance

Action learning sets are a powerful tool for professional and personal development, and can be used to support individuals or groups in addressing real-life challenges and issues. Here are some steps to set up an action learning set:

1. **Identify the Participants:** Identify group members participating in an action learning set. It is recommended to have 4`~`6 participants in a set, but this can vary depending on the cohort size.
2. **Establish the Purpose:** Clarify the purpose of the action learning set and identify the challenges or issues that the participants would like to address. Each week the focus of the action learning set should be on the ongoing progress of the MBA project proposal.
3. **Set the Ground Rules:** Establish ground rules for the action learning set, including confidentiality, respect for other participants, commitment to attendance, and willingness to share personal experiences and insights. Each group should set these from the start and have them documented.
4. **Assign Roles:** Assign roles to the participants, including the facilitator, timekeeper, and note-taker. The facilitator is responsible for guiding the group through the process, while the timekeeper ensures that each participant has equal time to share and ask questions. The note-taker is responsible for capturing key insights and learning from each session. Roles can change in each meeting so everyone can develop their skills and build their experience.
5. **Conduct the Sessions:** Conduct regular action learning sessions, following the agreed-upon structure and process. Participants should take turns presenting their challenges or issues, and the group should work together to explore and understand the problem, generate potential solutions, and identify actions to be taken.
6. **Evaluate and Refine:** Evaluate the effectiveness of the action learning set and refine the process as necessary. Solicit feedback from the participants on what worked well and what could be improved, and make adjustments accordingly.

By following these steps, you can set up an effective action learning set that supports individual and group learning, fosters collaboration, and enables the resolution of real-life challenges and issues.

# MBA Research Project Advisor Agreement and Guidelines for Students and Advisors

*University of Liverpool*

## Responsibilities of the Research Project Advisor

- a. It is the responsibility of the advisor to establish a professional relationship with their student(s), advisors should be aware of and act in accordance with University of Liverpool's Dignity at Work and Study Policy.
- b. Advisors should ensure that all communications with their students relating to the research project, are recorded within the virtual learning environment. For example, if conversations or messages do occur outside the learning environment, the advisor will ensure that a summary is made of their content and posted into the classroom.
- c. Advisors should ensure that students are aware of current research related to their research project and that their choice of research method is appropriate for the subject of their research project.
- d. Advisors should encourage students to question critically the existing literature around the specific subject area, the assumptions of the research project and the results they obtain.
- e. In their professional supervisory relationship with students, advisors should endeavour to encourage students' engagement with their research project and should remain critical and realistic about students' progress. They are required to record any concerns they have, particularly during any formal process for evaluating a student's academic progress.
- f. Advisors should advise students to familiarise themselves with matters of academic integrity and signpost them to comply with the University of Liverpool's Academic Integrity Policy. However, it is not the responsibility of the advisor to specifically assist students in avoiding poor academic practice or unfair and/or dishonest practice.
- g. Advisors should reinforce at an early stage to students that their research project is the students' own responsibility and that it is the examiners who will ultimately determine the final mark for the submitted research project.
- h. It is the responsibility of advisors to remind students of information regarding required timescales for completion of progress reports, completed chapters, and their final research project.
- i. An Advisor should normally reply to a student's query within 4 days and provide feedback on each submitted draft complete chapter within 10 days.
- j. The Advisor will provide feedback in the form of comments on the student's draft. They will NOT provide feedback in the form of tracked changes on the student's draft. In addition, advisors may summarise their comments or add further indications in a post uploaded to the corresponding thread in the Advisor/Student discussion. The advisor will provide a review once only for each submitted draft complete chapter and the final research project.

**It is *NOT* the responsibility of an advisor to:**

- k. Undertake grammar, formatting referencing, data presentation and spell checks for students. An advisor might point out if a section is particularly unclear or problematic structuring of the work, but it is the student's responsibility to act on this advice.
- l. Provide reviews of incomplete chapters. A student must provide a complete draft of each chapter for the advisor to review. See 1j) above for the format of the advisor's review that will be provided to the student.
- m. Provide feedback on more than one version of either a complete individual chapter or of the final research project. See 1j) above.

## Responsibilities of Students Regarding Supervision of the Research Project by their Project Advisors

- a. Students are required to take responsibility for the planning of their project with their advisor and to engage with their programme as per the terms and conditions outlined in the student agreement prior to and during their formal registration period.
- b. Students should work independently on their research and writing up their project, taking advice when necessary. The research project is the students' own responsibility and it is the examiners who will ultimately determine the final mark for the submitted project.
- c. Students should take ownership and responsibility for meeting agreed objectives (e.g. regarding sequential stages of their project such as obtaining ethical approval) and deadlines and ultimately should take responsibility for the submission of their project and recorded presentation for examination within the programme deadline. This will include complying with any requirements set out in the individual programme guidelines regarding deadlines, for example regarding submitting draft complete chapters.
- d. Students should maintain a professional and courteous relationship with their advisor and should be prepared to receive critical feedback of their work. They are entitled to expect, however, that any feedback will be provided by their advisor in a constructive way.
- e. Students should maintain regular contact with their advisor during the period of research until submission of the completed project via Turnitin. In particular they must comply with the requirements of the programme regarding submission of complete chapters for their advisor to review before the final research project is submitted for assessment.
- f. Students must inform their advisor of any changes in personal circumstances, which might affect the progress of their work, and must also inform the student support team.
- g. Students must uphold and maintain academic integrity in accordance with the University of Liverpool's Academic Integrity Policy: [https://www.liverpool.ac.uk/aqsd/academic-codes-of-practice/code-of-practice-on-assessment/](https://www.liverpool.ac.uk/aqsd/academic-codes-of-practice/code-of-practice-on-assessment/) (Section 9 and Appendix L), and take responsibility for the quality of the work submitted as their formal project and recorded presentation.
- h. Students must submit their full draft project to the advisor at least four weeks before the final submission deadline. Submitting after this deadline may result in the advisor not being able to provide feedback on the full draft project.
- i. It is the responsibility of the student to check the final version of their work for grammar, formatting, referencing, data presentation and spell checks before they submit it through Turnitin for assessment.
- j. It is not permitted under any circumstances for a student to write the project in another language and have it translated into English. It is also unacceptable for a student to give the text to a professional editor for corrections. The University of Liverpool will treat the use of a professional editor as unauthorised 'collusion'.

- I understand that all work produced for assessment is subject not only to the rules on academic Integrity but must conform to all guidelines and regulations regarding assessment which can be found in the University of Liverpool Code of Practice on Assessment.

### Dissertation Publishing Guidance

If you wish to publish any aspect of your Dissertation/Research Project, whether in full or in part, this must be co-authored with your Dissertation Adviser/Supervisor.

Your Dissertation Adviser/Supervisor should be included as a named author to ensure appropriate academic oversight, adherence to discipline-specific standards, and compliance with expectations of responsible research practice.

If your Dissertation/Research Project involves data collection from human participants then your intention to publish must be declared during the ethical review process. This requirement ensures that participants are fully informed about how their data may be used and that the research complies with institutional and sector-wide ethical standards:

- **Declaration in Ethics Application:** You must clearly indicate in your ethics application that you plan to pursue publication. This includes journal articles, book chapters, conference papers, or any other form of dissemination beyond your dissertation/research project.
- **Participant Information and Consent:** The intention to publish must be explicitly communicated in both the Participant Information Sheet and the Consent Form. Participants must be informed of the potential for publication prior to taking part and must agree to the conditions under which their data may be used.
- **Protection of Participants:** Any published work must safeguard participant anonymity and confidentiality. You are responsible for ensuring that published material respects the terms under which participants consented to be involved, including any limitations on the use, disclosure, or presentation of their data.
